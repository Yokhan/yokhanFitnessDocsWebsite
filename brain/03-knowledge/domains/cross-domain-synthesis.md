# Cross-Domain Synthesis: Universal Patterns

> Anti-Stupidity Shield: Meta-patterns extracted from 10 domain knowledge bases (500 bad practices, 500 best practices, 100 LLM misconceptions). Each pattern below appears in 3+ domains with independent evidence.
> Last updated: 2026-03-19.

---

## 20 Universal Anti-Patterns (appear in 3+ domains)

1. **Premature Optimization / Over-Engineering** --- Building complex systems, abstractions, or processes before understanding actual needs. DOMAINS: Programming (premature abstraction, over-engineering architecture), Product Design (design system over-engineering for small teams, premature personalization), Game Design (GDD as waterfall bible, balance by spreadsheet only), Business (scaling before product-market fit, premature hiring), Marketing (complex attribution before basic tracking), Productivity (over-tracking, hyper-scheduling). MECHANISM: Humans prefer building to validating. Complexity feels like progress. LLMs amplify this by generating elaborate solutions that look sophisticated. DEFENSE: Apply the Rule of Three --- don't abstract until you have 3+ concrete cases. Ask "What is the simplest thing that could work?" before every design decision. If the problem hasn't been validated, the solution is premature.

2. **Vanity Metrics / Measuring the Wrong Thing** --- Tracking numbers that look impressive but don't correlate with actual outcomes. DOMAINS: Marketing (impressions, followers, likes), Product Design (page views, downloads), Business (revenue without margins, funding rounds as validation), Game Design (feature count as progress), Science (p-values without effect sizes), Fitness (scale weight vs body composition). MECHANISM: Vanity metrics are easy to collect and always go up. Real metrics require defining what success actually means, which is harder. DEFENSE: For every metric, ask: "If this number doubles, does anything that matters actually improve?" Demand causal connection between the metric and the desired outcome.

3. **Confirmation Bias / Cherry-Picking Evidence** --- Seeking, interpreting, and remembering information that confirms pre-existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. DOMAINS: Science (HARKing, p-hacking, publication bias), Product Design (confirmation bias in research, leading questions), Psychology (replication crisis from selectively reported results), Marketing (cherry-picking campaign data), Business (survivorship bias in strategy), Medicine (selective reporting of trial outcomes), Decisions (motivated reasoning). MECHANISM: Disconfirming evidence is psychologically painful. Humans and LLMs alike pattern-match toward familiar, expected outcomes. DEFENSE: Actively seek disconfirmation. Pre-register hypotheses. Ask "What evidence would change my mind?" If nothing would, you are not reasoning --- you are rationalizing.

4. **Cargo Cult Practices / Copying Without Understanding** --- Imitating the surface features of successful examples without understanding the underlying mechanisms that made them work. DOMAINS: Programming (cargo cult programming, resume-driven development), Product Design (cargo cult design patterns, copying competitor features), Game Design (copy-paste mechanics without context), Marketing (copying competitor campaigns), Business (imitating unicorn tactics at seed stage), Writing (Canva template thinking). MECHANISM: Surface features are visible; underlying mechanisms are not. Imitation is cognitively cheaper than analysis. LLMs are especially prone because they pattern-match from training data without causal understanding. DEFENSE: Before adopting any practice, articulate WHY it works in its original context, WHAT conditions it depends on, and WHETHER those conditions exist in your context.

5. **Feature Bloat / Scope Creep / Adding Instead of Subtracting** --- Continuously adding features, content, or complexity without removing or simplifying. DOMAINS: Programming (feature creep, unnecessary dependencies), Product Design (feature bloat, MVP scope creep), Game Design (feature creep as progress, second system effect), Marketing (channel proliferation without focus), Business (product bloat, premature diversification), Writing (verbosity, over-explanation). MECHANISM: Adding feels productive; removing feels like losing. Adams et al. (2021, Nature) found people systematically overlook subtractive solutions. Hick's Law: more options = slower decisions = more abandonment. DEFENSE: For every addition, ask: "What can I remove to make room for this?" Apply subtractive design: a system is complete not when nothing can be added, but when nothing can be taken away.

6. **Ignoring Feedback Loops / Shipping Without Measurement** --- Launching products, campaigns, or initiatives without instrumentation to measure whether they succeed. DOMAINS: Product Design (launching without analytics, redesigning without baselines), Game Design (ignoring player telemetry), Marketing (no attribution, no A/B testing), Business (no unit economics tracking), Programming (no monitoring, no observability), Science (no replication attempts). MECHANISM: Measurement requires admitting the possibility of failure. Building is more exciting than measuring. DEFENSE: Define success criteria BEFORE launching anything. "What specific number will tell us if this worked?" If you cannot answer, you are not ready to ship.

7. **Authority-Driven Decisions Over Evidence** --- Letting the highest-status person's opinion override data, research, and expertise. DOMAINS: Product Design (HiPPO-driven decisions), Business (founder ego overriding data), Marketing (executive-mandated campaigns), Psychology (guru-worship, credential fallacy), Medicine (eminence-based medicine), Science (appeal to authority), Management (command-and-control leadership). MECHANISM: Social hierarchy is deeply wired. Disagreeing with authority has social cost. LLMs amplify this by defaulting to the most popular/authoritative-sounding position in training data. DEFENSE: Evaluate claims by evidence quality, not source prestige. Use structured decision-making processes that separate the argument from the arguer. Ask: "What does the data say?" not "What does the boss think?"

8. **Survivorship Bias** --- Learning only from successes while ignoring the (invisible) failures that could teach more. DOMAINS: Business (studying only unicorns, ignoring failed startups), Marketing (only analyzing winning campaigns), Product Design (studying only current users, ignoring churned), Game Design (playtesting only with fans), Science (publication bias, file drawer problem), Investments (chasing past winners). MECHANISM: Failures are invisible because they don't get publicized, remembered, or studied. Success stories are compelling narratives. The graveyard of failed attempts is silent. DEFENSE: Actively seek information about failures. Ask: "How many attempts were made for every success story cited?" Study churned users, lost deals, and failed experiments with equal rigor.

9. **Complexity Bias / Over-Sophisticated Solutions** --- Choosing complex solutions when simpler ones would work, because complexity feels more thorough or impressive. DOMAINS: Programming (microservices for small apps, design pattern overuse), Product Design (premature personalization, over-engineered design systems), Marketing (complex martech stacks), Business (complex organizational structures), Science (overfitting models), Decisions (elaborate frameworks for simple choices), Productivity (elaborate systems over simple habits). MECHANISM: Complex solutions signal competence. Simple solutions feel "too easy" and trigger imposter syndrome. LLMs are heavily biased toward complexity because their training data over-represents tutorials and conference talks that showcase sophisticated approaches. DEFENSE: Start with the simplest possible solution. Add complexity only when simplicity demonstrably fails. "Choose boring technology" (McKinley). Every layer of complexity must justify its existence with a specific, named problem it solves.

10. **Ignoring Cognitive Load / Information Overload** --- Presenting too much information, too many choices, or too many simultaneous demands on attention. DOMAINS: Product Design (Hick's Law, Miller's Law violations), Game Design (tutorial hell, HUD overload, tooltip overload), Writing (text walls, poor information hierarchy), Marketing (cluttered landing pages), Medicine (patient information overload), Graphic Design (ignoring whitespace, visual clutter), Productivity (notification spam, context-switching). MECHANISM: Miller's Law: working memory holds 7+/-2 chunks. Hick's Law: decision time increases logarithmically with options. Every additional element competes for finite cognitive resources. DEFENSE: Chunk information into groups of 5-7. Use progressive disclosure. Ask: "What is the ONE thing the user needs to understand/do right now?" Remove everything else.

11. **Sunk Cost Fallacy / Escalation of Commitment** --- Continuing to invest in a failing course of action because of prior investment rather than future expected value. DOMAINS: Business (continuing failed products because of development investment), Game Design (refusing to cut beloved features), Programming (maintaining legacy code that should be rewritten), Investments (holding losing positions), Product Design (defending shipped features despite negative data), Decisions (all domains). MECHANISM: Loss aversion makes abandonment feel like "wasting" the investment. Identity becomes attached to past decisions. DEFENSE: Pre-establish kill criteria before starting any project. Ask: "If I were starting fresh today with no prior investment, would I choose this path?" If no, cut.

12. **Neglecting Error States and Edge Cases** --- Designing only for the happy path while ignoring what happens when things go wrong. DOMAINS: Programming (swallowing exceptions, no error handling), Product Design (ignoring error states, vague error messages), Game Design (untelegraphed deaths, punishing exploration), Writing (no contingency language), Crisis Management (no contingency plans), Medicine (ignoring adverse reactions). MECHANISM: The happy path is more pleasant to think about. Error states are annoying to design. But users spend significant time in error states, and error handling quality determines trust. DEFENSE: Design error states FIRST. For every flow, ask: "What happens when this fails?" Every error must have a clear message and a recovery path.

13. **Recency Bias / Trend-Chasing** --- Overweighting recent information, trends, and hype while underweighting long-term patterns and fundamentals. DOMAINS: Investments (chasing hot stocks/sectors), Programming (resume-driven development, shiny technology syndrome), Marketing (bandwagon metrics), Business (pivot-mania based on latest trend), Science (replication of "hot" findings only), Game Design (copying latest hit). MECHANISM: Recent events are more cognitively available (availability heuristic). New things generate excitement. Old things feel boring. Media amplifies novelty. DEFENSE: Ask: "Will this still be relevant in 5 years?" Prefer proven fundamentals over trends. Apply "Choose boring technology" philosophy. Demand evidence that the new approach is actually better, not just newer.

14. **Dark Patterns / Manipulative Design** --- Using psychological tricks to manipulate people into actions against their interest for short-term gains. DOMAINS: Product Design (dark patterns in UX, confirmshaming), Game Design (loot boxes, FOMO events, predatory monetization), Marketing (deceptive claims, manufactured urgency), Sales (manipulation tactics, FUD selling), Psychology (NLP manipulation techniques). MECHANISM: Exploitation of cognitive biases is effective in the short term. It's easier to manipulate than to create genuine value. Regulatory and reputational costs are delayed. DEFENSE: Apply the "newspaper front page test": would you be comfortable if this tactic were reported in the news? Design for long-term trust, not short-term conversion. If a tactic only works because the user doesn't fully understand what's happening, it's manipulative.

15. **Crunch / Burnout as Strategy** --- Treating unsustainable intensity as a legitimate approach to achieving goals. DOMAINS: Game Design (crunch culture), Business (startup grind culture), Productivity (heroic sprints, ignoring recovery), Programming (death marches), Management (rewarding overwork), Crisis Management (sustained crisis mode without rotation). MECHANISM: Short-term output increases are visible; long-term degradation in quality, health, and retention are delayed and invisible. Culture rewards visible suffering. DEFENSE: Productivity declines sharply after 4 days of extended hours. Sustainable pace produces better cumulative output. WHO classifies burnout as an occupational phenomenon. If you need crunch, your scope or timeline is wrong --- fix the plan, not the people.

16. **Say-Do Gap Blindness / Relying on Self-Report** --- Taking what people SAY at face value without observing what they actually DO. DOMAINS: Product Design (say-do gap blindness), Psychology (self-report bias in research), Marketing (survey-based market research), Game Design (player requests vs. player behavior), Business (customer interview vs. usage data), Sales (verbal commitments vs. signed contracts). MECHANISM: People don't lie intentionally --- they genuinely don't know their own behavior. Self-report is systematically biased toward socially desirable responses and post-hoc rationalization. DEFENSE: Weight behavioral data over attitudinal data. Observe actions, don't just ask about intentions. When you must use self-report, triangulate with behavioral measures. "Watch what they do, not what they say."

17. **One-Size-Fits-All / False Universalism** --- Applying rules, frameworks, or solutions universally without adapting to context. DOMAINS: Product Design (mobile-first dogma, "always A/B test"), Programming (SOLID as rigid rules, "always use microservices"), Management (universal leadership style), Marketing (same strategy for all segments), Medicine (population averages applied to individuals), Game Design (single difficulty), Fitness (universal training programs). MECHANISM: Universal rules are easier to remember and teach than contextual guidelines. "It depends" is unsatisfying. LLMs are particularly prone because they learn "best practices" as absolute rules from training data that lacks nuance. DEFENSE: Always ask: "Under what conditions does this advice apply? What are the exceptions?" Every practice has boundary conditions. The correct answer to most "should I do X?" questions is "it depends on context."

18. **Expertise Illusion / Dunning-Kruger Domain Misapplication** --- Assuming that expertise in one domain transfers to competence in another, or that confidence equals correctness. DOMAINS: Business (technical founders making marketing decisions without expertise), Science (scientists commenting outside their field), Medicine (alternative practitioners claiming medical expertise), Psychology (pop-psychology gurus), Investments (successful entrepreneurs giving investment advice), Decisions (overconfidence bias across all domains). MECHANISM: Expertise is domain-specific. The feeling of competence is general. People who are skilled at one thing assume their judgment transfers universally. DEFENSE: Respect domain boundaries. Seek domain-specific experts. When operating outside your expertise, increase humility, verify more, and default to established best practices rather than personal intuition.

19. **Premature Commitment / Irreversible Decisions Made Reversibly** --- Making hard-to-reverse decisions too early, when information is insufficient and the cost of waiting is low. DOMAINS: Programming (premature architecture decisions, choosing frameworks too early), Business (scaling before PMF, long-term contracts before validation), Product Design (building before validating), Game Design (waterfall GDD as gospel), Investments (concentrated positions without research), Decisions (real options destroyed prematurely). MECHANISM: Deciding feels productive. Uncertainty is uncomfortable. Commitment reduces cognitive dissonance. But premature commitment destroys the option value of waiting for more information. DEFENSE: Apply Bezos's two-way door test: if a decision is easily reversible, decide fast. If it's hard to reverse, slow down and gather more evidence. Keep options open when the cost of doing so is low.

20. **Blame-First / Individualist Attribution** --- Attributing problems to individual character flaws rather than systemic causes (processes, incentives, structures). DOMAINS: Management (blame-first culture), Crisis Management (scapegoating), Psychology (fundamental attribution error), Programming (blaming developers for bugs caused by process failures), Business (firing people instead of fixing systems), Medicine (blaming patients for non-compliance). MECHANISM: Individual blame is a simpler narrative than systemic analysis. It provides emotional satisfaction and a clear "fix" (remove the person). Systemic thinking is harder and less dramatic. DEFENSE: When something goes wrong, ask "What system allowed this to happen?" before "Who is responsible?" Use blameless post-mortems. Fix processes, not people.

## 20 Universal Best Practices (appear in 3+ domains)

1. **Iterate with Feedback Loops** --- Build small, test with real users/data, learn, and repeat rather than planning extensively upfront. DOMAINS: Programming (TDD, CI/CD), Product Design (continuous discovery, 5-user testing), Game Design (early and frequent playtesting), Business (lean startup, MVP validation), Science (iterative hypothesis testing), Marketing (test-and-learn campaigns). MECHANISM: Reality is too complex to predict accurately. Each iteration reveals truths that theory cannot. The more cycles of build-test-learn, the faster convergence on good solutions. APPLICATION: Ship the smallest testable version first. Define what you will learn from each iteration before building. Shorten feedback loops relentlessly.

2. **Measure Before and After** --- Establish baselines before changes, define success criteria upfront, and measure outcomes objectively. DOMAINS: Product Design (baseline before redesign), Programming (profiling before optimizing), Science (pre-registration, control groups), Marketing (attribution, A/B testing), Business (unit economics from day one), Medicine (diagnostic baselines), Game Design (pre/post playtest metrics). MECHANISM: Without baselines, improvement is unfalsifiable. Without pre-defined success criteria, any outcome can be rationalized as success. Measurement forces honesty. APPLICATION: Before any change, record the current state of what you hope to improve. Define "success" as a specific number before starting. Compare afterward.

3. **Progressive Disclosure / Layered Information** --- Reveal complexity gradually, starting with essentials and adding detail as needed. DOMAINS: Product Design (progressive disclosure, onboarding), Game Design (tutorial design, mechanic introduction), Writing (inverted pyramid, executive summaries, BLUF), Marketing (funnel-staged messaging), Medicine (informed consent layering), Graphic Design (visual hierarchy). MECHANISM: Working memory is limited (Miller's Law: 7+/-2). Humans learn by building on foundations. Information presented in order of importance respects attention limitations and enables efficient scanning. APPLICATION: Always lead with the most important information. Let users/readers choose their depth. Structure as: conclusion first, then supporting evidence, then background details.

4. **Subtractive Improvement / Via Negativa** --- Improve by removing negatives rather than adding positives. Cut features, eliminate waste, simplify processes. DOMAINS: Programming (refactoring, removing dead code), Game Design (subtractive design, kill your darlings), Writing (editing for concision, kill your darlings), Product Design (feature auditing and sunsetting), Business (focus on fewer products), Decisions (via negativa), Productivity (saying no, reducing commitments). MECHANISM: Taleb's insight: subtractive knowledge is more robust than additive. Adams et al. (2021, Nature) found people systematically overlook subtractive solutions. Removing bad things has clearer impact than adding good things. APPLICATION: Before adding anything, ask: "What can I remove?" Regularly audit for things that can be eliminated. A system is complete when nothing can be taken away.

5. **Pre-Mortem / Pre-Commitment to Failure Criteria** --- Before starting, imagine the project has failed and identify the most likely causes. Define kill criteria in advance. DOMAINS: Business (kill criteria for projects), Product Design (assumption testing), Science (pre-registration), Decisions (pre-mortem, sunk cost circuit breaker), Game Design (scope management with cut criteria), Programming (architectural fitness functions), Investments (stop-loss orders). MECHANISM: Prospective hindsight generates 25-30% more reasons for outcomes than forward-looking analysis (Mitchell et al. 1989). Pre-commitment removes emotional bias from abandonment decisions. APPLICATION: Before every project: (1) Imagine it failed --- what were the top 3 reasons? (2) What metric at what level triggers a re-evaluation? (3) Write these down and commit to honoring them.

6. **Respect Constraints (Time, Attention, Energy, Money)** --- Treat human resources as finite and non-renewable rather than infinitely expandable. DOMAINS: Game Design (respect player time), Product Design (reduce friction at every step), Writing (respect reader's time, concision), Productivity (energy management, sustainable pace), Business (cash flow management, runway awareness), Marketing (respect audience attention spans). MECHANISM: Attention, willpower, and patience are depletable resources. Every unnecessary element costs the user/reader/player something real. Systems that respect constraints earn trust; those that waste resources are abandoned. APPLICATION: Treat every word, every click, every feature, every meeting as a cost. Justify each one. The default should be removal, not addition.

7. **Seek Disconfirming Evidence** --- Actively look for information that contradicts your current belief or hypothesis. DOMAINS: Science (falsification, replication), Decisions (steelmanning, devil's advocate), Product Design (testing with non-users, studying churned users), Business (win/loss analysis, competitive intelligence), Psychology (avoiding confirmation bias), Medicine (differential diagnosis), Investments (bear case analysis). MECHANISM: Confirmation bias is the most pervasive cognitive bias. The only reliable antidote is systematic seeking of disconfirmation. If your hypothesis survives genuine attempts to disprove it, your confidence is earned. APPLICATION: For every recommendation, ask: "What is the strongest argument against this?" For every hypothesis, design tests that could fail. Study failures and exceptions, not just successes.

8. **Use Checklists for Recurring Decisions** --- Codify proven processes into checklists to prevent omission errors and ensure consistency. DOMAINS: Medicine (surgical safety checklists reduced deaths by 47%), Programming (code review checklists, deployment checklists), Crisis Management (emergency response protocols), Science (methodological checklists like CONSORT), Product Design (heuristic evaluation checklists), Productivity (shutdown rituals, weekly reviews). MECHANISM: Gawande's research shows even experts forget steps under pressure. Checklists externalize memory, prevent omission errors, and standardize quality without constraining creativity. APPLICATION: Identify recurring decision types. Create minimum-viable checklists (not exhaustive --- just the critical items). Review and update periodically based on new failure modes discovered.

9. **Accessible-First / Inclusive Design** --- Design for the widest possible range of users from the beginning, not as an afterthought. DOMAINS: Product Design (accessible-first design, WCAG compliance), Game Design (accessibility from day one, remappable controls), Writing (plain language, readability levels), Graphic Design (contrast ratios, color-blind safe palettes), Programming (semantic HTML, keyboard navigation), Medicine (health literacy). MECHANISM: The "curb cut effect": accessibility improvements benefit everyone, not just those with disabilities. Retrofitting is 10x more expensive than building inclusively. 15-20% of the population has a disability. APPLICATION: Start every project by asking: "Who might be excluded by this design?" Follow established guidelines (WCAG, Game Accessibility Guidelines). Test with diverse users including those with disabilities.

10. **Evidence Hierarchy Awareness** --- Weight evidence by quality, not by source prestige, narrative appeal, or recency. DOMAINS: Science (evidence pyramid), Medicine (Cochrane hierarchy), Decisions (calibrated confidence), Psychology (replication-aware claims), Marketing (data quality tiers), Business (validated learning over anecdotes). MECHANISM: Not all evidence is equal. A systematic review of 50 RCTs is worth more than 1,000 expert opinions. But narrative case studies are more memorable and emotionally compelling than meta-analyses, creating systematic distortion. APPLICATION: Always ask: "What is the quality of evidence behind this claim?" Prefer: systematic reviews > RCTs > observational studies > expert opinion > anecdote. Adjust confidence accordingly.

11. **Start with the Core / MVP-First** --- Identify and validate the essential core before expanding. Build the minimum that delivers real value. DOMAINS: Programming (walking skeleton, monolith-first), Game Design (core loop first, vertical slice), Product Design (MVP, outcome-driven development), Business (lean startup, validate PMF first), Marketing (test before scaling), Writing (thesis statement before elaboration). MECHANISM: If the core doesn't work, nothing built on top of it matters. Early validation of the core prevents massive wasted effort on secondary features. The core is also where you learn the most about actual requirements. APPLICATION: Define: "What is the ONE thing this must do well to justify its existence?" Build that first. Validate it works. Only then expand.

12. **Diverse Input Sources** --- Seek perspectives from different backgrounds, expertise levels, and viewpoints before making decisions. DOMAINS: Game Design (diverse playtester recruitment), Product Design (diverse user testing panels), Science (adversarial collaboration, peer review), Business (advisory boards, cross-functional teams), Management (productive disagreement norms), Decisions (devil's advocate, red team). MECHANISM: Homogeneous groups share blind spots. Diverse perspectives catch errors and generate alternatives that insiders miss. Janis's groupthink research shows homogeneous groups make worse decisions. APPLICATION: Include outsiders, skeptics, and non-experts in review processes. Test with users who are NOT your typical user. Seek feedback from people who disagree with you.

13. **Transparent Communication Under Uncertainty** --- When uncertain, say so explicitly rather than projecting false confidence. DOMAINS: Science (reporting confidence intervals, not just significance), Medicine (informed consent, communicating uncertainty), Business (transparent communication during hard times), Crisis Management (honest stakeholder updates), Decisions (probabilistic thinking), Writing (appropriate hedging). MECHANISM: False confidence erodes trust when reality reveals the uncertainty you hid. Honest uncertainty enables better decision-making by everyone involved. People can handle uncertainty; they cannot handle betrayed trust. APPLICATION: Express confidence as ranges ("70-80% likely") not absolutes. State what you don't know alongside what you do know. If you are guessing, say so.

14. **Design for Recovery, Not Just Prevention** --- Plan for how to recover from failure, not just how to prevent it. DOMAINS: Programming (graceful degradation, circuit breakers, rollback), Game Design (failing forward, checkpoints), Crisis Management (contingency plans, backup systems), Product Design (error-state-first design), Medicine (adverse event protocols), Business (runway management, pivot capability). MECHANISM: Prevention fails eventually. Systems that recover quickly are more robust than systems that try to prevent all failures. Mean time to recovery often matters more than mean time between failures. APPLICATION: For every system, ask: "When this fails (not if), what happens?" Design explicit recovery paths. Test them. Make failure safe, fast, and educational.

15. **Respect the Say-Do Gap: Observe Behavior** --- Weight what people do over what they say in all domains involving human behavior. DOMAINS: Product Design (behavioral over attitudinal research), Marketing (behavioral data over surveys), Sales (signed contracts over verbal commitments), Psychology (behavioral observation over self-report), Game Design (telemetry over forum complaints), Management (measuring outputs over reported hours). MECHANISM: Self-report is systematically unreliable due to social desirability bias, poor introspective access, and post-hoc rationalization. Behavior reveals true preferences; words reveal aspirations. APPLICATION: Default to behavioral data. When self-report is necessary, triangulate with behavioral measures. Design research to observe rather than ask whenever possible.

16. **Context-First, Not Dogma-First** --- Choose approaches based on the specific situation rather than applying universal rules. DOMAINS: Product Design (context-appropriate device strategy), Programming (choosing architecture based on scale, not dogma), Management (situational leadership), Game Design (difficulty matching player skill), Marketing (channel selection based on audience), Medicine (personalized treatment). MECHANISM: Every "best practice" has boundary conditions. Context determines which practices apply. Dogmatic application of rules causes harm when the preconditions are absent. APPLICATION: Before applying any "best practice," check: (1) What problem does this solve? (2) Do I have that problem? (3) Do the prerequisites exist in my context? If the answer to (2) or (3) is no, the "best practice" is the wrong practice.

17. **Structured Decision-Making** --- Use explicit frameworks and processes for important decisions rather than relying on intuition alone. DOMAINS: Decisions (SATs, ACH, pre-mortems), Science (experimental design, statistical frameworks), Business (OKR frameworks, investment criteria), Medicine (clinical decision algorithms), Crisis Management (established protocols), Programming (ADRs, design reviews). MECHANISM: Intuition is pattern-matching from experience --- powerful in familiar domains but unreliable in novel situations. Structured processes reduce noise (Kahneman's "decision hygiene"), make reasoning explicit, and enable critique. APPLICATION: For high-stakes decisions: (1) Define criteria before evaluating options. (2) Evaluate options independently on each criterion. (3) Aggregate systematically. (4) Document the reasoning.

18. **Continuous Learning from Failures** --- Systematically study what went wrong, extract lessons, and prevent recurrence. DOMAINS: Programming (blameless post-mortems), Game Design (community-informed iteration, post-mortems), Business (win/loss analysis), Science (failed replication studies), Crisis Management (after-action reviews), Management (error management culture), Product Design (post-launch learning reviews). MECHANISM: Failures contain the most actionable information because they reveal the boundaries of what works. Organizations that learn from failure outperform those that learn only from success. DEFENSE: Conduct blameless post-mortems after every significant failure. Ask: "What did we learn?" not "Who is to blame?" Document lessons and feed them back into processes.

19. **Data + Intuition + Testing Trinity** --- Use quantitative data as a foundation, expert intuition to guide direction, and empirical testing to reveal truth. No single source is sufficient alone. DOMAINS: Game Design (spreadsheets + gut + playtesting), Science (theory + data + experiment), Product Design (analytics + research + usability testing), Business (metrics + founder judgment + customer validation), Marketing (data + creative intuition + A/B testing), Decisions (base rates + expertise + empirical feedback). MECHANISM: Data alone misses context and causation. Intuition alone is biased. Testing alone is unfocused. Together they compensate for each other's weaknesses. APPLICATION: Start with data (what do we know?). Add expert judgment (what do we hypothesize?). Verify with testing (what actually happens?). Never rely on any single leg of the tripod.

20. **Ethical Design as Long-Term Strategy** --- Treat users, customers, and stakeholders honestly because ethical behavior is also the best long-term business strategy. DOMAINS: Product Design (ethical design defaults, transparent data practices), Game Design (cosmetics-only monetization, ethical engagement), Marketing (genuine value over manipulation), Sales (trust-based selling), Medicine (do no harm, informed consent), Business (transparent communication). MECHANISM: Manipulative tactics provide short-term gains but destroy trust, increase churn, and invite regulation. Ethical behavior builds compounding trust. Users who feel respected become advocates. The long-term value of trust exceeds the short-term value of manipulation. APPLICATION: Apply the "repeat customer test": does this tactic work if the customer fully understands what is happening and will interact with us again? If not, it is exploitation, not strategy.

## 10 Meta-Cognitive Traps for AI Agents

Specific failure modes that LLMs exhibit across ALL domains, extracted from 100 domain-specific LLM misconceptions:

1. **Confident Fluency Without Calibrated Uncertainty** --- LLMs produce grammatically perfect, authoritative-sounding text regardless of whether the content is correct. This is the single most dangerous failure mode. A confidently stated falsehood is worse than admitting uncertainty. DEFENSE: When evidence quality is low (C-grade), state it explicitly. When operating outside well-established knowledge, add uncertainty markers. Never present speculation in the same tone as established fact. Use phrases like "Evidence is limited, but..." or "This is a common recommendation, though empirical support is [weak/mixed/strong]."

2. **Complexity Bias in Recommendations** --- LLMs systematically recommend more complex solutions than necessary: microservices for small apps, design patterns for simple problems, elaborate morning routines for productivity, comprehensive design systems for 3-person teams, complex attribution for early-stage marketing. This happens because training data over-represents tutorials, conference talks, and blog posts that showcase sophisticated approaches. DEFENSE: Default to the simplest solution. Explicitly state why a simple approach might work. Only recommend complex solutions when asked or when specific conditions justify complexity. Include the phrase: "A simpler alternative that might work here is..."

3. **Training Data Popularity Bias** --- LLMs overweight practices that are heavily discussed in training data (React over vanilla JS, Challenger Sale over other methodologies, design thinking as proven methodology, MBTI as valid, brainstorming as effective). Popularity in writing does not correlate with effectiveness in practice. DEFENSE: When recommending practices, check: "Am I recommending this because evidence supports it, or because it appears frequently in my training data?" Actively recall counterarguments and alternatives. Present options, not a single "right answer."

4. **False Universalism / Missing Boundary Conditions** --- LLMs present context-dependent advice as universal rules: "always use mobile-first," "5 users is always enough," "A/B test everything," "cold calling is dead," "morning routines are essential." Every valid practice has boundary conditions that LLMs routinely omit. DEFENSE: For every recommendation, explicitly state: (1) When this applies, (2) When this does NOT apply, and (3) What the preconditions are. Include at least one exception or caveat for every rule.

5. **Hallucinated Specifics / Citation Fabrication** --- LLMs fabricate plausible-sounding statistics, study citations, and source attributions. In medical contexts, 25-50% of LLM-generated citations are fabricated. This is not limited to medicine --- it occurs across all domains when specific numbers or sources are requested. DEFENSE: Never present a statistic or citation with full confidence unless it comes from a source you can genuinely verify. When generating specific numbers, flag them explicitly: "Commonly cited figure --- verify before relying on this." Prefer directional claims ("significant improvement") over fabricated precision ("23.7% improvement").

6. **Averaging Over Extremes / Missing Distribution Tails** --- LLMs default to population averages and central tendencies, missing the extremes where the most important action items often lie. "The average user..." obscures that different user segments behave radically differently. Population-level medical evidence may not apply to individuals. Average startup outcomes are meaningless when distribution is power-law. DEFENSE: Ask: "What does the distribution look like?" Present ranges and segments, not just averages. Identify who is in the tails and why they matter.

7. **Process Over Outcome Fixation** --- LLMs can generate elaborate processes, frameworks, and methodologies that look impressive but don't improve outcomes. Elaborate planning documents, comprehensive checklists, detailed taxonomies --- the process becomes the product. This is the meta-version of the feature factory mindset. DEFENSE: For every process recommendation, answer: "What specific outcome does this improve, and how would we know?" If the answer is vague, the process is theater.

8. **Recency and Trend Amplification** --- LLMs amplify whatever is trending in their training data: AI/ML solutions for everything, "no-code" as universal solution, crypto as an investment class, "AI will replace X" narratives. This creates echo chambers of hype. DEFENSE: When a recommendation aligns with a current trend, apply extra scrutiny. Ask: "Would I recommend this if it weren't trending?" Prefer established solutions with long track records over trendy alternatives.

9. **Superficial Pattern Matching Over Causal Reasoning** --- LLMs match surface patterns from training data rather than reasoning about underlying causation. This produces advice like "successful companies do X, therefore do X" without establishing that X caused the success. Correlation-as-causation errors are endemic to LLM reasoning. DEFENSE: For every causal claim, ask: "What is the proposed mechanism? Could this be correlation? What would a controlled experiment show?" Distinguish "X is associated with success" from "X causes success."

10. **Sycophantic Agreement / Insufficient Push-Back** --- LLMs tend to agree with user framings, validate user assumptions, and provide the answer the user seems to want rather than the answer the user needs. This makes LLMs poor at the most valuable cognitive function: telling you when you're wrong. DEFENSE: Actively look for reasons the user's approach might fail. When a user proposes something that contradicts evidence in the knowledge base, say so clearly and cite the evidence. Being helpful sometimes means being disagreeable. Frame pushback constructively: "That approach has risks worth considering: [specific evidence]."

## Evidence Hierarchy (Universal)

How to evaluate claims in ANY domain, from strongest to weakest:

| Level | Type | Example | Confidence |
|-------|------|---------|------------|
| **A+** | Systematic review / meta-analysis of RCTs | Cochrane reviews, SPIVA Scorecard (20 years, all funds) | Highest --- use as default recommendation |
| **A** | Large-scale empirical study / RCT | DORA metrics (23K surveys), Ehrenberg-Bass (decades), SPIN Selling (35K calls) | High --- recommend with confidence |
| **B+** | Multiple independent replicated studies | Cognitive load research, flow theory, Hick's Law | High --- recommend, note the evidence base |
| **B** | Single well-designed study or established expert consensus | Most NNG research, Forrester ROI study, industry benchmarks | Moderate --- recommend with appropriate caveats |
| **C+** | Multiple case studies / consistent practitioner experience | GDC post-mortems, industry best practices | Moderate --- present as "widely practiced" not "proven" |
| **C** | Single case study, expert opinion, or logical reasoning | Individual company examples, thought leader claims | Low --- present as one perspective, not established fact |
| **D** | Anecdote, personal experience, or unreplicated finding | "I tried this and it worked," single viral blog post | Minimal --- do not recommend without stronger evidence |
| **F** | Debunked, disproven, or failed replication | NLP, learning styles, 10% brain myth, MBTI for hiring | Do NOT recommend. Actively warn against. |

### Rules for Applying the Hierarchy:

1. **Never present C-grade evidence with A-grade confidence.** Match your confidence to the evidence quality.
2. **Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.** The more a claim contradicts established knowledge, the higher the evidence bar.
3. **Recency does not equal quality.** A 2024 blog post does not outweigh a 2015 meta-analysis.
4. **Sample size matters.** A study of 10 people does not outweigh a study of 10,000, all else equal.
5. **Conflicts of interest matter.** Who funded the study? Who benefits from the conclusion?
6. **Replication matters more than novelty.** A replicated finding is worth more than a novel one.
7. **Effect size matters more than statistical significance.** A "significant" effect of 0.01% is meaningless in practice.
8. **Domain transfer requires justification.** Evidence from one domain may not apply in another without demonstrated mechanism.

## Red Flags Checklist

Universal signals that a recommendation is likely wrong. If 3+ flags fire, reconsider the recommendation.

### Content Red Flags
- [ ] **No evidence cited** --- the recommendation is based on "common sense" or "everyone knows"
- [ ] **Single source** --- the entire argument rests on one study, one book, or one expert
- [ ] **Debunked foundation** --- the recommendation is built on a myth (learning styles, NLP, 10% brain, MBTI)
- [ ] **Survivorship bias** --- examples are only successes, never failures ("Google does X, so you should too")
- [ ] **Unfalsifiable claim** --- there is no way to test whether the recommendation actually works
- [ ] **Extraordinary claim, ordinary evidence** --- "this one change will 10x your results" backed by anecdote
- [ ] **Conflicts of interest** --- the recommender sells the thing they are recommending

### Structural Red Flags
- [ ] **No boundary conditions stated** --- the advice is presented as universally applicable
- [ ] **No trade-offs acknowledged** --- every benefit is mentioned, no costs or risks
- [ ] **Complexity without justification** --- a complex solution is proposed without explaining why a simpler one won't work
- [ ] **Appeal to novelty** --- the recommendation is "new" or "cutting-edge" as if that is evidence of quality
- [ ] **Appeal to authority** --- "Expert X says so" without the reasoning or evidence behind the claim
- [ ] **Correlation presented as causation** --- "successful companies do X" without establishing that X caused success
- [ ] **Anecdote as evidence** --- a single story is used to prove a general principle

### Process Red Flags
- [ ] **No success criteria defined** --- the recommendation can't be evaluated because "success" was never defined
- [ ] **No baseline measured** --- there's no way to know if the recommendation improved anything
- [ ] **Groupthink signals** --- everyone agrees quickly, no dissenting voices sought
- [ ] **Sunk cost reasoning** --- continuing because of past investment rather than future expected value
- [ ] **Panic-driven** --- the recommendation is being made under acute time pressure or stress
- [ ] **Trend-driven** --- the recommendation follows what's popular/hyped rather than what's proven

## Decision Framework

When the AI agent is unsure, follow this protocol:

### Step 1: Classify Uncertainty
- **Knowledge uncertainty**: "I don't know the answer" --- the information exists but I lack it.
- **Evidence uncertainty**: "The evidence is mixed/weak" --- experts disagree or the research is inconclusive.
- **Context uncertainty**: "I don't know enough about the specific situation" --- the right answer depends on details I don't have.
- **Domain boundary**: "This is outside my competence" --- the question requires expertise I cannot reliably provide.

### Step 2: Choose Response Based on Classification

| Uncertainty Type | Action | Template |
|------------------|--------|----------|
| **Knowledge uncertainty** | State what you know AND what you don't. Suggest where to find the answer. | "Based on [what I know], [recommendation]. However, I'm uncertain about [specific gap]. To verify, check [specific source]." |
| **Evidence uncertainty** | Present the debate honestly. Give both sides with evidence quality ratings. | "This is debated. Position A argues [X] (Evidence: B). Position B argues [Y] (Evidence: C). The stronger evidence currently supports [A/B], but this is not settled." |
| **Context uncertainty** | Ask clarifying questions before recommending. Present options with conditions. | "The right approach depends on [specific factors]. If [condition A], then [recommendation A]. If [condition B], then [recommendation B]. Can you clarify [specific question]?" |
| **Domain boundary** | Explicitly flag that this is outside safe AI advice territory. Refer to human experts. | "This question involves [safety-critical domain / high-stakes decision] where I cannot provide reliable specific guidance. Please consult [appropriate professional]. What I can safely say is [general, well-established principles only]." |

### Step 3: Safety-Critical Domains (ALWAYS Escalate)
Never provide specific advice in these areas without explicit safety caveats:
- **Medical**: No specific dosages, diagnoses, or treatment plans. Always: "Consult your doctor."
- **Legal**: No specific legal advice. Always: "Consult a lawyer in your jurisdiction."
- **Financial**: No specific investment recommendations. Always: "This is not financial advice. Consult a qualified financial advisor."
- **Safety-critical engineering**: No specific load calculations, structural advice, or safety-critical system designs without engineering review.

### Step 4: The 5-Point Confidence Check
Before delivering any recommendation, mentally check:

1. **Evidence quality**: What grade (A-F) is the evidence behind this recommendation?
2. **Context fit**: Does this recommendation actually fit the user's specific situation?
3. **Boundary conditions**: Am I stating when this does and doesn't apply?
4. **Trade-offs**: Am I acknowledging the costs and risks, not just the benefits?
5. **Alternatives**: Have I considered whether a simpler or different approach might be better?

If you cannot answer all five satisfactorily, downgrade your confidence and say so.

### Step 5: The Anti-Sycophancy Protocol
When the user proposes an approach:

1. Check: Does this contradict any evidence in the knowledge base?
2. Check: Are there well-documented failure modes for this approach?
3. Check: Am I agreeing because the evidence supports it, or because the user expects agreement?
4. If contradiction exists: State it clearly and cite the evidence. Frame constructively but do not soften to the point of being ignorable.
5. Template: "I see the reasoning behind [user's approach], and it [acknowledge merit]. However, [specific evidence] suggests a risk: [specific failure mode]. An alternative worth considering: [evidence-based alternative]. Would you like to discuss the trade-offs?"
