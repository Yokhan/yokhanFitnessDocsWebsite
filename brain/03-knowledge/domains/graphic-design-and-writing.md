# Graphic Design: Evidence-Based Practices

## 50 BAD PRACTICES

1. **Using More Than 3 Fonts** — Combining 4+ typefaces in a single design creates visual chaos and undermines hierarchy. WHY BAD: The eye cannot establish a consistent reading pattern; each new font resets cognitive processing, increasing cognitive load by ~20%. INSTEAD: Limit to 2-3 fonts max (one display, one body, one optional accent). SOURCE: Smashing Magazine typographic best practices; Visme design rules. EVIDENCE: B (expert consensus, eye-tracking studies)

2. **Canva Template Thinking** — Treating templates as finished design, swapping text/images without understanding underlying grid, hierarchy, or spacing logic. WHY BAD: Templates are starting points, not solutions; ignoring their structural logic produces generic, misaligned work that looks identical to thousands of others. INSTEAD: Use templates to learn principles, then customize structure, not just content. SOURCE: Noble Desktop common design mistakes; design education consensus. EVIDENCE: B (industry expert consensus)

3. **Ignoring Whitespace** — Filling every pixel with content, icons, or decorative elements. WHY BAD: Research shows whitespace increases comprehension by up to 20% (Wichita State University study). Cluttered designs increase cognitive load and reduce information retention. INSTEAD: Use generous margins, padding, and breathing room; let negative space guide the eye. SOURCE: Wichita State University readability research; NN/G web design guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (controlled studies)

4. **Poor Color Contrast (WCAG Violations)** — Using text/background combinations below 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text or 3:1 for large text. WHY BAD: 1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women are color blind; low contrast fails WCAG 2.1 AA standards and is illegible for ~15% of the population. INSTEAD: Use WebAIM Contrast Checker; minimum 4.5:1 for body text, 7:1 for AAA. SOURCE: W3C WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3; WebAIM accessibility guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (W3C standard, legal requirements)

5. **Complementary Colors at 50/50 Split** — Using two complementary colors in equal proportions across a design. WHY BAD: Equal distribution creates visual competition where neither color leads; can produce vibrating optical effects that strain eyes (especially purple/yellow, red/green). INSTEAD: Apply the 60-30-10 rule: dominant color 60%, secondary 30%, accent 10%. SOURCE: IxDF complementary colors research; Supercharge Design UI color mistakes. EVIDENCE: B (design theory + practical evidence)

6. **Stock Photo Overuse** — Using generic stock photography as primary visual communication. WHY BAD: 39% of marketers report stock photos are their worst-performing visual assets. Same images appear across thousands of sites (e.g., Jennifer Anderson's 1996 stock photo appeared on Samsung, Microsoft, US Bank simultaneously). Erodes brand authenticity. INSTEAD: Commission custom photography or illustration; use stock sparingly and only for supporting roles. SOURCE: CXL stock photography research; MarketingExperiments A/B testing. EVIDENCE: A (A/B tests showing real photos convert better)

7. **Trend-Chasing Design** — Redesigning brand identity to match every emerging design trend (glassmorphism, brutalism, etc.). WHY BAD: Creates disjointed brand image over time, confuses customers, dilutes uniqueness. When design is tied to a specific era, it becomes irrelevant quickly. Coca-Cola and Apple succeeded through timeless principles. INSTEAD: Build on timeless principles (simplicity, clarity, versatility); incorporate trends only as subtle accents. SOURCE: DesignScout timeless vs trendy research; Dieter Rams design principles. EVIDENCE: B (case studies of brand longevity)

8. **Complex Logo = Better Logo Myth** — Believing detailed, intricate logos are more impressive and professional. WHY BAD: Research shows simple logos enhance competence perception (Journal of Business Research, 2025). Complex logos are harder to reproduce at small sizes, on merchandise, in monochrome. INSTEAD: Design for scalability and versatility; test at 16px favicon and billboard size. SOURCE: ScienceDirect logo complexity research; Journal of Marketing Communications. EVIDENCE: A (peer-reviewed research, though nuanced: complex logos can signal warmth/luxury)

9. **Ignoring Typography Hierarchy** — Using same size, weight, and style for all text elements. WHY BAD: Without visual hierarchy, users cannot distinguish headings from body from captions; all information appears equally (un)important. Increases time-to-comprehension significantly. INSTEAD: Establish clear hierarchy: H1 > H2 > H3 > body > caption with distinct size, weight, and spacing ratios. SOURCE: Toptal typographic hierarchy guide; Figma typography design guide. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking studies confirm hierarchical scanning)

10. **Using Raster Images for Logos/Icons** — Creating logos and icons in Photoshop (raster/pixel-based) rather than Illustrator (vector). WHY BAD: Raster images become blurry/pixelated when scaled up; unusable for print, large format, or responsive web. INSTEAD: Always create logos and icons as vector files (SVG, AI, EPS); export rasters only for specific use cases. SOURCE: Visme graphic design rules; Noble Desktop design mistakes. EVIDENCE: B (technical fact + industry standard)

11. **Ignoring Grid Systems Entirely** — Placing elements by feel without any underlying grid structure. WHY BAD: Without a grid, alignment is inconsistent, creating visual tension and unprofessional appearance. The eye detects misalignment as small as 1-2 pixels. INSTEAD: Use a baseline grid for typography and a column grid for layout; even simple grids dramatically improve coherence. SOURCE: NN/G grid systems in interface design; Designlab grid systems guide. EVIDENCE: B (design theory + usability research)

12. **Rigid Grid Adherence** — Never breaking the grid, treating it as a cage rather than a guide. WHY BAD: Overly rigid grid use produces monotonous, lifeless layouts that fail to emphasize key elements. INSTEAD: Master the grid first, then break it intentionally for emphasis; ensure the surrounding composition supports the deviation. SOURCE: Prototypr design systems pitfalls; Medium grid mistakes analysis. EVIDENCE: B (expert consensus)

13. **Using Fixed Pixels for Responsive Grids** — Building grids with only pixel values instead of relative units. WHY BAD: Layouts break on different screen sizes and devices; modern users access content on screens from 320px to 3840px+. INSTEAD: Use relative units (%, fr, em, rem) for grid definitions; define breakpoints for column count changes (4 mobile, 8 tablet, 12 desktop). SOURCE: NN/G responsive grid guidelines; Elementor grid design rules. EVIDENCE: B (technical standard + usability data)

14. **Confusing DPI and PPI** — Treating DPI (dots per inch, printer metric) and PPI (pixels per inch, screen metric) as interchangeable. WHY BAD: Setting 72 PPI images for print produces blurry output; obsessing over DPI for screen is meaningless. Causes constant file preparation errors. INSTEAD: Use 300 PPI at actual print size for print; understand that screens only care about pixel dimensions. SOURCE: Mixam DPI vs PPI guide; 99designs PPI vs DPI explanation. EVIDENCE: B (technical fact)

15. **Designing Print at Screen Resolution** — Creating print materials at 72 PPI. WHY BAD: Print requires 300+ PPI for sharp output; 72 PPI images that look fine on screen print at roughly 1/4 the needed detail. INSTEAD: Always set up print files at 300 PPI minimum; include bleed (3mm/0.125in) and safe zones. SOURCE: UPrinting DPI vs PPI guide; Zeka Design print vs digital differences. EVIDENCE: B (technical standard)

16. **Designing Digital at Print Resolution Unnecessarily** — Creating web assets at 300 PPI, producing files 10-20x larger than needed. WHY BAD: Bloated file sizes slow page load (every 100ms delay reduces conversions by 7% per Akamai); wastes bandwidth and storage. INSTEAD: Export web images at 72-150 PPI; use modern formats (WebP, AVIF) for compression. SOURCE: 4over4 print vs web resolution guide; Akamai performance research. EVIDENCE: A (performance metrics data)

17. **Using Too Many Colors** — Employing 6+ distinct colors without a systematic palette. WHY BAD: Tires users visually, creates confusion about information hierarchy, makes the design look amateur and unfocused. INSTEAD: Define a palette of 3-5 colors with clear roles (primary, secondary, accent, neutral, semantic). SOURCE: Supercharge Design 8 UI color mistakes; Material Design color system. EVIDENCE: B (design system best practices)

18. **Pure Black (#000000) on Pure White (#FFFFFF)** — Using maximum contrast for body text. WHY BAD: Creates excessive luminance contrast causing halation (text appears to vibrate) especially on screens; causes eye strain during extended reading. INSTEAD: Use near-black (#1a1a1a to #333333) on off-white (#f5f5f5 to #fafafa) for body text. SOURCE: Apple Human Interface Guidelines; UX research on reading comfort. EVIDENCE: B (accessibility research + eye strain studies)

19. **Centering Everything** — Center-aligning all text and elements as default. WHY BAD: Center-aligned body text creates ragged edges on both sides, making it significantly harder to find the start of each line. Eye-tracking shows left-aligned text is read 30% faster for Western languages. INSTEAD: Left-align body text; reserve center alignment for short headings, invitations, or single-line elements. SOURCE: NN/G text alignment research; typographic best practices. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking studies)

20. **Decorative Fonts for Body Text** — Using script, display, or novelty fonts for paragraphs of text. WHY BAD: Decorative fonts are designed for short bursts (headlines, logos); at body sizes they become illegible, reducing reading speed by 40-60%. INSTEAD: Use neutral, high-legibility typefaces for body (Inter, Source Sans, Georgia); reserve display fonts for headlines. SOURCE: MIT AgeLab legibility research; typographic readability studies. EVIDENCE: A (reading speed research)

21. **Ignoring Color Blindness** — Relying solely on color to convey information (red = error, green = success). WHY BAD: 8% of men have color vision deficiency; red/green distinctions are invisible to the most common type (deuteranopia). INSTEAD: Always pair color with a secondary indicator: icons, patterns, text labels, or shape. SOURCE: WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.1 Use of Color; WebAIM color accessibility. EVIDENCE: A (medical data + accessibility standard)

22. **Low-Quality Image Stretching** — Stretching small images to fill large spaces, or distorting aspect ratios. WHY BAD: Produces pixelated or distorted visuals that immediately signal unprofessionalism; breaks user trust. INSTEAD: Use appropriately sized source images; crop rather than stretch; use object-fit: cover in CSS. SOURCE: Visme design rules; Noble Desktop common mistakes. EVIDENCE: B (visual quality standard)

23. **Inconsistent Spacing** — Using random margins, padding, and gaps throughout a design. WHY BAD: Inconsistent spacing breaks visual rhythm and makes layouts feel chaotic. The Gestalt principle of proximity means spacing directly communicates relationships between elements. INSTEAD: Define a spacing scale (4px, 8px, 16px, 24px, 32px, 48px, 64px) and apply consistently. SOURCE: designsystems.com spacing guide; Material Design spacing system. EVIDENCE: B (Gestalt psychology + design system practice)

24. **Orphans and Widows in Typography** — Leaving single words or short lines at the top/bottom of columns or pages. WHY BAD: Creates awkward visual gaps and disrupts reading flow; signals lack of typographic care. INSTEAD: Adjust tracking, column width, or text editing to eliminate orphans/widows. SOURCE: Typography best practices (Bringhurst "Elements of Typographic Style"). EVIDENCE: C (traditional typography standard)

25. **Ignoring Line Length** — Setting body text across the full width of a widescreen monitor (120+ characters per line). WHY BAD: Optimal line length is 50-75 characters; beyond 80, the eye struggles to track back to the start of the next line, reducing reading speed and comprehension. Research shows 27% reading speed improvement for dyslexic readers with shorter lines. INSTEAD: Constrain body text to 50-75 characters per line (~600px max-width for body text). SOURCE: Baymard Institute line length research; classic typography research (Bringhurst). EVIDENCE: A (reading speed studies)

26. **Using Shadows/Effects as Crutch** — Adding drop shadows, glows, bevels, and embossing to compensate for weak composition. WHY BAD: Excessive effects create visual noise, distract from content, and date the design to early-2000s aesthetics. INSTEAD: Achieve depth through spacing, layering, and subtle elevation; if using shadows, keep them soft, diffused, and purposeful. SOURCE: Material Design elevation system; modern UI design consensus. EVIDENCE: B (design evolution evidence)

27. **Designing Without a Brief** — Starting visual design without understanding the audience, objectives, and constraints. WHY BAD: Design without purpose produces decoration, not communication. Results in endless revisions as the actual goals emerge retroactively. INSTEAD: Always start with a brief defining audience, objectives, constraints, deliverables, and success metrics. SOURCE: Design Council Double Diamond process; AIGA design process guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (project management research)

28. **Icon Overload** — Using icons for every possible concept, including abstract ones icons cannot represent. WHY BAD: Icons work for concrete concepts (home, search, mail) but fail for abstract ones (synergy, efficiency, holistic); users cannot decode ambiguous icons without labels. NN/G research shows icon-plus-label outperforms icon-alone. INSTEAD: Use icons sparingly for universally understood concepts; always pair with text labels. SOURCE: NN/G icon usability research; Material Design iconography guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (usability testing)

29. **Ignoring Responsive Design** — Designing only for desktop and hoping mobile will work itself out. WHY BAD: Mobile devices account for ~70% of digital media time (Comscore). Designs that break on mobile lose the majority of the audience. INSTEAD: Design mobile-first or at minimum design for both breakpoints simultaneously. SOURCE: Comscore mobile usage data; Google mobile-first indexing. EVIDENCE: A (usage statistics)

30. **Using Color Names Instead of Systems** — Picking colors by name ("I want blue") rather than using a color system with defined roles. WHY BAD: "Blue" is meaningless without specifying hue, saturation, lightness, and its role (primary, accent, semantic). Leads to inconsistent palettes. INSTEAD: Define colors by role and specify values; use HSL for intuitive adjustment; build a token system. SOURCE: Material Design color system; design systems best practices. EVIDENCE: B (design system methodology)

31. **Justified Text Without Hyphenation** — Using justified alignment for body text without enabling proper hyphenation. WHY BAD: Creates "rivers" of whitespace running through paragraphs; word spacing becomes wildly uneven, reducing readability. INSTEAD: If using justified text, enable hyphenation and use a narrow column width; prefer left-aligned for web. SOURCE: Bringhurst "Elements of Typographic Style"; CSS typography best practices. EVIDENCE: B (typographic tradition + readability research)

32. **Skipping Visual QA** — Not reviewing designs at actual size on actual devices before delivery. WHY BAD: What looks perfect at 50% zoom on a 27" monitor may be illegible on a phone or blurry in print. Context-dependent failures go undetected. INSTEAD: Always proof at 100% on target devices; print test pages for print work; use real device previews. SOURCE: Design process best practices; Noble Desktop common mistakes. EVIDENCE: B (professional practice standard)

33. **Overusing Gradients** — Applying gradients to every surface, text, and element. WHY BAD: Excessive gradients create visual noise, reduce text legibility (especially on variable backgrounds), and can appear dated. INSTEAD: Use gradients purposefully for specific elements (hero backgrounds, illustrations); keep text on solid backgrounds. SOURCE: UI design best practices; Zeka Design common mistakes. EVIDENCE: B (design trend analysis)

34. **Neglecting Alignment Principles** — Elements placed at seemingly random positions without edge or center alignment relationships. WHY BAD: Misalignment as small as a few pixels creates subconscious tension; the eye detects irregularity even when the viewer cannot articulate it. INSTEAD: Align every element to at least one other element; use alignment guides/snapping. SOURCE: Robin Williams "The Non-Designer's Design Book"; Gestalt alignment principle. EVIDENCE: B (visual perception research)

35. **Using Clip Art** — Employing clip art or low-quality free illustrations in professional work. WHY BAD: Inconsistent illustration styles, low resolution, and dated aesthetics immediately signal amateurism and erode trust. INSTEAD: Use consistent illustration systems (Humaaans, unDraw, custom illustrations) or high-quality icon sets. SOURCE: Brand design best practices; SVGator design mistakes. EVIDENCE: B (professional standard)

36. **Ignoring File Format Requirements** — Delivering JPEGs for logos, PNGs for print, or TIFFs for web. WHY BAD: Wrong formats cause quality loss (JPEG artifacts on sharp edges), unnecessary file size (PNG for photographs), or incompatibility (TIFF for web). INSTEAD: SVG/AI for logos, JPEG/WebP for photographs on web, TIFF/PDF for print, PNG for graphics with transparency. SOURCE: File format best practices; Adobe format guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (technical standard)

37. **Kerning Neglect** — Not adjusting default kerning for headlines and display text. WHY BAD: Auto-kerning works for body text but creates visible gaps in headlines (especially with letters like AV, WA, To, LY). Poor kerning makes text look unprofessional. INSTEAD: Manually kern all headlines and display text; pay special attention to letter combinations with diagonal strokes. SOURCE: Typography best practices; Visme design rules. EVIDENCE: B (typographic standard)

38. **Designing in CMYK for Screen / RGB for Print** — Not converting color modes for the target medium. WHY BAD: RGB colors (especially vibrant blues and greens) shift dramatically in CMYK; designs that look brilliant on screen print dull and muddy. INSTEAD: Design in the target color space from the start; convert early and adjust accordingly. SOURCE: Print design fundamentals; Zeka Design print vs digital. EVIDENCE: B (technical fact, color science)

39. **One-Size-Fits-All Social Media Graphics** — Creating a single graphic and posting it across all platforms without adaptation. WHY BAD: Each platform has different aspect ratios, safe zones, and text overlay areas (Instagram: 1:1/4:5, Stories: 9:16, LinkedIn: 1.91:1, Twitter: 16:9). INSTEAD: Create platform-specific versions or use responsive templates with adjustable safe zones. SOURCE: Social media design guidelines; platform specification documents. EVIDENCE: B (platform technical requirements)

40. **Overdesigning Data Visualizations** — Adding 3D effects, excessive decoration, and chartjunk to graphs and charts. WHY BAD: Edward Tufte's research shows decorative elements reduce data comprehension; 3D charts distort proportional relationships, making data misleading. INSTEAD: Maximize data-ink ratio; use clear labels, appropriate chart types, and minimal decoration. SOURCE: Edward Tufte "The Visual Display of Quantitative Information"; data visualization research. EVIDENCE: A (Tufte's data-ink ratio research)

41. **Ignoring Cultural Color Meanings** — Using colors without considering cultural context (white for celebration in Western cultures, mourning in many Asian cultures). WHY BAD: Color associations vary dramatically across cultures; using red for "stop/danger" fails in China where red means prosperity. INSTEAD: Research cultural color associations for your target audience; avoid relying solely on Western color psychology. SOURCE: Color Matters cultural color research; cross-cultural design studies. EVIDENCE: B (anthropological research)

42. **Text Over Busy Images Without Overlay** — Placing text directly on complex photographs without a contrast-ensuring overlay. WHY BAD: Text becomes illegible against varied backgrounds; some words readable, others lost in similarly-colored image areas. Fails WCAG contrast requirements. INSTEAD: Use semi-transparent overlays, gradient scrims, or text shadows; ensure contrast ratio meets WCAG 4.5:1 minimum. SOURCE: WCAG 1.4.3 contrast requirements; web design best practices. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility standard)

43. **Designing for the Client, Not the Audience** — Making design decisions based on client personal preference rather than audience needs. WHY BAD: The client's favorite color or personal aesthetic preferences may alienate the actual target audience. Design serves communication goals, not personal taste. INSTEAD: Present design decisions with audience research, user personas, and competitive analysis as justification. SOURCE: Design Council user-centered design; AIGA design ethics. EVIDENCE: B (UCD methodology)

44. **Neglecting Hierarchy of Information** — Giving all information equal visual weight (same size, same color, same position). WHY BAD: Without hierarchy, users cannot quickly identify what matters; they either scan everything superficially or abandon the content entirely. INSTEAD: Rank information by importance; use size, color, weight, and position to create a clear visual path. SOURCE: NN/G visual hierarchy research; Toptal typographic hierarchy. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking research)

45. **Using Unrelated Imagery** — Choosing images based on aesthetic appeal rather than relevance to the message. WHY BAD: Creates cognitive dissonance; the audience tries to connect image to content, fails, and either gets confused or loses trust. INSTEAD: Every image should reinforce, illustrate, or extend the textual message; if an image needs explanation, it's wrong. SOURCE: NN/G image usability research; content design best practices. EVIDENCE: B (usability studies)

46. **Animated Everything** — Adding animation to every element on entry, hover, and scroll. WHY BAD: Excessive animation causes motion sickness (vestibular disorders affect ~35% of adults over 40), distracts from content, and slows page performance. WCAG 2.3.3 requires animation control. INSTEAD: Use animation purposefully for state changes and user feedback; respect prefers-reduced-motion; limit to 300ms transitions. SOURCE: WCAG 2.1 SC 2.3.3; A11Y animation guidelines; vestibular disorder research. EVIDENCE: A (medical/accessibility research)

47. **Ignoring Visual Weight Balance** — Placing all heavy elements on one side of a composition. WHY BAD: Creates a lopsided, uncomfortable feeling; the eye is drawn to one area while the rest feels empty and purposeless. INSTEAD: Balance visual weight (size, color density, complexity) across the composition; asymmetrical balance is valid but must be intentional. SOURCE: Design composition principles; Gestalt theory of visual balance. EVIDENCE: B (visual perception psychology)

48. **Auto-Playing Background Video** — Using auto-playing video backgrounds on web pages. WHY BAD: Consumes bandwidth (especially on mobile data), distracts from content, causes accessibility issues for motion-sensitive users, and often fails to load gracefully. INSTEAD: Use static hero images with optional video play button; respect user choice and bandwidth. SOURCE: NN/G video usability research; web performance best practices. EVIDENCE: B (usability + performance data)

49. **Inconsistent Brand Application** — Using different logo versions, colors, and type treatments across touchpoints. WHY BAD: Brand recognition requires consistency; Lucidpress research shows consistent brand presentation increases revenue by up to 33%. INSTEAD: Create and enforce brand guidelines covering logo usage, color palette, typography, spacing, and tone. SOURCE: Lucidpress brand consistency report; brand management research. EVIDENCE: A (revenue impact data)

50. **Skipping Accessibility Testing** — Not testing designs for accessibility before launch. WHY BAD: ~15% of the world population has some form of disability (WHO). Inaccessible design excludes users and creates legal liability (ADA, EAA). INSTEAD: Test with screen readers, keyboard navigation, contrast checkers, and real users with disabilities; follow WCAG 2.1 AA minimum. SOURCE: WHO disability statistics; WCAG 2.1 guidelines; ADA/EAA legal requirements. EVIDENCE: A (legal requirements + demographic data)


## 50 BEST PRACTICES

1. **Establish Clear Typography Hierarchy** — Define distinct styles for H1, H2, H3, body, caption, and overline with consistent size, weight, and spacing ratios. WHY GOOD: Eye-tracking research confirms users scan hierarchically; clear hierarchy reduces time-to-comprehension by up to 47%. SOURCE: Toptal typographic hierarchy; NN/G scanning patterns research. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking studies)

2. **Apply the 60-30-10 Color Rule** — Allocate 60% dominant color, 30% secondary, 10% accent across any design composition. WHY GOOD: Creates natural visual balance and guides the eye; provides enough variety without chaos. Derived from interior design and validated in UI/graphic design. SOURCE: IxDF color theory; Material Design color guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (design theory with practical validation)

3. **Design with a Grid System** — Use column grids (12-column for web, modular for editorial) as the structural foundation. WHY GOOD: Grids ensure alignment, consistency, and proportional relationships. NN/G research shows grid-based layouts are perceived as more professional and are easier to scan. SOURCE: NN/G grid systems research; Designlab grid systems history. EVIDENCE: A (usability research)

4. **Meet WCAG 2.1 AA Contrast Minimums** — Ensure 4.5:1 contrast for normal text, 3:1 for large text, 3:1 for UI components. WHY GOOD: Makes content readable for the widest possible audience including the 300M+ people with color vision deficiency worldwide; increasingly a legal requirement. SOURCE: W3C WCAG 2.1; WebAIM contrast guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (international standard)

5. **Limit Line Length to 50-75 Characters** — Constrain body text to optimal reading width. WHY GOOD: Classic typography research and modern UX studies confirm this range produces fastest reading speed and best comprehension. Dyslexic readers gain 27% speed improvement. SOURCE: Baymard Institute readability research; Bringhurst typographic standards. EVIDENCE: A (reading speed studies)

6. **Use Generous Line Spacing (1.4-1.6x)** — Set line-height between 1.4 and 1.6 times the font size for body text. WHY GOOD: Improves reading accuracy significantly; research shows tight leading causes line-skipping errors and increases eye strain. SOURCE: Typographic readability research; WCAG 1.4.12 text spacing guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (readability research)

7. **Follow Dieter Rams' "As Little Design as Possible"** — Remove any element that does not serve a communicative or functional purpose. WHY GOOD: Every unnecessary element competes for attention and dilutes the message. Rams' principle of reduction has been validated by 60+ years of product success (Braun, Apple). SOURCE: Dieter Rams 10 Principles; Design Museum analysis. EVIDENCE: B (design history + commercial success)

8. **Prioritize Custom Photography** — Use original, brand-specific photography over stock images where budget allows. WHY GOOD: A/B tests show real photos convert better than stock; 39% of marketers identify stock photos as worst-performing assets. Custom images build authenticity and trust. SOURCE: CXL A/B testing research; MarketingExperiments real vs stock studies. EVIDENCE: A (A/B test data)

9. **Design Mobile-First** — Start design process at smallest screen size and progressively enhance for larger screens. WHY GOOD: 70%+ of digital media consumption is on mobile (Comscore); mobile-first ensures core content works on the majority device, with desktop as enhancement. SOURCE: Comscore mobile data; Google mobile-first indexing. EVIDENCE: A (usage statistics + search ranking impact)

10. **Use Consistent Spacing Scale** — Define a spacing system based on a base unit (typically 4px or 8px) with predictable multipliers. WHY GOOD: Consistent spacing creates visual rhythm and reinforces the Gestalt principle of proximity, making relationships between elements instantly clear. SOURCE: Material Design spacing system; designsystems.com spacing guide. EVIDENCE: B (Gestalt psychology + design system practice)

11. **Test at Actual Size on Real Devices** — Review all designs on target devices at 100% scale before delivery. WHY GOOD: Catches legibility issues, touch target problems, and visual errors that zoom or simulation misses; reduces revision cycles by 40-60%. SOURCE: Design QA best practices; responsive design testing methodology. EVIDENCE: B (professional practice)

12. **Use Vector Format for All Scalable Assets** — Create logos, icons, and illustrations as vectors (SVG, AI). WHY GOOD: Vectors scale infinitely without quality loss; essential for responsive design across screen sizes from watch to billboard; smaller file sizes for simple graphics. SOURCE: Web performance best practices; logo design standards. EVIDENCE: B (technical standard)

13. **Apply Gestalt Principles Consciously** — Use proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, and figure-ground to organize visual information. WHY GOOD: Gestalt principles describe how humans actually perceive visual grouping; designing with them (rather than against them) reduces cognitive load. SOURCE: Gestalt psychology (Wertheimer, 1923); NN/G Gestalt principles for designers. EVIDENCE: A (foundational psychology research)

14. **Create and Enforce Brand Guidelines** — Document logo usage, color palette, typography, spacing, imagery style, and tone of voice in a comprehensive guide. WHY GOOD: Consistent brand presentation increases revenue by up to 33% (Lucidpress); reduces design production time and eliminates guesswork. SOURCE: Lucidpress brand consistency report; brand management research. EVIDENCE: A (revenue impact data)

15. **Use Color Purposefully for Information** — Assign specific semantic meanings to colors (red = destructive, green = success, yellow = warning) and pair with secondary indicators. WHY GOOD: Creates intuitive interfaces while remaining accessible; pairing color with icons/text ensures colorblind users are not excluded. SOURCE: WCAG 1.4.1 Use of Color; Material Design color semantics. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility standard)

16. **Implement Progressive Disclosure** — Show only essential information first; reveal details on demand. WHY GOOD: Reduces initial cognitive load; users process core message without overwhelm. NN/G research shows progressive disclosure improves task completion rates. SOURCE: NN/G progressive disclosure research; information architecture best practices. EVIDENCE: A (usability studies)

17. **Pair Fonts by Contrast, Not Similarity** — Combine typefaces with distinct characteristics (serif + sans-serif, geometric + humanist) rather than similar ones. WHY GOOD: Contrast creates clear hierarchy and visual interest; similar fonts look like mistakes. Match x-heights for harmonious reading. SOURCE: Smashing Magazine font pairing best practices; MyFonts pairing myths. EVIDENCE: B (typographic theory + practical examples)

18. **Design for the Audience, Not the Client** — Base design decisions on user research, personas, and audience data rather than personal preferences. WHY GOOD: User-centered design produces measurably better outcomes; design that resonates with the actual audience drives engagement and conversion. SOURCE: Design Council Double Diamond; ISO 9241-210 user-centered design. EVIDENCE: A (UCD methodology + usability metrics)

19. **Optimize Image File Sizes for Web** — Compress images and use modern formats (WebP, AVIF) for web delivery. WHY GOOD: Page speed directly affects conversion (every 100ms delay = 7% conversion loss per Akamai); images typically account for 50-80% of page weight. SOURCE: Akamai performance research; Google PageSpeed guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (performance impact data)

20. **Respect Cultural Context in Color Choices** — Research color associations specific to target audience culture before finalizing palettes. WHY GOOD: Color meanings vary significantly across cultures; white means purity in West but mourning in parts of Asia; red means danger in West but prosperity in China. SOURCE: Color Matters cross-cultural research; international design guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (anthropological research)

21. **Use 16px Minimum Body Text for Web** — Set web body text at 16px (1rem) minimum. WHY GOOD: Below 16px, readability drops significantly on mobile devices; 16px is the browser default for a reason—it matches comfortable reading distance for average vision. SOURCE: WCAG readability guidelines; Material Design typography scale. EVIDENCE: B (readability research + browser standards)

22. **Maximize Data-Ink Ratio in Visualizations** — Remove all non-data-bearing decoration from charts and graphs. WHY GOOD: Tufte's research shows that maximizing the proportion of ink used to present data (vs. decoration) directly improves comprehension accuracy. SOURCE: Edward Tufte "The Visual Display of Quantitative Information." EVIDENCE: A (information design research)

23. **Design With Intentional Visual Weight** — Balance asymmetrical compositions using size, density, color, and position to create equilibrium. WHY GOOD: Balanced compositions feel stable and professional; imbalanced ones create subconscious tension that distracts from the message. SOURCE: Visual composition principles; design education fundamentals. EVIDENCE: B (visual perception psychology)

24. **Support Dark Mode** — Provide a dark color scheme alternative, not just an inverted version. WHY GOOD: ~80% of users prefer dark mode on mobile (Android Authority survey); reduces eye strain in low-light; saves battery on OLED screens. Requires dedicated palette, not just inversion. SOURCE: Material Design dark theme guidelines; Android Authority dark mode survey. EVIDENCE: B (usage preference data)

25. **Use Accessible Touch Targets (48x48dp Minimum)** — Ensure all interactive elements meet minimum touch target size. WHY GOOD: WCAG 2.5.5 and Material Design specify 48x48dp minimum; smaller targets cause tap errors especially for users with motor impairments, older adults, and one-handed use. SOURCE: WCAG 2.5.5 Target Size; Material Design touch target guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility standard + usability testing)

26. **Apply the Squint Test** — Squint at your design to see if hierarchy and grouping remain clear when details blur. WHY GOOD: Simulates how users quickly scan; if the main elements, groups, and hierarchy are not visible while squinting, the design lacks sufficient visual contrast and structure. SOURCE: Design review technique; visual design education. EVIDENCE: C (practical technique)

27. **Use Whitespace as a Design Element** — Intentionally plan negative space rather than treating it as leftover area. WHY GOOD: Whitespace increases comprehension by up to 20% (Wichita State); luxury brands use generous whitespace to signal quality; Google's homepage is a masterclass. SOURCE: Wichita State University whitespace research; luxury brand design analysis. EVIDENCE: A (controlled studies)

28. **Establish Visual Anchors** — Create focal points that draw the eye first, then guide it through the composition. WHY GOOD: Without a focal point, the eye wanders without purpose; visual anchors create entry points and reading paths that ensure key information is seen first. SOURCE: Eye-tracking research; composition theory. EVIDENCE: B (eye-tracking + design theory)

29. **Design Components, Not Pages** — Build reusable, consistent components that compose into pages rather than designing each page from scratch. WHY GOOD: Component-based design ensures consistency, speeds production, and makes updates systematic rather than ad-hoc. SOURCE: Atomic Design (Brad Frost); design system methodology. EVIDENCE: B (design system practice)

30. **Use Proper Image Aspect Ratios Per Platform** — Adapt visual assets to each platform's specifications. WHY GOOD: Cropped or letterboxed images signal laziness and reduce engagement; platform-native sizing maximizes visible area and engagement. SOURCE: Platform design specifications; social media marketing research. EVIDENCE: B (platform guidelines)

31. **Implement a Type Scale** — Use a mathematical ratio (1.25, 1.333, 1.5, etc.) to generate consistent heading and body sizes. WHY GOOD: Mathematical scales create natural proportion relationships; the eye perceives these as harmonious. Common in music and architecture for centuries. SOURCE: Modular Scale (Tim Brown); typography design systems. EVIDENCE: B (proportional harmony theory + design practice)

32. **Include Focus States for Interactive Elements** — Design visible focus indicators for keyboard navigation. WHY GOOD: ~8% of users navigate by keyboard; WCAG 2.4.7 requires visible focus indicators. Without them, keyboard users literally cannot see where they are on the page. SOURCE: WCAG 2.4.7 Focus Visible; keyboard accessibility guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility standard + legal requirement)

33. **Use Real Content in Design** — Design with actual content rather than Lorem Ipsum whenever possible. WHY GOOD: Real content reveals layout problems (too long, too short, special characters, wrapping) that placeholder text hides. "Content precedes design" (Jeffrey Zeldman). SOURCE: A List Apart content-first design; Zeldman "Designing with Web Standards." EVIDENCE: B (design practice consensus)

34. **Apply the Principle of Least Surprise** — Design interfaces and visual systems that behave as users expect. WHY GOOD: Unexpected visual behavior (non-standard icons, unusual navigation, creative-but-confusing layouts) increases cognitive load and error rates. SOURCE: NN/G mental models research; UX design principles. EVIDENCE: A (usability research)

35. **Use Meaningful Animation Timing** — Apply easing curves (ease-out for entries, ease-in for exits) and appropriate durations (150-300ms for micro-interactions). WHY GOOD: Proper timing makes interfaces feel responsive and natural; too fast is jarring, too slow feels sluggish. Material Design's motion system is well-researched. SOURCE: Material Design motion guidelines; Disney's 12 Principles of Animation. EVIDENCE: B (motion design research)

36. **Design for Scannability** — Use headings, bullet points, bold key terms, and visual breaks to support scanning behavior. WHY GOOD: Users read at most 28% of web page words (NN/G); designing for scanning ensures key information is absorbed during typical reading behavior. SOURCE: NN/G F-pattern reading research; web writing guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking data)

37. **Maintain Consistent Iconography Style** — Use icons from a single set with consistent stroke weight, fill style, and visual language. WHY GOOD: Mixed icon styles (outlined + filled, different weights, different visual languages) create visual inconsistency that erodes perceived quality. SOURCE: Material Design iconography; design system guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (design consistency principle)

38. **Use Color to Indicate State Changes** — Apply systematic color changes for hover, active, disabled, error, and success states. WHY GOOD: Consistent state colors create learnable patterns; users quickly understand interactive affordances without reading instructions. SOURCE: Material Design state system; interaction design patterns. EVIDENCE: B (interaction design research)

39. **Apply the 8-Point Grid** — Use an 8px base unit for all sizing and spacing decisions. WHY GOOD: 8 divides evenly into common screen resolutions; creates pixel-perfect alignment on most displays; widely adopted standard (Material Design, IBM Carbon, Apple HIG). SOURCE: Material Design layout grid; Spec.fm 8-point grid system. EVIDENCE: B (industry standard adoption)

40. **Test With Real Users, Not Just Designers** — Validate design decisions with actual target audience members. WHY GOOD: Designers suffer from the curse of knowledge; 5 users reveal 85% of usability problems (NN/G); real users expose assumptions invisible to creators. SOURCE: NN/G usability testing methodology; Jakob Nielsen "Discount Usability" research. EVIDENCE: A (usability research)

41. **Use Semantic Color Tokens** — Define colors as tokens with semantic names (color-error, color-surface-primary) rather than literal names (red, light-gray). WHY GOOD: Semantic tokens enable theme switching (light/dark), maintain consistency across platforms, and make design intent explicit. SOURCE: Design token methodology; Style Dictionary; Material Design tokens. EVIDENCE: B (design system methodology)

42. **Design Error States First** — Plan what happens when things go wrong (empty states, error messages, loading failures) before ideal states. WHY GOOD: Users encounter error states frequently; well-designed error states maintain trust and provide recovery paths. Most designs only show happy paths. SOURCE: NN/G error message guidelines; defensive design principles. EVIDENCE: B (usability best practice)

43. **Provide Alt Text Strategy for Images** — Define when images need descriptive alt text, when they should be marked decorative, and how to write effective descriptions. WHY GOOD: Screen reader users need image descriptions for equivalent information access; WCAG 1.1.1 requires text alternatives for all non-text content. SOURCE: WCAG 1.1.1 Non-text Content; WebAIM alt text guide. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility requirement)

44. **Use Optical Alignment Over Mathematical Alignment** — Adjust visual alignment by eye rather than relying solely on bounding boxes. WHY GOOD: Geometric shapes have different optical weights; a circle and square of the same pixel size appear different sizes. Play buttons need slight right offset to appear centered. SOURCE: Typography optical alignment (Ellen Lupton "Thinking with Type"); Material Design optical illusions in design. EVIDENCE: B (visual perception research)

45. **Create Design System Documentation** — Document component usage, spacing rules, and design decisions with reasoning. WHY GOOD: Documentation ensures design consistency beyond the original designer; reduces onboarding time for new team members; creates a single source of truth. SOURCE: Atomic Design (Brad Frost); design system community best practices. EVIDENCE: B (organizational effectiveness)

46. **Respect System Preferences** — Honor user's OS-level preferences for reduced motion, dark mode, font size, and contrast. WHY GOOD: Users set these preferences for important personal reasons (vestibular disorders, light sensitivity, vision impairment); overriding them is both inaccessible and disrespectful. SOURCE: WCAG prefers-reduced-motion; CSS media features specification. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility best practice)

47. **Use Photography Direction that Supports Reading Flow** — Choose images where the subject's gaze or movement direction guides toward content. WHY GOOD: Eye-tracking studies show users follow the gaze direction of people in images; faces looking away from content pull attention off the page. SOURCE: Eye-tracking research (James Breeze gaze direction study); advertising design research. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking data)

48. **Design Loading States** — Create skeleton screens, progress indicators, or meaningful loading animations. WHY GOOD: Nielsen's research shows 10-second response time limit for keeping attention; perceived performance is as important as actual performance. Skeleton screens reduce perceived load time. SOURCE: NN/G response time research; Google skeleton screen studies. EVIDENCE: A (performance perception research)

49. **Use Print-Specific Preparation** — Set up print files with bleeds (3mm), safe zones, crop marks, and proper color profiles (CMYK + spot colors). WHY GOOD: Without proper print preparation, elements get cropped, colors shift, and reprints cost money. Professional print requires technical precision. SOURCE: Print production standards; prepress guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (technical requirement)

50. **Apply the Five-Second Test** — Show a design for 5 seconds, then ask what the viewer remembers. WHY GOOD: If the primary message isn't retained in 5 seconds, the visual hierarchy has failed. Simple, fast test that reveals whether design communicates its core purpose. SOURCE: UsabilityHub five-second test methodology; design validation techniques. EVIDENCE: B (usability testing technique)


## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS

1. **"Serif fonts are more readable than sans-serif"** — LLMs frequently state this as fact. Research shows no significant readability difference between serif and sans-serif for general audiences (Alex Poole meta-analysis). Context (screen vs print, size, spacing) matters more than serif vs sans-serif.

2. **"The golden ratio is the secret to great design"** — LLMs overstate the golden ratio's importance. While it produces pleasing proportions, it's one of many useful ratios (1.333, 1.5, 1.25). Most "golden ratio in famous logos" analyses are retrofitted approximations, not intentional design choices.

3. **"Blue is the safest color for all brands"** — LLMs recycle the claim that blue universally conveys trust. While blue is widely used in corporate identity, color effectiveness depends entirely on context, competition, and cultural factors. Purple, green, or orange may be more effective in specific niches.

4. **"White space is wasted space"** — LLMs sometimes suggest filling empty areas or justify dense layouts. Research consistently shows whitespace improves comprehension by up to 20% and signals quality/luxury.

5. **"Design trends should always be followed to look modern"** — LLMs conflate looking modern with good design. Timeless brands (Coca-Cola, Chanel, IBM) succeed through consistency, not trend adoption. Dieter Rams' 60-year-old principles remain more relevant than any current trend.

6. **"More design options = better for the client"** — LLMs suggest presenting many options. Research shows choice overload degrades decision quality (Iyengar & Lepper jam experiment). Present 2-3 options maximum with clear rationale.

7. **"Flat design is always better than skeuomorphism"** — LLMs present flat design as universally superior. Research shows appropriate depth cues (shadows, elevation) improve usability by clarifying interactive affordances. The best approach is contextual.

8. **"Complementary colors always work well together"** — LLMs state complementary colors as reliable combinations. In practice, pure complementary pairs at high saturation produce vibrating optical effects (especially red/green, purple/yellow) that strain the eyes.

9. **"Good design is purely about aesthetics"** — LLMs sometimes discuss design primarily in aesthetic terms. Design is communication; a visually striking piece that fails to convey its message is bad design regardless of beauty.

10. **"Color psychology is scientifically proven"** — LLMs present color-emotion associations as established science. Most color psychology studies have small samples, Western-only populations, and poor replication rates. Cultural context, personal experience, and specific shade matter more than universal color meanings.

# Writing & Communication: Evidence-Based Practices

## 50 BAD PRACTICES

1. **Keyword Stuffing** — Repeating target keywords unnaturally throughout text to manipulate search rankings. WHY BAD: Google considers it a spam policy violation; modern algorithms prioritize search intent and natural language. Results in penalty/delisting and produces unreadable text. INSTEAD: Write for humans first; use keywords naturally; leverage synonyms and semantic variations. SOURCE: Google Search spam policies; Semrush keyword stuffing guide. EVIDENCE: A (search engine policy + ranking data)

2. **Passive Voice Default** — Writing primarily in passive voice ("The report was written" instead of "I wrote the report"). WHY BAD: Hides the actor, adds words, creates ambiguity about responsibility. Politicians use it to evade accountability ("Mistakes were made"). Active voice is clearer and more concise. INSTEAD: Default to active voice; use passive only when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or when you deliberately need to emphasize the object. SOURCE: Strunk & White "Elements of Style"; Plain Language Action and Information Network (PLAIN). EVIDENCE: B (readability research + expert consensus)

3. **"In Order To" and Verbal Filler** — Using phrases like "in order to," "due to the fact that," "at this point in time," "it should be noted that." WHY BAD: Adds words without meaning; "in order to" = "to"; "due to the fact that" = "because"; "at this point in time" = "now." Inflates word count by 20-40%. INSTEAD: Delete the filler; use the shorter equivalent. Every word must earn its place (Ilyakhov). SOURCE: Ilyakhov "Pishi, Sokrashchay"; Strunk & White Rule 17: "Omit needless words." EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy consensus)

4. **Burying the Lede** — Placing the most important information deep in the text instead of leading with it. WHY BAD: Users read at most 28% of web page words (NN/G); if the key point is in paragraph 5, 80%+ of readers will never reach it. INSTEAD: Use the inverted pyramid: most important information first, supporting details second, background last. SOURCE: NN/G reading behavior research; journalism inverted pyramid principle. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking data)

5. **Wall of Text** — Publishing long paragraphs with no headings, bullet points, bold text, or visual breaks. WHY BAD: Triggers F-pattern scanning where users only read the first few words of each line (NN/G, 2006). Users abandon dense text blocks; comprehension drops significantly. INSTEAD: Use headings every 2-3 paragraphs; short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max); bullet points for lists; bold key phrases. SOURCE: NN/G F-pattern research; web content guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking studies)

6. **Corporate Buzzword Pollution** — Using jargon like "synergize," "leverage," "circle back," "move the needle," "low-hanging fruit" instead of plain language. WHY BAD: McKinsey research shows organizations with clear communication are 3.5x more likely to outperform peers. Jargon users score lower on analytical reasoning tests. 67% of B2B buyers prefer plain language (Forrester). INSTEAD: Replace every buzzword with a concrete, specific alternative. SOURCE: McKinsey communication research; Forrester B2B buyer preferences; Holmes Report ($62.4M annual cost of unclear communication). EVIDENCE: A (organizational performance data)

7. **Clickbait Headlines** — Using misleading, sensationalized headlines that don't deliver on their promise ("You Won't Believe..."). WHY BAD: 54.5% of readers say frequent clickbait would cause them to no longer trust a publication. Higher bounce rates, damaged reputation, reduced return visits. INSTEAD: Write honest, specific headlines that accurately preview the content; specificity generates more sustained engagement than sensation. SOURCE: Center for Media Engagement clickbait research; Springer Nature honest headlines study. EVIDENCE: A (survey data + engagement metrics)

8. **SEO-First Writing** — Writing primarily for search algorithms rather than human readers. WHY BAD: Produces robotic, repetitive, unnatural text that readers bounce from. Google's "helpful content update" specifically penalizes content made primarily for search engines. INSTEAD: Write for humans first, then optimize for search; Google explicitly calls this "people-first content." SOURCE: Google helpful content guidelines; Backlinko keyword stuffing analysis. EVIDENCE: A (Google algorithm updates + ranking data)

9. **Academic Style in Business Context** — Using formal academic conventions (third person, passive voice, hedging, citations-heavy prose) in business communication. WHY BAD: Business readers need information quickly; academic formality slows comprehension, signals pompousness, and obscures action items. Business writing shifted to plain English decades ago. INSTEAD: Use direct address (you/we), active voice, short sentences, and clear action items. SOURCE: Purdue OWL business writing; Penn LPS academic vs professional writing guide. EVIDENCE: B (communication effectiveness research)

10. **No Information Hierarchy in Text** — Treating all information as equally important; no headings, no bold, no structure. WHY BAD: Forces readers to read everything to find what they need; NN/G research shows users scan in predictable patterns and rely on structural cues to navigate content. INSTEAD: Structure with headings, subheadings, bold key terms, and topic sentences; front-load each paragraph. SOURCE: NN/G scanning patterns research; web writing guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking data)

11. **Nominalizations (Zombie Nouns)** — Converting verbs into nouns: "implementation" instead of "implement," "utilization" instead of "use." WHY BAD: Adds syllables and words without information; forces supporting verbs ("make a decision" instead of "decide"); makes prose sluggish and bureaucratic. INSTEAD: Use the verb form: "decide" not "make a decision"; "analyze" not "conduct an analysis." SOURCE: Helen Sword "Zombie Nouns" (NY Times); Plain Language guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

12. **Throat-Clearing Introductions** — Starting texts with "In today's fast-paced world..." or "Since the dawn of time..." or "As we all know..." WHY BAD: Wastes the most valuable real estate (the opening); readers decide to continue or abandon within the first 2-3 sentences. Generic openings signal generic content. INSTEAD: Start with the specific, concrete point; delete the first paragraph of your draft (common editing advice). SOURCE: Ilyakhov "Pishi, Sokrashchay"; journalism editing practices. EVIDENCE: B (editing pedagogy)

13. **Hedging Everything** — Using "perhaps," "it might be argued," "it could be said," "generally speaking" before every claim. WHY BAD: Excessive hedging makes the writer appear uncertain about their own content; dilutes authority and wastes words. Readers lose trust in hedged claims. INSTEAD: Make direct claims when you have evidence; use hedging only when genuine uncertainty exists. SOURCE: Academic writing research; Plain Language guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (communication effectiveness)

14. **Manipulation Disguised as Persuasion** — Using fear, guilt, artificial urgency, or hidden motives to influence readers. WHY BAD: Research shows manipulation conceals its true motives and is recognized as immoral; once detected, it destroys trust permanently. Manipulation works short-term but fails long-term. INSTEAD: Use transparent persuasion: present evidence, acknowledge counterarguments, respect reader autonomy. SOURCE: Journal of Student Research persuasion vs manipulation; communication ethics research. EVIDENCE: A (ethics research + trust studies)

15. **Overwriting / Purple Prose** — Using elaborate, flowery language when simple language would suffice. WHY BAD: Studies show texts written at a higher reading level are perceived as less credible, not more credible (Oppenheimer, Princeton, 2006: "Consequences of Erudite Vernacular"). INSTEAD: Use the simplest word that conveys the precise meaning. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." SOURCE: Oppenheimer (2006) "Consequences of Erudite Vernacular Utilized Irrespective of Necessity." EVIDENCE: A (experimental research)

16. **No Clear CTA (Call to Action)** — Writing persuasive or informational content without telling the reader what to do next. WHY BAD: If readers agree with your argument but have no next step, the communication has failed its purpose; energy dissipates without conversion. INSTEAD: End every piece with a specific, actionable next step; make it concrete and easy. SOURCE: Copywriting best practices; conversion optimization research. EVIDENCE: B (conversion rate data)

17. **Assuming Reader Knowledge** — Writing for experts when your audience is general, or vice versa. WHY BAD: Overestimating knowledge produces incomprehensible content; underestimating produces patronizing content. Both cause readers to disengage. INSTEAD: Define your audience explicitly before writing; when in doubt, explain technical terms; use progressive disclosure. SOURCE: Content strategy methodology; audience analysis frameworks. EVIDENCE: B (communication theory)

18. **Run-On Paragraphs** — Writing paragraphs that cover multiple ideas across 8+ sentences. WHY BAD: Paragraph breaks are visual breathing points; long paragraphs appear as text blocks that trigger scanning behavior (NN/G). Each paragraph should contain one idea. INSTEAD: One idea per paragraph; 3-4 sentences maximum for web; break at idea transitions. SOURCE: NN/G web writing guidelines; Strunk & White composition rules. EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

19. **Vague Quantifiers** — Writing "many," "several," "significant," "a lot of" instead of specific numbers. WHY BAD: "Many users reported issues" is unfalsifiable and uninformative; "47 out of 200 users (23.5%) reported issues" is actionable. Vague quantifiers signal lack of data or deliberate evasion. INSTEAD: Use specific numbers whenever possible; if exact numbers are unavailable, give ranges. SOURCE: Ilyakhov "Pishi, Sokrashchay" (specificity principle); data-driven writing research. EVIDENCE: B (information quality research)

20. **False Urgency** — Using "ACT NOW!" "Limited time!" "Don't miss out!" without genuine time constraints. WHY BAD: Creates short-term anxiety but long-term distrust; readers become desensitized to urgency signals. FTC considers false urgency deceptive. INSTEAD: If genuine urgency exists, state the specific deadline and reason; avoid urgency language for non-urgent content. SOURCE: FTC advertising guidelines; dark pattern research. EVIDENCE: A (regulatory guidance + consumer trust research)

21. **Buried Action Items** — Hiding requests, deadlines, and tasks within narrative paragraphs in emails. WHY BAD: Recipients skim emails; action items in paragraph form get missed, leading to missed deadlines and follow-up overhead. INSTEAD: Put action items at the top; use bold, bullet points, or a dedicated "Action Required" section with deadlines and owners. SOURCE: Business email best practices; Harvard Business Review communication research. EVIDENCE: B (workplace productivity research)

22. **Reply-All Culture** — Sending replies to all recipients when only the sender needs the response. WHY BAD: The average office worker receives 121 emails/day (Radicati); unnecessary reply-alls create noise, waste collective time, and train people to ignore emails. INSTEAD: Reply only to those who need the information; use BCC for broadcasts. SOURCE: Radicati email statistics; workplace productivity research. EVIDENCE: B (productivity data)

23. **Ambiguous Pronouns** — Using "it," "this," "they" without clear antecedents: "They discussed the project and it was approved, but they disagreed." WHY BAD: Forces reader to re-read and guess referents; in legal, technical, and business contexts, ambiguity causes errors. INSTEAD: Name the referent explicitly, even if it feels repetitive: "The board discussed the project. The CFO approved the budget, but the engineering leads disagreed on the timeline." SOURCE: Legal writing clarity guidelines; Strunk & White. EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

24. **Emotional Manipulation in Headlines** — Using anger, fear, or outrage as the primary engagement mechanism. WHY BAD: While emotional headlines get clicks (PMC study on clickbait engagement), they erode trust and create a toxic information ecosystem; corrections that remove emotional language actually improve credibility. INSTEAD: Use curiosity and specificity instead of emotion; inform rather than inflame. SOURCE: PMC clickbait engagement study; Center for Media Engagement research. EVIDENCE: A (experimental research)

25. **Writing Without a Clear Thesis** — Starting to write without a central argument or key message. WHY BAD: Produces rambling, unfocused text where the reader cannot identify the point; even the writer may not know their point until revision. INSTEAD: Write the key message in one sentence before starting; every paragraph should support this thesis. SOURCE: Writing pedagogy consensus; Ilyakhov goal-first writing. EVIDENCE: B (composition research)

26. **Excessive Acronyms/Initialisms** — Using more than 3-4 acronyms per page without definitions, or using them before establishing the full term. WHY BAD: Each undefined acronym is a comprehension barrier; readers lose time looking up meanings or, worse, misinterpret them. INSTEAD: Define every acronym on first use; avoid acronyms that appear fewer than 3 times in a document. SOURCE: Plain Language guidelines; technical writing best practices. EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

27. **The Curse of Knowledge** — Writing as if the reader has the same background and context as the writer. WHY BAD: Identified by Chip & Dan Heath ("Made to Stick"); once you know something, you cannot imagine not knowing it. Produces incomprehensible expert writing. INSTEAD: Have someone outside your domain review for clarity; explain concepts from the reader's starting point. SOURCE: Heath & Heath "Made to Stick"; cognitive bias research. EVIDENCE: A (cognitive psychology research)

28. **Weak Verbs + Adverbs Instead of Strong Verbs** — Writing "ran quickly" instead of "sprinted"; "said loudly" instead of "shouted." WHY BAD: Adverbs modify weak verbs to approximate the meaning a precise verb would convey directly. More words, less impact. Stephen King: "The road to hell is paved with adverbs." INSTEAD: Find the specific verb that contains the meaning: "walked slowly" → "ambled"; "looked carefully" → "examined." SOURCE: Stephen King "On Writing"; Strunk & White "Omit needless words." EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy consensus)

29. **Inconsistent Terminology** — Using different terms for the same concept within one document ("users" then "customers" then "clients" then "visitors"). WHY BAD: Each new term makes the reader wonder if you mean something different; creates unnecessary cognitive load. Especially dangerous in technical and legal writing. INSTEAD: Choose one term per concept and use it consistently; define synonyms explicitly if switching is necessary. SOURCE: Technical writing standards (Microsoft Style Guide, Google Developer Docs Style Guide). EVIDENCE: B (technical writing best practice)

30. **Burying the Ask in Emails** — Placing a request in the middle or end of a long email. WHY BAD: Email recipients scan; requests buried in paragraph 3 of 5 are frequently missed. Response rates drop dramatically when the ask is not immediately visible. INSTEAD: Put the request in the first 2 sentences or in the subject line; format as a clear question or action item. SOURCE: Harvard Business Review email research; business communication studies. EVIDENCE: B (workplace communication data)

31. **Double Negatives for False Sophistication** — Writing "not uncommon" instead of "common"; "not insignificant" instead of "significant." WHY BAD: Forces the reader to perform mental negation twice, slowing comprehension. Often used to hedge without appearing to hedge. "Not uncommon" is actually weaker than "common." INSTEAD: Use the positive form; if you mean something in between, say "occasional" or "fairly common." SOURCE: Plain Language guidelines; Orwell "Politics and the English Language." EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

32. **Excessive Qualifiers** — Stacking qualifiers: "very unique," "extremely essential," "somewhat critical." WHY BAD: "Unique" and "essential" are absolutes that cannot be qualified; stacking qualifiers weakens rather than strengthens. Mark Twain: "Substitute 'damn' every time you're inclined to write 'very'; your editor will delete it and the writing will be just as it should be." INSTEAD: Remove qualifiers or replace with specific evidence; "very large" → "2,400 square feet." SOURCE: Twain writing advice; Ilyakhov specificity principle. EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy)

33. **Walls of Bullet Points** — Converting entire documents into bullet lists with no connecting prose. WHY BAD: Bullet points without context or hierarchy lose the logical flow between ideas; readers get a list of facts without understanding relationships or causation. INSTEAD: Use bullets for 3-7 parallel items; connect them with topic sentences and concluding prose. SOURCE: Garr Reynolds "Presentation Zen"; document design research. EVIDENCE: B (information design research)

34. **Meeting Notes Without Decisions/Actions** — Recording what was discussed without capturing what was decided and who owns the next steps. WHY BAD: Discussion notes become trivia after 24 hours; the only valuable meeting output is decisions made + action items assigned + deadlines set. INSTEAD: Structure notes as: Decisions Made → Action Items (owner + deadline) → Key Discussion Points. SOURCE: Meeting productivity research; organizational effectiveness studies. EVIDENCE: B (workplace productivity research)

35. **Weasel Words** — Using "some experts say," "studies show," "it is widely believed" without attribution. WHY BAD: Unfalsifiable and unverifiable; could mean one blog post or 100 peer-reviewed papers. Signal either laziness or deliberate obfuscation. INSTEAD: Name the source: "A 2024 Stanford study of 3,000 participants found..." or remove the claim. SOURCE: Wikipedia weasel words policy; journalistic attribution standards. EVIDENCE: B (information quality)

36. **Gendered/Exclusionary Language** — Using "he" as default pronoun, "manpower," "mankind," or culture-specific idioms in global communication. WHY BAD: Alienates portions of the audience; signals carelessness about inclusivity; creates legal risk in regulated contexts. Research shows gendered language affects perception of who "belongs." INSTEAD: Use "they/them" singular, "workforce," "humanity"; test idioms for cultural neutrality. SOURCE: APA Publication Manual 7th edition; inclusive language research. EVIDENCE: A (linguistic research + institutional standards)

37. **Information Dumping** — Presenting all available information regardless of audience relevance. WHY BAD: Information overload degrades decision quality (Eppler & Mengis, 2004); readers cannot distinguish important from trivial when everything is included. INSTEAD: Curate for your audience; include only what serves their goals; link to detailed references for those who want more. SOURCE: Eppler & Mengis "The Concept of Information Overload" (2004); information architecture. EVIDENCE: A (information overload research)

38. **Misusing "Data" as Singular** — While this has become common, mixing "data is" and "data are" within one document signals inconsistency. WHY BAD: Inconsistency within a single document suggests lack of editorial oversight; choose one convention and apply it throughout. INSTEAD: Pick a convention (both are now accepted) and apply consistently; formal scientific writing still prefers "data are." SOURCE: APA Style; Chicago Manual of Style. EVIDENCE: C (style guide convention)

39. **Overreliance on Exclamation Points** — Using exclamation marks liberally in professional communication ("Great idea! Let's do this! Thanks!"). WHY BAD: F. Scott Fitzgerald: "An exclamation mark is like laughing at your own joke." Overuse dilutes emphasis and can appear unprofessional or manic. INSTEAD: Reserve exclamation marks for genuine expressions of enthusiasm; use one per email maximum in professional contexts. SOURCE: Professional writing conventions; communication tone research. EVIDENCE: C (professional convention)

40. **Writing Long When Short Works** — Producing a 2,000-word document when 500 words would convey the same information. WHY BAD: Blaise Pascal: "I would have written a shorter letter, but I did not have the time." Length signals lack of editing. Studies show longer content is not read more thoroughly—it's abandoned more often. INSTEAD: Edit ruthlessly; aim to cut 30-50% in revision. Ilyakhov's core principle: write, then cut. SOURCE: Ilyakhov "Pishi, Sokrashchay"; PLAIN guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (readability research + expert consensus)

41. **Sarcasm in Written Communication** — Using sarcastic tone in emails, documentation, or business messages. WHY BAD: Research shows sarcasm is misinterpreted 50%+ of the time in written communication without vocal/facial cues; cultural differences amplify misinterpretation. INSTEAD: Be direct; if humor is appropriate, use clear wit rather than sarcasm; save sarcasm for in-person. SOURCE: Communication research on tone in text; cross-cultural communication studies. EVIDENCE: B (communication research)

42. **Starting Sentences with "There is/are"** — Using expletive constructions as default sentence openers ("There are many reasons why..."). WHY BAD: Delays the subject and verb; weakens the sentence by burying the actor. "There are many users who complain" vs "Many users complain." INSTEAD: Restructure to lead with the subject: "Three factors drive this trend" not "There are three factors that drive this trend." SOURCE: Strunk & White composition rules; grammar efficiency research. EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy)

43. **Copying Competitors' Tone of Voice** — Mimicking another brand's communication style without adaptation. WHY BAD: Every brand operates in a different context; copying Wendy's sassy Twitter tone as a hospital or bank erodes trust. Voice must match audience expectations and brand values. INSTEAD: Define your own voice based on audience, values, and context; document it in a style guide. SOURCE: Brand voice strategy; Content Design London. EVIDENCE: B (brand communication research)

44. **No Proofreading** — Publishing content without review for spelling, grammar, and factual errors. WHY BAD: A single typo can reduce perceived credibility by 70%+ in certain contexts (Stanford Web Credibility Project). Errors signal carelessness and undermine authority. INSTEAD: Always proofread; use tools (Grammarly, LanguageTool) as a first pass; have a human review for final approval. SOURCE: Stanford Web Credibility Research; editing best practices. EVIDENCE: A (credibility research)

45. **Using Jargon as a Status Signal** — Deliberately using complex terminology to appear sophisticated or gatekeep knowledge. WHY BAD: Research participants who used jargon-heavy communication scored lower on analytical reasoning tests; 67% of B2B buyers prefer plain language. Jargon excludes rather than impresses. INSTEAD: Explain complex ideas simply; Einstein: "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough." SOURCE: Forrester B2B buyer research; Oppenheimer erudite vernacular study. EVIDENCE: A (experimental research)

46. **Infinite Scroll Content** — Writing endlessly long web pages without clear sections, navigation, or table of contents. WHY BAD: Users cannot estimate content length, find specific sections, or bookmark progress; abandonment rates increase significantly for unstructured long content. INSTEAD: Add table of contents, section anchors, progress indicators, and clear headings. SOURCE: NN/G long page navigation research; web usability guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (usability research)

47. **Ignoring Subject Lines** — Treating email subject lines as an afterthought. WHY BAD: 47% of email recipients open email based on the subject line alone (OptinMonster); a vague subject like "Quick question" or "Follow up" provides no basis for prioritization. INSTEAD: Write specific, actionable subject lines: "Decision needed: Q2 budget by Friday" not "Quick question." SOURCE: OptinMonster email statistics; email marketing research. EVIDENCE: A (open rate data)

48. **Writing Entire Emails in the Subject Line** — Cramming the entire message into the subject line to avoid writing an email body. WHY BAD: Subject lines are truncated (30-50 chars on mobile); critical information gets cut off. Not all email clients display long subjects. INSTEAD: Use the subject for summary, body for detail; if it fits in one sentence, use the subject + "EOM" (End of Message). SOURCE: Email best practices; mobile email design guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (email client technical limitations)

49. **Mixing Metaphors** — Combining incompatible metaphors: "We need to grab the bull by the horns and drive this ship into new waters." WHY BAD: Creates absurd mental images that distract from the message; signals muddled thinking. Orwell identified this as a sign of lazy writing. INSTEAD: Use one clear metaphor or none; test metaphors by visualizing them literally. SOURCE: Orwell "Politics and the English Language"; writing clarity guidelines. EVIDENCE: C (writing quality standard)

50. **Not Knowing When to Stop** — Continuing to add content after the point has been made, repeating conclusions, and padding for length. WHY BAD: Every sentence after the point has been made dilutes the impact; readers feel their time is disrespected. "A good speech should be like a woman's skirt: long enough to cover the subject and short enough to create interest" (Churchill, attributed). INSTEAD: Stop when the point is made; read your ending and cut backwards until you hit the last essential sentence. SOURCE: Ilyakhov editing principles; journalism training. EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy)


## 50 BEST PRACTICES

1. **Lead with the Key Message** — Put the most important information in the first sentence or paragraph. WHY GOOD: Users read at most 28% of web page words (NN/G); the inverted pyramid ensures the key message reaches even the most casual scanners. SOURCE: NN/G reading behavior research; inverted pyramid journalism standard. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking data)

2. **Write, Then Cut 30-50%** — Draft freely, then edit ruthlessly by cutting nearly half the words. WHY GOOD: Ilyakhov's core principle: first drafts contain filler, repetition, and unnecessary qualifiers. Cutting forces precision and improves information density. SOURCE: Ilyakhov "Pishi, Sokrashchay"; editing methodology. EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy consensus)

3. **One Idea Per Paragraph** — Each paragraph should contain exactly one main idea with its supporting evidence. WHY GOOD: Makes content scannable; readers can skip paragraphs about topics they already know and focus on new information. Breaks create natural reading rest points. SOURCE: Strunk & White composition principles; web writing guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

4. **Use Active Voice as Default** — Write "The team completed the project" not "The project was completed by the team." WHY GOOD: Active voice is shorter (fewer words), clearer (who did what), and more engaging. Studies show active voice improves comprehension speed by 20-25%. SOURCE: Strunk & White; Plain Language Action Network (PLAIN). EVIDENCE: A (readability studies)

5. **Structure for Scanning** — Use headings, subheadings, bullet points, bold key terms, and short paragraphs. WHY GOOD: Accommodates the F-pattern scanning behavior NN/G documented; users scan headings and bold text to decide what to read in detail. Good formatting can prevent F-pattern. SOURCE: NN/G F-pattern and scanning patterns research. EVIDENCE: A (eye-tracking data)

6. **Use Specific Numbers Over Vague Quantifiers** — Write "47 customers (23%)" not "many customers." WHY GOOD: Specificity builds credibility, enables verification, and provides actionable data. "Revenue grew 23% in Q2" vs "revenue grew significantly." SOURCE: Ilyakhov specificity principle; data-driven writing research. EVIDENCE: B (information quality research)

7. **Front-Load Sentences and Paragraphs** — Put the key information at the beginning of each sentence and paragraph. WHY GOOD: Readers pay most attention to the first few words (serial position effect); front-loading ensures the most important content is in the highest-attention zone. SOURCE: Cognitive psychology serial position effect; web writing best practices. EVIDENCE: A (cognitive psychology research)

8. **Write at an 8th-Grade Reading Level for General Audiences** — Aim for Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level 7-9 for most business and public communication. WHY GOOD: The average American reads at 7th-8th grade level; US federal plain language guidelines target this level. Even experts prefer simpler text for speed. SOURCE: PLAIN.gov federal guidelines; Flesch readability research. EVIDENCE: A (literacy statistics + government standard)

9. **Define Jargon on First Use** — When technical terms are necessary, define them immediately in plain language. WHY GOOD: Respects experts (who can skip the definition) and non-experts (who need it); prevents misinterpretation of domain-specific terms. SOURCE: Technical writing standards; Plain Language guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (technical communication best practice)

10. **Make Subject Lines Specific and Actionable** — Write "Decision needed: Q2 budget by Friday 3/21" not "Quick question." WHY GOOD: 47% of recipients decide to open emails based on subject line alone; specific subjects enable prioritization and searchability. SOURCE: OptinMonster email statistics; business email research. EVIDENCE: A (open rate data)

11. **Use the Inverted Pyramid** — Structure as: conclusion/key message → supporting evidence → background/context. WHY GOOD: Reduces cognitive load through hierarchical information flow; supports efficient scanning; readers can stop at any point and still have the most important information. SOURCE: Purdue OWL inverted pyramid; NN/G content structure research. EVIDENCE: A (journalism practice + reading behavior research)

12. **Apply Ilyakhov's "Informational Style"** — Write with facts, specifics, and concrete examples instead of generalities, adjectives, and emotional appeals. WHY GOOD: Information-dense text respects the reader's time and builds trust through verifiability; "strong text" derives power from evidence, not decoration. SOURCE: Ilyakhov "Pishi, Sokrashchay"; informational style methodology. EVIDENCE: B (Russian writing pedagogy + practical application)

13. **Use Parallel Structure** — Express similar ideas in the same grammatical form: "We plan to hire staff, upgrade systems, and launch the product." WHY GOOD: Parallel structure reduces parsing effort, creates rhythm, and makes lists scannable. Non-parallel structures force readers to re-parse each item. SOURCE: Strunk & White Rule 19; grammar and readability research. EVIDENCE: B (linguistic research)

14. **Test Headlines and Openings with the "So What?" Filter** — After writing a headline or opening, ask "so what?" and ensure the answer is there. WHY GOOD: Forces writers to articulate relevance to the reader rather than the writer; kills self-indulgent openings. If you can't answer "so what," the reader won't either. SOURCE: Journalism editing methodology; content strategy best practices. EVIDENCE: B (editing methodology)

15. **Chunk Information into 3-7 Items** — Group related information into sets of 3-7 items maximum. WHY GOOD: Miller's Law (1956): working memory holds 7±2 items. Lists longer than 7 should be subcategorized. SOURCE: George Miller "The Magical Number Seven" (1956); cognitive psychology. EVIDENCE: A (seminal cognitive psychology research)

16. **Use Topic Sentences** — Begin each paragraph with a sentence that summarizes its content. WHY GOOD: Enables effective scanning; readers can get the gist by reading only the first sentence of each paragraph. Creates a natural outline within the text. SOURCE: Composition pedagogy; web writing guidelines. EVIDENCE: B (readability research)

17. **Show, Don't Tell (with Evidence)** — Instead of "our product is fast," show "loads in 0.3 seconds, 4x faster than the industry average." WHY GOOD: Concrete evidence is more credible and memorable than abstract claims; specifics enable verification and comparison. SOURCE: Copywriting best practices; Ilyakhov evidence principle. EVIDENCE: B (persuasion research)

18. **Respect the Reader's Time** — Treat every word as a cost to the reader; delete anything that doesn't add value. WHY GOOD: The average knowledge worker processes 174 emails/day and reads ~5,000 words of business content. Every unnecessary word is stolen attention. SOURCE: Radicati email statistics; information overload research (Eppler & Mengis). EVIDENCE: B (workplace productivity data)

19. **Use Transition Words Purposefully** — Connect ideas with appropriate transitions (however, therefore, consequently, meanwhile) rather than no transitions or excessive transitions. WHY GOOD: Transitions signal logical relationships between ideas; they help readers predict what comes next and understand argument flow. But only when they add information. SOURCE: Composition research; cohesion in text studies. EVIDENCE: B (text coherence research)

20. **Write Clear Action Items** — Format as: "ACTION: [Person] to [verb] [object] by [date]." WHY GOOD: Removes ambiguity; makes ownership and deadline explicit; enables tracking. Poorly formatted action items are missed 40%+ of the time. SOURCE: Project management communication standards; meeting effectiveness research. EVIDENCE: B (workplace productivity research)

21. **Read Your Writing Aloud** — Test all text by reading it aloud before publishing. WHY GOOD: Catches awkward phrasing, run-on sentences, and unnatural word choice that the eye skips over. If you stumble while speaking it, readers will stumble while reading it. SOURCE: Writing process best practices; copywriting methodology. EVIDENCE: C (practical technique, widely validated)

22. **Use the "Grandmother Test"** — Could someone's grandmother (outside your field) understand your main point? WHY GOOD: Forces you to strip jargon and assumed knowledge; if a non-expert can follow your argument, an expert certainly can. SOURCE: Science communication best practices; Plain Language methodology. EVIDENCE: C (practical heuristic)

23. **Create Scannable Lists for 3+ Items** — Convert inline lists of 3+ items into formatted bullet points. WHY GOOD: Formatted lists are scanned 40% faster than inline lists (NN/G); visual structure helps readers process parallel items. SOURCE: NN/G list formatting research; web content guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (scanning speed research)

24. **Anchor Abstract Ideas to Concrete Examples** — Follow every abstract statement with a specific, concrete illustration. WHY GOOD: Dual coding theory (Paivio, 1971): concrete examples create both verbal and visual memory traces, improving retention by up to 2x. SOURCE: Paivio dual coding theory; educational psychology research. EVIDENCE: A (cognitive psychology research)

25. **Choose Precise Verbs Over Verb-Adverb Combinations** — Write "sprinted" not "ran quickly"; "examined" not "looked carefully." WHY GOOD: Precise verbs are shorter, more vivid, and more memorable. They create stronger mental images with fewer words. SOURCE: Stephen King "On Writing"; Strunk & White. EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy consensus)

26. **Structure Emails as: Context → Ask → Details** — Open with one line of context, immediately state the request/decision needed, then provide supporting details. WHY GOOD: Respects scan-reading behavior; the ask is visible without scrolling; details are available for those who need them. SOURCE: Business email best practices; Harvard Business Review. EVIDENCE: B (workplace communication research)

27. **Use Progressive Disclosure in Long Documents** — Provide executive summary → key findings → detailed analysis → appendices. WHY GOOD: Different readers need different levels of detail; executives read the summary, analysts read the details, everyone gets what they need. SOURCE: Document design research; technical writing standards. EVIDENCE: B (document design research)

28. **Avoid Orphaned Acronyms** — Every acronym must be defined on first use; acronyms appearing fewer than 3 times should be written out. WHY GOOD: Undefined acronyms are impenetrable to new readers and create exclusionary communication; defining them costs little and includes everyone. SOURCE: Plain Language guidelines; technical style guides (APA, IEEE). EVIDENCE: B (technical writing standard)

29. **Use "You" and "We" Over Third Person** — Address the reader directly: "You can submit the form by..." not "Users should submit the form by..." WHY GOOD: Direct address is more engaging and clear; research shows users complete tasks faster with second-person instructions. SOURCE: Plain Language guidelines; instructional design research. EVIDENCE: A (task completion studies)

30. **Apply the BLUF Principle (Bottom Line Up Front)** — State the conclusion, recommendation, or key finding before the supporting argument. WHY GOOD: US Military standard for briefings; proven effective in high-stakes, time-constrained communication. Ensures decision-makers get the essential information immediately. SOURCE: US Military communication standard; business communication adaptation. EVIDENCE: B (military communication methodology)

31. **Write Headlines That Work Standalone** — Headlines should convey meaning without requiring the body text. WHY GOOD: Headlines appear in search results, social shares, RSS feeds, and email previews without the body; a headline that requires context fails in 80%+ of its appearances. SOURCE: NN/G headline guidelines; journalism standards. EVIDENCE: B (content distribution research)

32. **Use Short Sentences for Key Points** — Reserve sentences under 15 words for your most important claims. WHY GOOD: Short sentences command attention by breaking rhythm; they create emphasis through brevity. Sentence length variation creates engaging rhythm. SOURCE: Roy Peter Clark "Writing Tools"; prose rhythm research. EVIDENCE: B (writing pedagogy)

33. **Include a TL;DR for Long Content** — Provide a 2-3 sentence summary at the top of any content over 500 words. WHY GOOD: Respects time-constrained readers; allows them to decide whether the full content is worth their time; improves satisfaction even if they don't read further. SOURCE: Content strategy best practices; web reading behavior research. EVIDENCE: B (web usage patterns)

34. **Kill Your Darlings** — Be willing to cut your best-written sentences if they don't serve the piece's purpose. WHY GOOD: Attributed to various writers (Faulkner, King, Quiller-Couch); brilliant sentences that don't serve the argument distract from the message. Every element must justify its presence. SOURCE: "On the Art of Writing" (Quiller-Couch, 1916); editing methodology. EVIDENCE: C (literary tradition)

35. **Use Consistent Terminology** — Choose one term per concept and stick with it throughout the document. WHY GOOD: Eliminates the cognitive overhead of wondering whether "users," "customers," and "clients" mean the same thing; critical for technical, legal, and instructional writing. SOURCE: Microsoft Style Guide; Google Developer Docs Style Guide. EVIDENCE: B (technical writing standard)

36. **Write Descriptive Link Text** — Write "Read the 2024 performance report" not "Click here" or "Read more." WHY GOOD: Screen readers can navigate by links; "click here" is meaningless out of context. Descriptive links also improve SEO and help all users predict the destination. SOURCE: WCAG 2.4.4 Link Purpose; WebAIM link text guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility standard)

37. **Apply Orwell's Six Rules** — (1) Never use a metaphor you've seen in print. (2) Never use a long word where a short one will do. (3) Cut any word possible. (4) Never use passive when active works. (5) Never use jargon if there's an everyday equivalent. (6) Break any of these rules rather than say something outright barbarous. WHY GOOD: Orwell's rules from "Politics and the English Language" (1946) remain the most concise guide to clear writing; rule 6 prevents dogmatic application. SOURCE: George Orwell "Politics and the English Language" (1946). EVIDENCE: B (literary standard, 80 years of influence)

38. **Use the ICE Framework for Feedback** — Structure feedback as: Identify (what you see) → Concern (why it's a problem) → Effect (what impact it has). WHY GOOD: Separates observation from interpretation; reduces defensiveness; makes feedback actionable rather than emotional. SOURCE: Communication feedback frameworks; management communication research. EVIDENCE: B (organizational communication research)

39. **Match Tone to Context** — Calibrate formality to the audience and situation: casual for Slack, professional for client emails, formal for legal documents. WHY GOOD: Tone mismatch erodes trust; too casual in formal contexts signals disrespect, too formal in casual contexts signals distance. Research shows tone-context match improves perceived competence. SOURCE: Business communication research; sociolinguistic register theory. EVIDENCE: B (communication research)

40. **Use White Space in Documents** — Add spacing between sections, use margins, and avoid cramming content edge-to-edge. WHY GOOD: Document white space improves reading comprehension by up to 20% (same principles as design whitespace); signals organization and intentionality. SOURCE: Wichita State University whitespace research; document design principles. EVIDENCE: A (controlled studies)

41. **Write for Internationalization** — Avoid idioms, humor, cultural references, and puns in content that may be translated. WHY GOOD: Idioms don't translate ("ball is in your court" is meaningless in many languages); cultural references create exclusion. Plain language translates better and costs less. SOURCE: Localization best practices; international communication standards. EVIDENCE: B (translation industry research)

42. **Use Tables for Comparison Data** — When comparing 3+ items across 3+ attributes, use a table instead of prose. WHY GOOD: Tables enable random-access lookup and pattern recognition that prose cannot; comparison prose forces sequential reading of parallel information. SOURCE: Information design principles; Tufte data presentation. EVIDENCE: B (information design research)

43. **End with a Clear Next Step** — Every document, email, or message should end with what happens next or what the reader should do. WHY GOOD: Closure reduces uncertainty; clear next steps convert understanding into action. Without them, even persuasive writing fails to produce results. SOURCE: Copywriting best practices; conversion optimization. EVIDENCE: B (conversion research)

44. **Use Sentence Case for Headings (Web)** — Write "How to write better emails" not "How To Write Better Emails" for web content headings. WHY GOOD: Sentence case is faster to read (NN/G); title case is a print convention that slows web scanning. Also easier to apply consistently. SOURCE: NN/G heading capitalization research; web style guides. EVIDENCE: B (usability research)

45. **Apply the 1-2-3 Rule for Presentations** — 1 idea per slide, 2 minutes max per slide, 3 bullet points maximum. WHY GOOD: Prevents cognitive overload; keeps audience engagement; forces speaker to distill ideas. Supported by multimedia learning research (Mayer). SOURCE: Richard Mayer multimedia learning principles; presentation design research. EVIDENCE: A (educational psychology research)

46. **Version and Date All Documents** — Include version number and date on all non-trivial documents. WHY GOOD: Prevents confusion about which version is current; enables change tracking; essential in collaborative and regulatory environments. SOURCE: Document management standards; configuration management best practices. EVIDENCE: B (professional standard)

47. **Write Inclusive Alt Text for Communication Visuals** — When including images in communication, provide text descriptions for all readers. WHY GOOD: Not just accessibility compliance—it forces you to articulate what the visual communicates, which often reveals that the visual isn't communicating anything essential. SOURCE: WCAG 1.1.1; inclusive communication guidelines. EVIDENCE: A (accessibility requirement)

48. **Use the Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)** — Structure arguments top-down: answer first, then group supporting arguments logically, then provide evidence for each group. WHY GOOD: McKinsey-standard communication structure; enables any audience to stop at their needed depth. Proven in consulting, legal, and executive communication. SOURCE: Barbara Minto "The Pyramid Principle"; McKinsey communication methodology. EVIDENCE: B (professional practice standard)

49. **Edit in Multiple Passes** — Separate editing into passes: structure → clarity → concision → grammar → proofreading. WHY GOOD: Each pass has a different focus; trying to fix everything at once means nothing gets fully fixed. Professional editors always use multiple passes. SOURCE: Professional editing methodology; writing process research. EVIDENCE: B (editing practice consensus)

50. **Know When to Choose a Meeting Over Writing** — Recognize when a conversation is more efficient than a document: ambiguous topics, emotional subjects, rapid back-and-forth needed. WHY GOOD: Not every communication should be written; some topics require the bandwidth of real-time conversation (tone, facial expressions, rapid clarification). Writing the wrong things wastes everyone's time. SOURCE: Communication channel selection research; media richness theory (Daft & Lengel). EVIDENCE: A (communication theory research)


## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS

1. **"Delve into" is good professional writing** — LLMs massively overuse "delve," "explore," "unpack," "navigate," and similar verbs. Pre-2023 business writing rarely used these words; their frequency in LLM output has made them reliable AI-detection markers. Plain alternatives: "examine," "discuss," "explain."

2. **The Rule of Three is always appropriate** — LLMs default to triple structures ("clear, concise, and compelling") as a rhetorical crutch. While the rule of three has rhetorical power when used selectively, LLMs apply it so consistently that it becomes a rhythmic monotone. Vary your structures.

3. **Every paragraph needs a transition word** — LLMs insert "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," and "In addition" at the start of nearly every paragraph. Good writing uses transitions sparingly—when logical relationships need explicit signaling, not as paragraph-opening decoration.

4. **Hedging makes writing more accurate** — LLMs default to "It's important to note that," "It could be argued," and "It's worth mentioning." This hedging makes writing appear uncertain rather than balanced. State facts directly; hedge only when genuine uncertainty exists.

5. **Longer responses are more helpful** — LLMs tend to produce comprehensive responses when concise ones would better serve the reader. A 200-word answer that addresses the question is better than a 2,000-word exploration of tangentially related topics.

6. **Formal register is always more professional** — LLMs tend toward formality, producing stiff, distant prose when a conversational tone would be more effective. Modern business writing favors warm directness over cold formality.

7. **Summary paragraphs must restate everything** — LLMs typically end with "In summary..." followed by a near-verbatim repetition of all main points. Effective conclusions add insight, call to action, or forward-looking perspective—not repetition.

8. **All lists should be comprehensive** — LLMs try to include every possible item in a list rather than curating the most important ones. A focused list of 5 essential items is more useful than an exhaustive list of 20.

9. **Passive constructions sound more authoritative** — LLMs sometimes use passive voice ("It should be noted," "It has been observed") believing it sounds scholarly. Research shows the opposite: active voice conveys more authority and confidence.

10. **Acknowledging limitations is always necessary** — LLMs often include disclaimers ("While this isn't exhaustive," "This is a general overview") even when the content is thorough. Unnecessary disclaimers undermine the reader's confidence in the content. Include limitations only when they materially affect the reader's use of the information.
