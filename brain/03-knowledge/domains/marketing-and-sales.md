# Marketing: Evidence-Based Practices

> Anti-Stupidity Shield for AI Agents — Marketing Domain
> Sources: Byron Sharp (Ehrenberg-Bass Institute), Les Binet & Peter Field (IPA Databank), Neil Rackham (SPIN Selling), Ries & Trout (Positioning), Dixon & Adamson (Challenger Sale), HubSpot Research, Google Search Quality, FTC Reports, Cognism/Gong sales research.

## 50 BAD PRACTICES

1. **Vanity Metric Obsession** — Optimizing for impressions, followers, and likes instead of revenue-correlated metrics. WHY BAD: Facebook organic reach is 1.37% (2024); impressions have near-zero correlation with conversions. Teams celebrate growing follower counts while revenue stagnates. INSTEAD: Track CAC, CLV, conversion rate, revenue per channel, and ROAS. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass Institute; MarTech research. EVIDENCE: A

2. **"Content Is King" Without Distribution** — Creating content endlessly with zero distribution strategy. WHY BAD: 4.6 billion pieces of content published daily; without distribution, even great content gets zero visibility. Most marketers spend 100% on creation, 0% on distribution. INSTEAD: Follow the 50/50 rule — spend equal effort on creation and distribution. Ross Simmonds: "D.R.E.A.M. — Distribution Rules Everything Around Me." SOURCE: Neil Patel research; Simmonds. EVIDENCE: A

3. **SEO Gaming Instead of Genuine Content** — Keyword stuffing, link schemes, and AI-generated mass content for rankings. WHY BAD: Google's March 2024 core update demoted 45% of low-value sites. Helpful Content Update (integrated 2024) targets content written to rank rather than to help. Penalties cause 5-18% average traffic drops. INSTEAD: Create genuinely helpful, original content; use AI as a drafting tool with human refinement and fact-checking. SOURCE: Google Search Quality Guidelines; Semrush data. EVIDENCE: A

4. **Ignoring Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)** — Focusing only on acquisition cost without measuring long-term customer value. WHY BAD: A 5% increase in retention increases profits by 25-95% (Bain & Company). CLV is the most important metric companies ignore — without it, you overspend acquiring low-value customers. INSTEAD: Calculate CLV by segment; allocate budget proportional to segment lifetime value; optimize retention alongside acquisition. SOURCE: Wharton research; Bain & Company. EVIDENCE: A

5. **Short-Term Performance Over Brand Building** — Allocating 90%+ of budget to performance/activation, neglecting brand. WHY BAD: Binet & Field's IPA Databank analysis (30+ years of data) shows brand-building campaigns decay slowly and drive long-term profitability, while activation effects decay within days. Over-indexing on activation harvests demand but doesn't create it. INSTEAD: Follow the 60/40 rule — 60% brand-building, 40% activation (adjust by category). SOURCE: Les Binet & Peter Field, "The Long and the Short of It" (IPA). EVIDENCE: A

6. **Marketing Without Positioning** — Launching products without a clear position in the customer's mind. WHY BAD: In an oversaturated market, an unpositioned brand is invisible. Ries & Trout showed the first brand in a category gets 2x the market share of #2. Without positioning, you compete on price alone. INSTEAD: Define a single, clear position that manipulates what's already in the prospect's mind. Name, category, and differentiator must be crystal clear. SOURCE: Ries & Trout, "Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind." EVIDENCE: B

7. **Funnel Obsession (Ignoring the Flywheel)** — Treating customers as an output of a linear funnel, not as growth drivers. WHY BAD: The funnel views customers as an afterthought. Post-purchase engagement and retention are ignored. Acquiring a new customer costs 5x more than retaining an existing one. INSTEAD: Adopt the flywheel model — marketing, sales, and service all attract, engage, and delight. Existing customers become your primary growth engine. SOURCE: HubSpot flywheel research. EVIDENCE: B

8. **A/B Testing Theater** — Running tests without statistical significance, stopping early, or testing too many variables. WHY BAD: Stopping before 95% confidence yields false positives. Running 10 tests at 95% confidence = ~40% chance of at least one false positive. External factors (seasonality, promotions) confound results. INSTEAD: Pre-calculate required sample size; run to completion; test one variable at a time; account for multiple comparisons. SOURCE: CXL; Adobe Target; Analytics Toolkit. EVIDENCE: A

9. **Spam Outreach Disguised as Marketing** — Mass unsolicited emails with no segmentation or personalization. WHY BAD: Average cold email reply rate dropped to 5.1% in 2024 (from 7% in 2023). 160 billion spam emails sent daily. Gmail 2024 spam filtering rules further reduce deliverability. 95% of cold emails fail to get any reply. INSTEAD: Segment ruthlessly; personalize based on research; warm up leads through content and social proof first. SOURCE: Martal Group B2B email statistics; Mailchimp segmentation data. EVIDENCE: A

10. **"Be Everywhere" Channel Strategy** — Trying to maintain presence across every platform simultaneously. WHY BAD: Spreading across 4-5 channels simultaneously produces mediocre returns everywhere. You can't produce enough quality content, track performance accurately, or allocate budget efficiently. Diminishing returns set in faster. INSTEAD: Master one channel first, then expand. Focus on where your customers actually are, not where you think you should be. SOURCE: Northbeam; Funnel.io diminishing returns research. EVIDENCE: B

11. **Influencer Marketing Without ROI Measurement** — Paying influencers based on follower count with no attribution framework. WHY BAD: Measurement tools haven't caught up to the spend. Nano-influencers deliver 3x+ higher return on spend than macro-influencers, yet brands overpay for follower counts. Without measurement, you can't optimize. INSTEAD: Use trackable links, unique codes, and lift studies. Prioritize nano/micro-influencers for cost-effectiveness. Measure conversions, not impressions. SOURCE: IPA Influencer Database (220 campaigns, 144 brands); AMA research. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Social Media for Its Own Sake** — Maintaining social accounts because "you have to" without strategy or ROI tracking. WHY BAD: Organic reach on Facebook dropped from 16% (2012) to 1-2% (2025). Instagram reach down 18% YoY. LinkedIn down 34%. Social media is now effectively a paid channel. INSTEAD: Treat social as paid media with organic as credibility-building supplement. Measure against business outcomes, not engagement metrics. SOURCE: Hootsuite; Sprout Social organic reach data. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Dark Patterns in Marketing** — Using deceptive UX (hidden fees, difficult cancellation, disguised ads) to drive conversions. WHY BAD: FTC fined Epic Games $245M for dark patterns. Research shows dark patterns damage brand trust and hurt legitimate competitors. Short-term conversion gains lead to long-term trust erosion. INSTEAD: Design for transparency. Make it easy to buy AND easy to cancel. Trust compounds; deception decays. SOURCE: FTC 2022 dark patterns report; Cambridge University research. EVIDENCE: A

14. **Growth Hacking as Magic Shortcut** — Believing viral loops and "hacks" replace systematic marketing work. WHY BAD: Growth hacking is not wizardry — it's iterative, scientific testing. The "hack" mentality encourages shortcuts over sustainable processes. Most "hacks" are non-repeatable one-time tricks. INSTEAD: Build repeatable, data-driven growth systems. Focus on product-market fit first, then systematic experimentation across the full AARRR funnel. SOURCE: Sean Ellis; CXL growth hacking analysis; ScienceDirect critical review. EVIDENCE: B

15. **Brand Building = Logo Design** — Equating brand strategy with visual identity work. WHY BAD: A logo is one artifact of brand identity. Brand is the total perception in the customer's mind — positioning, experience, reputation, consistency. Spending $50K on a logo rebrand while ignoring brand strategy is wasted money. INSTEAD: Start with brand positioning and strategy. Visual identity follows from, and serves, the strategic foundation. SOURCE: Ries & Trout; Byron Sharp on mental availability. EVIDENCE: B

16. **"Build It and They Will Come"** — Launching a product with no go-to-market strategy. WHY BAD: CB Insights found 42% of startups fail due to no market need. Even with market need, without distribution and awareness, products die in obscurity. INSTEAD: Validate demand before building. Plan distribution and acquisition channels as part of product development, not after launch. SOURCE: CB Insights startup post-mortems; Marc Andreessen on product-market fit. EVIDENCE: A

17. **Chasing Viral Content** — Designing marketing around "going viral" rather than consistent reach. WHY BAD: Virality is unpredictable and uncontrollable. Byron Sharp's research shows consistent, broad reach matters more than occasional spikes. Viral content rarely converts to lasting customers. INSTEAD: Build consistent mental and physical availability (Sharp's twin pillars). Invest in always-on campaigns that reach all category buyers. SOURCE: Byron Sharp, "How Brands Grow." EVIDENCE: A

18. **Over-Targeting / Hyper-Segmentation** — Narrowing audience to only "ideal" customers, missing growth potential. WHY BAD: Byron Sharp's research shows brands grow primarily by acquiring new light buyers, not by increasing loyalty among heavy buyers. Hyper-targeting creates a ceiling. INSTEAD: Reach all category buyers with broad, distinctive messaging. Growth comes from penetration, not loyalty optimization. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass Institute; Byron Sharp. EVIDENCE: A

19. **Loyalty Programs as Growth Strategy** — Over-investing in loyalty programs expecting them to drive growth. WHY BAD: Sharp's research shows loyalty metrics follow market share, not the other way around. Double Jeopardy Law: small brands have fewer buyers who are also slightly less loyal. Loyalty programs mainly reward existing behavior. INSTEAD: Focus on customer acquisition and broad reach. Loyalty follows market share growth, not vice versa. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass; Double Jeopardy Law. EVIDENCE: A

20. **Ignoring Distinctive Brand Assets** — Failing to build and protect recognizable brand codes (colors, sounds, shapes). WHY BAD: Jenni Romaniuk's research shows distinctive assets drive brand recognition and mental availability. Without them, advertising spend is wasted — people can't connect the ad to the brand. INSTEAD: Audit and build distinctive brand assets. Use them consistently across all touchpoints. Test for recognition, not just recall. SOURCE: Jenni Romaniuk, "Building Distinctive Brand Assets" (Ehrenberg-Bass). EVIDENCE: A

21. **Marketing by Committee** — Making creative decisions through large group consensus. WHY BAD: Committee decisions default to the least objectionable (and least distinctive) option. Great marketing requires bold positioning that some people won't like. Consensus produces blandness. INSTEAD: Empower a clear decision-maker with strategic authority. Test with customers, not committees. SOURCE: David Ogilvy principles; creative effectiveness research. EVIDENCE: C

22. **Copying Competitors' Strategy** — Replicating what competitors do rather than differentiating. WHY BAD: Me-too positioning means competing on price. Ries & Trout's research shows the market leader has entrenched mental position; copying them reinforces their dominance. INSTEAD: Find an unoccupied position. If they're first in a category, create a new one. Differentiate or die. SOURCE: Ries & Trout; Michael Porter on competitive strategy. EVIDENCE: B

23. **Premature Scaling of Paid Acquisition** — Throwing money at paid channels before achieving product-market fit. WHY BAD: Paid acquisition amplifies whatever exists — if the product doesn't retain users, you're paying to fill a leaky bucket. CAC spirals while LTV stays flat. INSTEAD: Achieve strong retention and unit economics first. Then scale paid acquisition to amplify proven demand. SOURCE: Andrew Chen, "The Cold Start Problem"; Y Combinator startup data. EVIDENCE: B

24. **Rebranding to Solve Business Problems** — Rebrandsing when the issue is product, pricing, or market fit. WHY BAD: Rebrands destroy accumulated brand equity and distinctive assets. Research shows it takes years to rebuild mental availability. The underlying business problem persists with a new logo. INSTEAD: Diagnose the actual problem (product, pricing, distribution, positioning) before touching brand identity. SOURCE: Romaniuk on distinctive assets; brand equity research. EVIDENCE: B

25. **Email List Neglect** — Building a list but never segmenting, cleaning, or personalizing. WHY BAD: Unsegmented campaigns get 50% lower CTR than segmented ones (Mailchimp). Dirty lists increase bounce rates, trigger spam filters, and destroy sender reputation. INSTEAD: Segment by behavior and value. Clean lists quarterly. Personalize beyond {{first_name}}. SOURCE: Mailchimp segmentation benchmarks. EVIDENCE: A

26. **Measuring Marketing by Spend, Not ROI** — Evaluating marketing success by budget deployed rather than returns generated. WHY BAD: Spending $1M on marketing that returns $500K is worse than spending $100K that returns $300K. Budget-based thinking rewards waste. INSTEAD: Implement marketing mix modeling or multi-touch attribution. Evaluate channels by incremental revenue per dollar spent. SOURCE: Binet & Field; marketing mix modeling research. EVIDENCE: B

27. **Ignoring Market Research** — Making product and marketing decisions based on assumptions rather than customer data. WHY BAD: 42% of startups fail because there was no market need (CB Insights). Internal conviction is not customer validation. INSTEAD: Conduct qualitative and quantitative research before major decisions. Talk to customers continuously. SOURCE: CB Insights; JTBD methodology research. EVIDENCE: A

28. **Over-Personalizing to the Point of Creepiness** — Using data in ways that feel surveillance-like to customers. WHY BAD: Research shows a "personalization-privacy paradox" where too-precise targeting creates distrust. Customers find it unsettling when ads reference information they didn't knowingly share. INSTEAD: Personalize based on declared preferences and explicit behavior. Be transparent about data use. SOURCE: Journal of Marketing research on personalization paradox. EVIDENCE: B

29. **Clickbait Headlines Without Substance** — Driving clicks with misleading titles, leading to immediate bounces. WHY BAD: High bounce rates signal poor quality to search engines. Trust erodes with each misleading experience. Google's Helpful Content system penalizes pages that don't satisfy the search intent suggested by the title. INSTEAD: Write compelling but honest headlines. Match content to the promise. SOURCE: Google Helpful Content guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

30. **Marketing Automation Without Strategy** — Deploying automation tools before defining workflows, segments, and goals. WHY BAD: Automation amplifies bad strategy faster. Poorly configured drip campaigns feel robotic and damage relationships. The tool becomes an expensive spam machine. INSTEAD: Define customer journeys, segment criteria, and trigger logic before configuring automation. Start simple and iterate. SOURCE: HubSpot; Marketo implementation research. EVIDENCE: B

31. **Ignoring Attribution Modeling** — Giving all credit to last-click or first-click without understanding the full journey. WHY BAD: Last-click attribution over-credits bottom-funnel channels and under-credits brand awareness. This misallocates budget toward harvesting and away from demand creation. INSTEAD: Implement multi-touch attribution or marketing mix modeling. Understand that brand-building assists conversions even when it's not the last click. SOURCE: Binet & Field; Google attribution research. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Marketing Speak and Jargon** — Using industry jargon ("synergize," "leverage," "paradigm shift") in customer-facing content. WHY BAD: Customers don't speak marketer. Jargon creates cognitive friction and reduces comprehension. Research shows plain language increases engagement and trust. INSTEAD: Write at 8th-grade reading level for general audiences. Use customer's own words (mine reviews and support tickets for language). SOURCE: Nielsen Norman Group readability research. EVIDENCE: B

33. **Chasing Every New Platform** — Rushing to TikTok, Threads, Bluesky, etc. because they're new, without strategy. WHY BAD: Early-mover advantage is real but fleeting and costly. Most new platforms don't reach the audience critical mass needed for B2B. Opportunity cost of diverting from proven channels. INSTEAD: Monitor new platforms but commit resources only when your target audience demonstrates sustained engagement there. SOURCE: Social media platform lifecycle research. EVIDENCE: C

34. **Treating Marketing as a Cost Center** — Viewing marketing purely as expense rather than investment with measurable returns. WHY BAD: When marketing is a cost center, it's the first budget cut in downturns — exactly when brand investment matters most (Binet & Field show post-recession share of voice gains compound). INSTEAD: Build marketing measurement that ties to revenue. Present marketing as investment with expected returns. SOURCE: IPA effectiveness data; Binet & Field recession research. EVIDENCE: A

35. **One-Size-Fits-All Messaging** — Using the same messaging for all audiences, channels, and buying stages. WHY BAD: A C-suite buyer and an end user have different priorities. Awareness-stage content shouldn't read like a sales pitch. Generic messaging resonates with nobody. INSTEAD: Map messaging to buyer persona and funnel stage. Tailor value propositions to each decision-maker's concerns. SOURCE: Buyer persona research; ABM methodology. EVIDENCE: B

36. **Ignoring Negative Reviews** — Failing to respond to or address negative customer feedback publicly. WHY BAD: 94% of consumers say a bad review has convinced them to avoid a business (ReviewTrackers). Unaddressed complaints signal indifference and compound reputation damage. INSTEAD: Respond promptly, empathetically, and constructively. Use negative feedback to improve. SOURCE: ReviewTrackers; BrightLocal consumer survey data. EVIDENCE: A

37. **Feature-Focused Advertising** — Leading with product features instead of customer benefits and outcomes. WHY BAD: Customers buy outcomes, not features. "Our software has 47 integrations" means nothing without "so you save 10 hours/week." Feature-focus creates cognitive load. INSTEAD: Translate every feature into a benefit. Lead with the outcome the customer cares about. SOURCE: Clayton Christensen JTBD framework; Challenger Sale research. EVIDENCE: B

38. **Seasonal Marketing Only** — Marketing in bursts around seasons/events, going dark between them. WHY BAD: Byron Sharp's research shows brands need continuous mental availability. Going dark allows competitors to build salience. Restarting from zero each season wastes accumulated awareness. INSTEAD: Maintain always-on baseline campaigns with seasonal amplification. Continuous > intermittent. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass; Sharp on mental availability. EVIDENCE: A

39. **Landing Page Mismatch** — Running ads that lead to generic homepages instead of aligned landing pages. WHY BAD: Message mismatch between ad and landing page increases bounce rates by 50%+ and tanks quality scores, raising CPC. INSTEAD: Create dedicated landing pages that match ad copy, intent, and offer exactly. SOURCE: Unbounce landing page research; Google Ads quality score documentation. EVIDENCE: A

40. **Ignoring Mobile Optimization** — Designing marketing assets primarily for desktop experience. WHY BAD: 60%+ of web traffic is mobile (Statista 2024). Google uses mobile-first indexing. Poor mobile experience = lost conversions and lower search rankings. INSTEAD: Design mobile-first. Test all assets on multiple devices. Optimize load time for mobile networks. SOURCE: Google mobile-first indexing; Statista traffic data. EVIDENCE: A

41. **Relying Solely on Organic Social** — Expecting unpaid social media to drive business results. WHY BAD: Organic reach is effectively dead for business accounts (1-2% on Facebook, declining across all platforms). Organic social alone cannot be a revenue channel. INSTEAD: Use organic for community building and credibility; invest in paid social for reach and conversion goals. SOURCE: Hootsuite; Sprout Social organic reach data 2024-2025. EVIDENCE: A

42. **Price-Based Positioning Only** — Competing solely on price without differentiation. WHY BAD: Price wars erode margins for everyone. There's always someone willing to go lower. Price-based positioning attracts price-sensitive customers who churn at the next discount. INSTEAD: Compete on value, not price. Differentiate through product, service, experience, or positioning. SOURCE: Porter's competitive strategy; pricing research. EVIDENCE: A

43. **Ignoring Post-Purchase Experience** — All marketing effort stops after the sale. WHY BAD: Customer acquisition cost is 5-7x retention cost. Onboarding, support, and post-purchase delight drive referrals, reviews, and repeat purchases — the cheapest acquisition channel. INSTEAD: Map and optimize the post-purchase journey. Invest in onboarding, success, and advocacy programs. SOURCE: HubSpot flywheel; Bain & Company retention research. EVIDENCE: A

44. **Marketing-Sales Misalignment** — Marketing and sales operating as separate, non-communicating functions. WHY BAD: Misalignment wastes 60-70% of B2B content (Sirius Decisions). Sales ignores marketing leads; marketing doesn't know what sales needs. INSTEAD: Implement shared KPIs, regular alignment meetings, and shared definitions of MQL/SQL. SOURCE: SiriusDecisions (Forrester); HubSpot smarketing research. EVIDENCE: A

45. **Investing in Awareness Without Conversion Infrastructure** — Driving awareness through expensive brand campaigns without capture mechanisms. WHY BAD: Awareness without a way to convert is a leaky bucket. You're paying to introduce people to your brand with no way to continue the relationship. INSTEAD: Ensure every awareness touchpoint has a clear next step — email capture, retargeting pixel, content offer. SOURCE: Demand generation best practices; full-funnel marketing research. EVIDENCE: B

46. **Rigid Annual Marketing Plans** — Creating a fixed annual plan and refusing to adapt to market changes. WHY BAD: Markets move faster than annual cycles. Competitors launch, algorithms change, customer behavior shifts. Rigid plans waste budget on outdated tactics. INSTEAD: Set annual strategic direction but plan tactically in 90-day sprints. Build in flexibility for reallocation. SOURCE: Agile marketing methodology research. EVIDENCE: B

47. **Outsourcing Strategy, Not Just Execution** — Having agencies define marketing strategy rather than just execute it. WHY BAD: Agencies optimize for their billable scope, not your business outcomes. They lack deep knowledge of your customers, product, and market dynamics. Strategy must be owned internally. INSTEAD: Own strategy internally. Outsource specialized execution (design, media buying, SEO technical work). SOURCE: In-house vs agency effectiveness research. EVIDENCE: C

48. **Neglecting Existing Customers in Communications** — All marketing communications targeted at prospects, none at current customers. WHY BAD: Existing customers are most likely to buy again and refer. Byron Sharp shows growth comes from all category buyers, including refreshing existing customer memory structures. INSTEAD: Dedicate a meaningful portion of marketing to customer nurture, education, and expansion. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass; customer marketing research. EVIDENCE: B

49. **Over-Reliance on a Single Channel** — Putting 80%+ of marketing budget/effort into one channel. WHY BAD: Algorithm changes (Facebook 2018 news feed change, Google updates) can devastate businesses overnight. Single-channel dependency is existential risk. INSTEAD: Diversify across 2-3 proven channels once each is optimized. Build owned media (email, community) as insurance. SOURCE: Platform risk case studies; diversification research. EVIDENCE: A

50. **Measuring Success by Volume of Output** — Tracking "number of blog posts published" or "emails sent" rather than impact. WHY BAD: Activity metrics create busywork incentives. Publishing 50 mediocre blog posts is worse than 5 great ones with distribution. Volume without quality is noise. INSTEAD: Measure outcomes (traffic, leads, revenue attributed) not outputs (content pieces produced). SOURCE: Content Marketing Institute effectiveness research. EVIDENCE: B

## 50 BEST PRACTICES

1. **Broad Reach Over Hyper-Targeting** — Reach all category buyers, not just a narrow "ideal customer" segment. WHY: Byron Sharp's research across hundreds of brands shows growth comes primarily from acquiring new light buyers. Hyper-targeting creates growth ceilings. The Double Jeopardy Law shows loyalty follows market share. SOURCE: Byron Sharp, "How Brands Grow"; Ehrenberg-Bass Institute. EVIDENCE: A

2. **Build Distinctive Brand Assets** — Develop and consistently use recognizable brand codes (colors, shapes, sounds, characters, taglines). WHY: Jenni Romaniuk's research shows distinctive assets drive brand recognition and mental availability. Consistent use across touchpoints ensures advertising is correctly attributed to your brand. SOURCE: Romaniuk, "Building Distinctive Brand Assets"; Ehrenberg-Bass. EVIDENCE: A

3. **60/40 Brand-to-Activation Split** — Allocate approximately 60% of marketing budget to brand-building, 40% to sales activation. WHY: Binet & Field's analysis of 30+ years of IPA effectiveness data shows this ratio optimizes both short-term sales and long-term profitability. Brand building makes activation more efficient. SOURCE: Les Binet & Peter Field, "The Long and the Short of It." EVIDENCE: A

4. **Mental and Physical Availability** — Ensure your brand is easy to think of (mental availability) and easy to buy (physical availability). WHY: Sharp's twin pillars of brand growth. Mental availability = being salient in buying situations. Physical availability = being present where and when the customer wants to buy. Both must be maximized. SOURCE: Byron Sharp, "How Brands Grow." EVIDENCE: A

5. **Clear Positioning Strategy** — Occupy a single, clear position in the customer's mind. WHY: Ries & Trout demonstrated that in oversaturated markets, only clearly positioned brands get noticed. The first brand in a new category captures 2x the market share. Positioning is about the customer's mind, not your product. SOURCE: Ries & Trout, "Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind." EVIDENCE: A

6. **Content Distribution as Priority** — Spend equal time distributing content as creating it (50/50 rule). WHY: With 4.6B daily content pieces, creation without distribution is invisible. Segmented, promoted content outperforms even excellent undistributed content. INSTEAD OF: The "publish and pray" approach. SOURCE: Ross Simmonds distribution research; Neil Patel analysis. EVIDENCE: B

7. **CLV-Based Marketing Decisions** — Use Customer Lifetime Value as the primary metric for marketing investment decisions. WHY: CLV-based segmentation identifies the most profitable customer groups. Marketing spend can be allocated to activities yielding the highest long-term value. A 5% retention increase = 25-95% profit increase. SOURCE: Wharton CLV research; Bain & Company. EVIDENCE: A

8. **Flywheel Model Over Funnel** — Structure marketing, sales, and service around a flywheel where customers drive growth. WHY: Customers as growth engine reduces CAC over time. Referrals, reviews, and word-of-mouth from delighted customers are the cheapest and most trusted acquisition channel. SOURCE: HubSpot flywheel research. EVIDENCE: B

9. **Evidence-Based A/B Testing** — Run properly designed experiments with pre-calculated sample sizes, one variable at a time, to 95% statistical significance. WHY: Proper testing eliminates the 40% false positive risk from multiple testing. Results are actionable and reliable. Avoids costly implementation of non-winning variants. SOURCE: CXL; Analytics Toolkit; Adobe Target research. EVIDENCE: A

10. **Multi-Touch Attribution** — Implement attribution modeling that accounts for the full customer journey, not just last-click. WHY: Last-click attribution over-credits conversion channels and under-credits awareness. This systematically misallocates budget away from brand-building. Multi-touch reveals the true influence of each touchpoint. SOURCE: Google attribution research; Binet & Field. EVIDENCE: A

11. **Always-On Marketing** — Maintain continuous marketing presence rather than campaign bursts. WHY: Byron Sharp shows mental availability requires continuous refreshment. Going dark between campaigns cedes salience to competitors. Continuous presence maintains brand memory structures. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass; Sharp on mental availability. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Customer-Language Messaging** — Use words and phrases from customer reviews, support tickets, and interviews in marketing copy. WHY: Customers don't speak marketer. Using their exact language increases comprehension, trust, and relevance. It signals understanding of their world. SOURCE: Voice-of-customer research; Nielsen Norman Group. EVIDENCE: B

13. **Segmented Email Marketing** — Segment email lists by behavior, purchase history, and engagement level. WHY: Segmented campaigns get 100.95% higher CTR than unsegmented (Mailchimp). Relevant content respects the subscriber's attention and reduces unsubscribe rates. SOURCE: Mailchimp segmentation benchmarks. EVIDENCE: A

14. **Mobile-First Design** — Design all marketing assets for mobile consumption first, then adapt to desktop. WHY: 60%+ of web traffic is mobile. Google uses mobile-first indexing. A 1-second delay in mobile load time decreases conversions by 7%. SOURCE: Google mobile-first indexing; Statista traffic data. EVIDENCE: A

15. **Marketing-Sales Alignment (Smarketing)** — Shared KPIs, regular meetings, and agreed definitions between marketing and sales. WHY: Aligned organizations achieve 36% higher customer retention and 38% higher sales win rates. Misalignment wastes 60-70% of B2B content. SOURCE: SiriusDecisions (Forrester); HubSpot smarketing research. EVIDENCE: A

16. **Negative Review Response** — Respond to all negative reviews promptly, empathetically, and constructively. WHY: 94% of consumers say bad reviews influenced avoidance. Constructive responses demonstrate accountability and can convert detractors. Public responses are read by future prospects. SOURCE: ReviewTrackers; BrightLocal surveys. EVIDENCE: A

17. **Owned Media Investment** — Build email lists, communities, and properties you control alongside rented channels. WHY: Platform algorithm changes can devastate businesses overnight (Facebook 2018 organic reach collapse). Owned media is insurance against platform risk. SOURCE: Platform risk case studies. EVIDENCE: B

18. **Post-Purchase Journey Optimization** — Map and invest in the customer experience after the sale. WHY: Retention is 5-7x cheaper than acquisition. Onboarding quality determines adoption, which determines renewal and referral. The post-purchase experience is your cheapest marketing channel. SOURCE: Bain & Company; HubSpot flywheel. EVIDENCE: A

19. **Helpful Content Creation** — Create content that genuinely solves customer problems, not content designed to rank. WHY: Google's Helpful Content system (integrated into core ranking 2024) rewards content that demonstrates experience, expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness (E-E-A-T). SOURCE: Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

20. **Channel Focus Then Expansion** — Master one acquisition channel before expanding to others. WHY: Depth beats breadth. Full investment in one channel produces faster learnings and better returns than splitting across many. Expand from a position of strength. SOURCE: Peter Thiel growth philosophy; Northbeam channel strategy research. EVIDENCE: B

21. **Nano/Micro-Influencer Partnerships** — Prioritize smaller influencers with engaged audiences over macro-influencers. WHY: Nano-influencers deliver 3x+ higher ROI than macro-influencers (IPA data). Smaller audiences have higher trust and engagement rates. Cost per genuine conversion is dramatically lower. SOURCE: IPA Influencer Database (220 campaigns); AMA research. EVIDENCE: A

22. **Landing Page-Ad Alignment** — Create dedicated landing pages that match each ad's copy, intent, and offer. WHY: Message match reduces bounce rates by 50%+, improves quality scores, reduces CPC, and increases conversion rates. SOURCE: Unbounce landing page research; Google Ads documentation. EVIDENCE: A

23. **Marketing Mix Modeling** — Use statistical modeling to understand the incremental contribution of each marketing channel. WHY: Goes beyond attribution to understand the true causal impact of marketing spend. Identifies diminishing returns and optimal budget allocation across channels. SOURCE: Marketing mix modeling research; Nielsen. EVIDENCE: A

24. **Agile Marketing Sprints** — Plan tactically in 90-day cycles with quarterly strategy reviews, not rigid annual plans. WHY: Markets move faster than annual cycles. Quarterly reviews allow budget reallocation based on performance data. Flexibility beats prediction. SOURCE: Agile marketing methodology. EVIDENCE: B

25. **Genuine Scarcity (Not Fake)** — Use real supply constraints and genuine time limits, never fabricated urgency. WHY: Fake scarcity, when discovered, damages brand trust by up to 45%. Genuine scarcity builds value perception without trust cost. SOURCE: ScienceDirect research on scarcity backfire; UX Psychology. EVIDENCE: A

26. **Jobs-to-Be-Done Framework** — Frame marketing around the customer's job to be done, not product features. WHY: Clayton Christensen's research shows customers "hire" products to make progress in their lives. Marketing that speaks to the job resonates at a deeper level than feature lists. SOURCE: Christensen, "Competing Against Luck." EVIDENCE: B

27. **Consistent Brand Experience** — Deliver the same brand promise across every touchpoint (website, store, support, social, product). WHY: Inconsistency erodes trust and creates confusion. Consistent brands are 3-4x more likely to have strong brand visibility. SOURCE: Lucidpress brand consistency report. EVIDENCE: B

28. **Data-Driven Personas** — Build buyer personas from actual customer data and behavior, not assumptions and demographics. WHY: Assumption-based personas lead to messaging that resonates with nobody real. Data-driven personas reflect actual buying motivations and behavior patterns. SOURCE: Buyer persona research; Adele Revella methodology. EVIDENCE: B

29. **Competitive Differentiation Audit** — Regularly audit how you're positioned versus competitors in customers' minds. WHY: Markets shift. Competitors reposition. What was differentiated becomes table stakes. Regular audits ensure your positioning remains distinct and relevant. SOURCE: Ries & Trout; competitive positioning research. EVIDENCE: B

30. **Transparency as Brand Strategy** — Be open about pricing, process, ingredients, and mistakes. WHY: Consumer trust in brands is at historical lows. Transparency differentiates. Patagonia, Buffer, and Everlane built loyalty through radical transparency. SOURCE: Edelman Trust Barometer; transparency marketing research. EVIDENCE: B

31. **Customer Advocacy Programs** — Systematically turn satisfied customers into advocates through referral programs and testimonial collection. WHY: Word-of-mouth is the most trusted form of marketing. Referral customers have 16% higher lifetime value. Advocacy is the cheapest acquisition. SOURCE: Nielsen trust research; referral marketing data. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Marketing Experimentation Culture** — Run continuous small experiments rather than big-bang campaigns. WHY: Small experiments de-risk decisions. Rapid learning compounds over time. The best marketing organizations run 10-20 experiments per month. SOURCE: Growth methodology research; CXL experimentation. EVIDENCE: B

33. **Full-Funnel Content Strategy** — Create content for every stage: awareness, consideration, decision, and post-purchase. WHY: Buyers self-educate through 60-70% of the journey before talking to sales. Content at each stage nurtures progress. Missing stages create gaps that lose prospects. SOURCE: Gartner B2B buying research. EVIDENCE: A

34. **SEO as Helpful Content Strategy** — Align SEO with creating the best answer to every relevant question customers ask. WHY: Google's systems increasingly reward genuinely helpful content. Helpful content accumulates organic traffic over years, creating compounding returns. SOURCE: Google E-E-A-T guidelines; Helpful Content system. EVIDENCE: A

35. **Lead Scoring Based on Behavior** — Score leads by engagement behavior (pages visited, content downloaded, email interaction) not just demographics. WHY: Behavioral signals indicate purchase intent more accurately than demographic fit alone. Sales teams receiving behaviorally-scored leads close at higher rates. SOURCE: HubSpot; Marketo lead scoring research. EVIDENCE: B

36. **Social Proof at Decision Points** — Place testimonials, case studies, and reviews at the moments where customers make decisions. WHY: Cialdini's research shows social proof is one of the strongest persuasion principles. Reviews influence 93% of purchase decisions. Placement matters as much as existence. SOURCE: Cialdini, "Influence"; BrightLocal consumer review survey. EVIDENCE: A

37. **Customer Retention Marketing** — Allocate dedicated marketing budget and resources to existing customer retention and expansion. WHY: Acquisition cost is 5-7x retention cost. Existing customers have higher conversion rates, larger order values, and generate referrals. SOURCE: Bain & Company; HubSpot retention research. EVIDENCE: A

38. **Share of Voice Strategy** — Maintain or exceed your share of voice relative to market share (ESOV = excess share of voice). WHY: Binet & Field's research shows brands that maintain excess share of voice (SOV > market share) grow; those below it shrink. ESOV is the strongest predictor of market share growth. SOURCE: Binet & Field; IPA Databank. EVIDENCE: A

39. **Community Building Over Audience Building** — Build communities where customers interact with each other, not just with your brand. WHY: Communities create switching costs, generate content, provide feedback, and reduce support costs. Community members have 19% higher spend. SOURCE: CMX community ROI research. EVIDENCE: B

40. **Plain Language Communication** — Write all customer-facing content at an 8th-grade reading level or below. WHY: Nielsen Norman Group research shows plain language increases comprehension, reduces cognitive load, and increases task completion rates across all education levels. Even experts prefer simple language. SOURCE: Nielsen Norman Group readability research. EVIDENCE: A

41. **Value-Based Pricing Communication** — Frame pricing around value delivered, not cost of production. WHY: Customers pay for outcomes, not inputs. Value-based framing increases willingness to pay and reduces price sensitivity. "Save 10 hours/week" beats "$99/month." SOURCE: Pricing psychology research; Simon-Kucher & Partners. EVIDENCE: B

42. **Cross-Functional Growth Teams** — Form teams combining marketing, product, engineering, and data to drive growth. WHY: Growth is a cross-functional challenge. Marketing alone can't fix product problems. Product alone can't drive acquisition. Integration accelerates learning and impact. SOURCE: Sean Ellis growth team methodology. EVIDENCE: B

43. **Customer Feedback Loop** — Systematically collect, analyze, and act on customer feedback in marketing decisions. WHY: Customer language becomes copy. Customer problems become content topics. Customer objections become FAQ content. The feedback loop grounds marketing in reality. SOURCE: Voice-of-customer research. EVIDENCE: B

44. **Incrementality Testing** — Run holdout experiments to measure the true incremental impact of marketing activities. WHY: Attribution models can't prove causation. Incrementality tests (geographic holdouts, randomized control groups) reveal what marketing actually causes vs. what would happen anyway. SOURCE: Facebook lift study methodology; Google incrementality research. EVIDENCE: A

45. **Brand Consistency Over Time** — Maintain core brand elements and positioning for years, not months. WHY: Sharp's research shows brand memory structures build slowly and cumulatively. Frequent rebrands and repositioning destroy accumulated mental availability. SOURCE: Ehrenberg-Bass; Romaniuk on distinctive assets. EVIDENCE: A

46. **First-Party Data Strategy** — Build direct relationships and first-party data collection as cookies deprecate. WHY: Third-party cookies are dying. Walled gardens are tightening. First-party data (email, purchase history, declared preferences) is the future of targeting and personalization. SOURCE: Privacy regulation research; Google cookie deprecation timeline. EVIDENCE: A

47. **Emotion in Brand Advertising** — Use emotional creative for brand-building campaigns, rational for activation. WHY: Binet & Field's research shows emotional campaigns are more effective for long-term brand building, while rational messages work for short-term activation. The two serve different purposes. SOURCE: Les Binet & Peter Field; IPA effectiveness data. EVIDENCE: A

48. **Marketing Measurement Framework** — Establish a clear hierarchy: business metrics > marketing metrics > channel metrics > activity metrics. WHY: Without hierarchy, teams optimize for activity (posts published) rather than outcomes (revenue generated). The framework prevents vanity metric traps. SOURCE: Marketing measurement best practices. EVIDENCE: B

49. **Strategic Patience** — Allow brand-building investments 6-12 months before expecting measurable ROI. WHY: Brand effects build slowly but compound and decay slowly. Cutting brand investment because it doesn't show results in 30 days guarantees it won't work. Activation shows quick results; brand shows bigger results later. SOURCE: Binet & Field on brand decay curves. EVIDENCE: A

50. **Category Entry Points (CEPs)** — Identify and dominate the mental cues that trigger customers to think of your category. WHY: Romaniuk and Sharp's research shows brands grow by linking to more category entry points. A coffee brand linked to "tired morning," "meeting with friends," and "afternoon break" has 3x the mental availability of one linked to only "morning." SOURCE: Romaniuk, Ehrenberg-Bass CEP research. EVIDENCE: A

## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS ABOUT MARKETING

1. **"Viral marketing is a strategy"** — LLMs often recommend "create viral content" as actionable advice. Virality is an outcome, not a strategy. It's unpredictable and uncontrollable. Sharp's research shows consistent broad reach matters more than spikes.

2. **"More content = more results"** — LLMs default to recommending high content volume. Quality and distribution matter far more than volume. 5 well-distributed articles outperform 50 undistributed ones.

3. **"Social media is free marketing"** — LLMs describe social media as a free channel. With 1-2% organic reach, social media is effectively a paid channel. The "free" framing leads to massive time waste.

4. **"Loyalty programs drive growth"** — LLMs recommend loyalty programs as growth strategies. Sharp's research shows loyalty follows market share, not the other way around. Loyalty programs mainly reward existing behavior.

5. **"Target your ideal customer precisely"** — LLMs over-recommend hyper-targeting. Byron Sharp shows brands grow by reaching ALL category buyers, especially light buyers. Hyper-targeting creates growth ceilings.

6. **"Differentiation is about being different"** — LLMs confuse differentiation with distinctiveness. Sharp shows perceived differentiation is weak; distinctiveness (being recognizable) drives brand choice. Being distinctive is more important than being different.

7. **"Build it and they will come"** — LLMs often describe great product features as sufficient for success. Distribution is everything. The best product with no distribution loses to an inferior product with excellent distribution.

8. **"SEO is about keywords"** — LLMs give keyword-centric SEO advice. Modern SEO is about helpful content, topical authority, user experience, and E-E-A-T. Keyword manipulation is penalized.

9. **"Email marketing is dying"** — LLMs sometimes suggest email is outdated. Email remains the highest-ROI digital marketing channel ($36-42 per $1 spent). It's the only owned, permission-based digital channel at scale.

10. **"Brand building is subjective and unmeasurable"** — LLMs often treat brand as a fuzzy concept. Brand metrics (aided/unaided awareness, mental availability, distinctive asset recognition, ESOV) are well-established and measurable. Binet & Field proved brand's impact on long-term profitability.

# Sales: Evidence-Based Practices

> Sources: Neil Rackham (SPIN Selling, 35,000 calls), Dixon & Adamson (Challenger Sale, 6,000 reps), Gong.io conversation intelligence data, HubSpot Buyer Behavior Reports, Cognism/ZoomInfo cold calling statistics, IPA Databank.

## 50 BAD PRACTICES

1. **High-Pressure Closing Techniques** — Using aggressive closing tactics (hard close, assumptive close, puppy dog close) to force decisions. WHY BAD: Research shows high-pressure tactics trigger psychological reactance — customers do the opposite of what's intended. 25-40% reduction in repeat business vs. consultative approaches. HubSpot found "pushy" is the #1 word buyers associate with salespeople. INSTEAD: Use consultative closing that confirms mutual fit. Ask if the customer is ready; don't push them to be ready. SOURCE: ResearchGate consumer perception studies; HubSpot "Buyers Speak Out." EVIDENCE: A

2. **"Always Be Closing" Mentality** — Treating every interaction as a closing opportunity. WHY BAD: ABC was relevant in transactional sales (Glengarry Glen Ross, 1992). In complex B2B sales, Rackham's research across 35,000 calls showed closing techniques actually DECREASE success rates in large sales. INSTEAD: Always Be Helping. Focus on understanding problems and creating value before asking for commitment. SOURCE: Neil Rackham, "SPIN Selling" (12-year, 35,000-call study). EVIDENCE: A

3. **Cold Call Volume Over Quality** — Making 100+ generic cold calls per day. WHY BAD: Average cold call success rate is 4.82% (2024). 10 well-researched calls outperform 50 generic ones. 42% of reps lack sufficient pre-call information. Top performers (6-10% success) differ from average (2-3%) by targeting precision, not volume. INSTEAD: Research prospects, personalize outreach, combine calls with email and LinkedIn. Quality > quantity. SOURCE: Cognism 2025 cold calling report; ZoomInfo benchmarks. EVIDENCE: A

4. **Feature Dumping** — Presenting every product feature in a rapid-fire monologue. WHY BAD: The Challenger Sale research shows features without context are meaningless. Rackham found top performers ask questions rather than present features. Feature dumps create cognitive overload and buyer disengagement. INSTEAD: Use the Challenger framework: teach, tailor, take control. Present only features that solve the prospect's specific problems. SOURCE: Dixon & Adamson, "The Challenger Sale"; Rackham SPIN research. EVIDENCE: A

5. **Ignoring Qualification** — Selling to anyone who will listen, regardless of fit. WHY BAD: Unqualified pipeline creates forecasting illusion — "full pipeline" that never closes. Sales teams waste 50%+ of time on prospects who will never buy. INSTEAD: Use BANT (quick screen) + MEDDIC (deep qualification). Disqualify fast. Time saved on bad prospects goes to good ones. SOURCE: BANT/MEDDIC qualification research; Salesforce sales data. EVIDENCE: A

6. **Discounting as Default Strategy** — Offering discounts at first price resistance. WHY BAD: A 10% discount on 40% margin requires 33% more volume to break even. 20% discount requires doubling sales. Frequent discounting trains customers to wait for sales and devalues brand. INSTEAD: Sell on value first. If discounting is necessary, tie it to commitment (multi-year, larger scope, faster close). Never discount without getting something in return. SOURCE: Phoenix Strategy Group margin analysis; pricing research. EVIDENCE: A

7. **Not Listening (Monologue Selling)** — Talking through the entire sales call without understanding the prospect's situation. WHY BAD: Rackham's research showed top performers ask far more questions and listen more than they talk. 69% of buyers want reps who listen to their needs. Monologue selling signals you don't understand or care about their problem. INSTEAD: Follow a structured question framework (SPIN: Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff). Aim for 60/40 prospect-to-rep talk ratio. SOURCE: Rackham SPIN research; HubSpot buyer survey. EVIDENCE: A

8. **Fake Urgency and Scarcity** — Creating artificial deadlines ("this price expires Friday") or fake supply limits. WHY BAD: False scarcity, when discovered, damages trust by up to 45%. Customers learn to ignore urgency cues. A fashion retailer's fake "limited edition" caused 37% sales drop the following quarter. INSTEAD: Use genuine deadlines (end of quarter pricing, real supply constraints). Be honest about timelines. SOURCE: ScienceDirect scarcity backfire research; UX Psychology studies. EVIDENCE: A

9. **Script Reading** — Delivering rigid scripts without adapting to the conversation. WHY BAD: Buyers detect scripted calls instantly and disengage. Scripts prevent genuine discovery and rapport. They signal the rep doesn't understand the prospect's specific situation. INSTEAD: Use structured frameworks (SPIN, Challenger) as guides, not scripts. Prepare key questions and talking points, but adapt in real-time. SOURCE: Rackham; Challenger methodology. EVIDENCE: B

10. **Churning Relationships for Short-Term Wins** — Prioritizing this quarter's number over long-term client relationships. WHY BAD: Customer acquisition costs 5-7x more than retention. Churned customers share negative experiences. Short-term thinking creates a reputation that precedes you. INSTEAD: Build relationships that compound. A customer who stays 3 years at full price is worth more than 3 customers who churn after a discounted first year. SOURCE: Bain & Company retention research. EVIDENCE: A

11. **Overselling / Overpromising** — Promising features, timelines, or outcomes the product can't deliver. WHY BAD: Creates a customer success crisis from day one. Churn increases, negative reviews accumulate, and trust is permanently damaged. Implementation teams inherit impossible expectations. INSTEAD: Underpromise, overdeliver. Set accurate expectations during sales. The sale doesn't end at signature — it begins there. SOURCE: Customer success research; Gainsight retention data. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Skipping Discovery** — Jumping straight to demo or pitch without understanding the prospect's situation. WHY BAD: Without discovery, you're presenting a generic solution to an unknown problem. Rackham's research shows discovery quality is the #1 predictor of sales success in complex deals. INSTEAD: Conduct thorough discovery before any presentation. Use SPIN questions to uncover the real problem and its implications. SOURCE: Neil Rackham SPIN Selling; Gong.io conversation data. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Selling to the Wrong Stakeholder** — Pitching to an individual contributor who can't authorize a purchase. WHY BAD: In B2B, average buying groups have 6-10 decision makers (Gartner). Selling to a single champion without mapping the buying committee wastes cycles. INSTEAD: Map the buying committee early. Use MEDDIC's "Champion" and "Economic Buyer" concepts. Multi-thread into the account. SOURCE: Gartner B2B buying research; MEDDIC methodology. EVIDENCE: A

14. **Ignoring Competitors in Deals** — Pretending competitors don't exist or dismissing them outright. WHY BAD: Buyers have already researched competitors. Dismissing them seems dishonest or ignorant. You lose credibility. INSTEAD: Acknowledge competitors respectfully. Differentiate on strengths. Win by reframing the evaluation criteria, not by bashing alternatives. SOURCE: Challenger Sale reframing methodology. EVIDENCE: B

15. **Following Up Without Value** — Sending "just checking in" or "circling back" emails with no new information. WHY BAD: Valueless follow-ups signal desperation and waste the prospect's time. They train prospects to ignore your emails. INSTEAD: Every follow-up should provide new value: a relevant article, industry insight, case study, or answer to a question they raised. SOURCE: Sales development best practices; Gong.io follow-up research. EVIDENCE: B

16. **Neglecting CRM Hygiene** — Not updating opportunity data, stages, or next steps in CRM. WHY BAD: Dirty CRM data destroys forecast accuracy. Pipeline reviews are based on fiction. Management can't provide accurate coaching. Lost institutional knowledge when reps leave. INSTEAD: Update CRM within 24 hours of every interaction. Automate where possible. Make CRM updates part of the sales process, not an afterthought. SOURCE: Salesforce CRM adoption research. EVIDENCE: B

17. **One-Size-Fits-All Demo** — Running the same product demonstration regardless of the prospect's industry, role, or needs. WHY BAD: Generic demos fail to connect features to the prospect's specific problems. The Challenger research shows tailoring is essential — customers need to see THEIR world, not your product. INSTEAD: Build demos around discovery findings. Show only relevant features. Use the prospect's language and scenarios. SOURCE: Challenger Sale "tailor" methodology; demo best practices. EVIDENCE: B

18. **Pitching Too Early in the Cycle** — Launching into the sales pitch before establishing trust or understanding needs. WHY BAD: Rackham found that premature solutions are the most common mistake in complex sales. Pitching before understanding signals you care about your quota, not their problem. INSTEAD: Earn the right to pitch through quality discovery. Transition to solution only after the prospect agrees the problem is worth solving. SOURCE: SPIN Selling; consultative selling research. EVIDENCE: A

19. **Ignoring Buying Signals** — Missing or not acting on prospect engagement signals (repeat visits, content downloads, questions about implementation). WHY BAD: Buying signals indicate readiness. Missing them means missing optimal timing. Continuing to "nurture" a ready buyer is as bad as pushing a non-ready one. INSTEAD: Track behavioral signals. When multiple signals align, advance the conversation. Ask directly about timeline and process. SOURCE: Gong.io buying signal research; lead scoring methodology. EVIDENCE: B

20. **Email Blast Prospecting** — Sending identical mass emails to entire prospect lists. WHY BAD: Cold email reply rates dropped to 5.1% in 2024. Gmail's 2024 spam rules further reduce deliverability. Mass emails damage sender reputation, reducing deliverability for all future emails. INSTEAD: Personalize each email with prospect-specific research. Limit daily send volume. Warm up domains. SOURCE: Cognism email statistics; Gmail deliverability guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

21. **Talking About Price Too Early** — Discussing pricing before establishing value. WHY BAD: Without value context, any price feels expensive. Price discussions before value establishment anchor the prospect on cost rather than ROI. Premature pricing invites comparison shopping on price alone. INSTEAD: Establish the cost of the problem first. Quantify value. Then present price as a fraction of value delivered. SOURCE: Value-based selling research; SPIN Need-Payoff methodology. EVIDENCE: B

22. **Selling Against the Status Quo Poorly** — Failing to address why change is necessary (not just why your product is good). WHY BAD: The Challenger research shows 60% of qualified deals end in "no decision." The real competitor is the status quo. If you don't make the case for change, nothing happens. INSTEAD: Use the Challenger "teach" step to show prospects a problem they didn't know they had. Make the cost of inaction concrete. SOURCE: Dixon & Adamson, "The Challenger Sale." EVIDENCE: A

23. **Commission-Driven Behavior Visible to Buyers** — Acting in ways that obviously prioritize commission over customer value. WHY BAD: Buyers are sophisticated. They detect commission-driven urgency, upselling, and product steering. It destroys trust and triggers reactance. INSTEAD: Genuinely recommend the best solution, even if it means a smaller deal. Long-term relationships and referrals are more valuable than any single commission. SOURCE: Trust-based selling research. EVIDENCE: B

24. **Ignoring the Implementation/Onboarding Handoff** — Making the sale then disappearing, leaving customer success to figure it out. WHY BAD: Poor handoffs are the #1 cause of early churn. Information loss between sales and CS means the customer repeats everything. Expectations set in sales are unknown to CS. INSTEAD: Create a structured handoff document. Join the kickoff call. Ensure continuity of relationship during the transition. SOURCE: Gainsight customer success research. EVIDENCE: B

25. **Relying on a Single Champion** — Building the entire deal around one internal advocate. WHY BAD: Champions change jobs, lose influence, or get overruled. Gartner shows 6-10 stakeholders in B2B decisions. Single-threading is single-point-of-failure. INSTEAD: Multi-thread into 3+ stakeholders. Map the buying committee. Build consensus across the organization. SOURCE: Gartner B2B buying groups research; MEDDIC. EVIDENCE: A

26. **Proposal Without Verbal Agreement** — Sending a detailed proposal before securing verbal alignment on scope and price range. WHY BAD: Proposals without prior alignment get shopped to competitors or ignored. They commoditize your offering. The prospect uses your proposal to negotiate with alternatives. INSTEAD: Gain verbal agreement on scope, timeline, and ballpark investment before formalizing. The proposal should confirm, not propose. SOURCE: Sales negotiation best practices. EVIDENCE: B

27. **Ignoring the Decision Process** — Not asking how the prospect makes buying decisions. WHY BAD: Without understanding the decision process (who approves, what's needed, timeline, legal review), you can't forecast accurately or remove obstacles. Deals stall in procurement hell. INSTEAD: MEDDIC's "Decision Process" and "Decision Criteria" — map these early. Understand every step between "yes" and signature. SOURCE: MEDDIC methodology. EVIDENCE: A

28. **Using Competitive FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt)** — Spreading negative, unverified claims about competitors. WHY BAD: Buyers have access to peer reviews and can verify claims. FUD damages YOUR credibility, not the competitor's. It signals insecurity. INSTEAD: Win on your strengths. Address competitor comparisons factually and respectfully. Reframe evaluation criteria in your favor. SOURCE: Challenger Sale reframing; trust-based selling. EVIDENCE: B

29. **Sandbagging Deals** — Deliberately holding deals to hit future quarter targets, harming customer timing. WHY BAD: Customers have their own timelines. Delaying to manipulate your forecast risks the deal (budget reallocation, competitor entry, champion departure). It's selfish and destructive. INSTEAD: Close when the customer is ready. Manage forecast expectations with management transparently. SOURCE: Sales management research. EVIDENCE: B

30. **Treating Objections as Attacks** — Becoming defensive when prospects raise concerns or objections. WHY BAD: Objections are buying signals — they indicate engagement and consideration. Defensive responses escalate tension and destroy rapport. INSTEAD: Welcome objections. Acknowledge the concern, clarify, and address with evidence. "That's a great question" isn't just a phrase — mean it. SOURCE: Rackham objection handling research. EVIDENCE: B

31. **Product-Centric Conversations** — Structuring every conversation around what your product does rather than what the customer needs. WHY BAD: Buyers don't care about your product; they care about their problem. Product-centric conversations feel like being marketed to, not helped. INSTEAD: Lead with the customer's world. Discuss their challenges, goals, and current state. Introduce product only as it relates to their specific needs. SOURCE: SPIN Selling; Challenger Sale methodology. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Ignoring Post-Sale Expansion Opportunities** — Treating the initial sale as the end of the relationship. WHY BAD: Existing customers convert at 60-70% vs. 5-20% for new prospects (Marketing Metrics). Expansion revenue costs a fraction of new acquisition. Ignoring expansion leaves revenue on the table. INSTEAD: Build an expansion playbook. Identify triggers (usage milestones, new team members, budget cycles) for expansion conversations. SOURCE: Gainsight; expansion revenue research. EVIDENCE: A

33. **Forecasting on Hope** — Including deals in the forecast based on gut feel rather than verified criteria. WHY BAD: Hope-based forecasts are consistently inflated. They lead to resource misallocation, missed targets, and lost credibility with leadership. INSTEAD: Use MEDDIC or similar framework to score deal health. Only forecast deals with verified decision criteria, identified economic buyer, and agreed timeline. SOURCE: MEDDIC methodology; Salesforce forecasting data. EVIDENCE: A

34. **Talking More Than Listening** — Dominating the conversation, especially during discovery. WHY BAD: Gong.io data shows top performers have a 43:57 talk-to-listen ratio (prospect talks more). Talking too much means missing critical information about needs, objections, and decision criteria. INSTEAD: Aim for 40/60 or better talk-to-listen ratio. Ask open-ended questions and then actively listen. SOURCE: Gong.io conversation intelligence data. EVIDENCE: A

35. **Neglecting Warm Introductions** — Relying exclusively on cold outreach when referral paths exist. WHY BAD: Referred leads convert at 3-5x the rate of cold leads and have 16% higher CLV. Yet most reps never ask for introductions. INSTEAD: Systematically ask satisfied customers for introductions. Build referral into the post-sale process. SOURCE: Referral marketing research; Nielsen trust data. EVIDENCE: A

36. **Treating All Leads Equally** — Giving the same attention to every inbound lead regardless of qualification signals. WHY BAD: Not all leads are created equal. Spending equal time on a low-intent content download and a demo request wastes the rep's most valuable resource: time. INSTEAD: Implement lead scoring. Prioritize by behavioral signals (pricing page visits, case study downloads, demo requests) and firmographic fit. SOURCE: HubSpot lead scoring; Marketo research. EVIDENCE: B

37. **Using Manipulation Techniques** — Employing NLP tricks, mirroring, anchoring, and other psychological manipulation. WHY BAD: Sophisticated buyers detect manipulation. Once detected, trust is permanently broken. These techniques work in textbooks, not in long-term B2B relationships. INSTEAD: Build genuine rapport through competence, honesty, and customer-centric behavior. Real influence comes from being genuinely helpful. SOURCE: Trust-based selling research. EVIDENCE: B

38. **Selling During Objection Handling** — Responding to objections by pitching more features instead of addressing the concern. WHY BAD: The prospect raised a concern; they need it addressed, not buried under more features. Feature-stacking during objections feels dismissive and increases resistance. INSTEAD: Isolate the objection. Acknowledge it. Address it directly. Confirm the concern is resolved before continuing. SOURCE: SPIN Selling objection handling. EVIDENCE: B

39. **Ignoring the Buyer's Journey** — Operating on the sales process timeline rather than the buyer's decision timeline. WHY BAD: Buyers are 60-70% through their decision before engaging sales (Gartner). Trying to force them through YOUR stages when they're on THEIR timeline creates friction. INSTEAD: Map your sales process to the buyer's journey. Meet them where they are, not where you want them to be. SOURCE: Gartner B2B buying research. EVIDENCE: A

40. **Failure to Document Mutual Action Plans** — No shared document tracking agreed next steps, timelines, and responsibilities. WHY BAD: Without a mutual action plan, deals drift. Both sides forget commitments. Momentum dies. No accountability mechanism exists. INSTEAD: Create a shared document (mutual action plan) with every committed prospect. Track next steps, owners, and dates collaboratively. SOURCE: Enterprise sales best practices. EVIDENCE: B

41. **Gatekeeping Information** — Withholding pricing, case studies, or technical details to "maintain leverage." WHY BAD: In the age of internet research, information asymmetry as a sales strategy is dead. Withholding creates frustration and signals distrust. Buyers find the information elsewhere and resent you for making it hard. INSTEAD: Be transparent. Share information freely. Your advantage is expertise and insight, not information control. SOURCE: Buyer behavior research; consultative selling. EVIDENCE: B

42. **Quarterly Hockey Stick Behavior** — Doing minimal activity in months 1-2 then panicking in month 3 of the quarter. WHY BAD: Pipeline is built over months. Activity compression into month 3 produces desperate behavior (heavy discounting, pushiness) that undermines deal quality. INSTEAD: Maintain consistent daily/weekly activity levels. Build pipeline continuously. Plan quarters backward from target. SOURCE: Sales productivity research. EVIDENCE: B

43. **Not Preparing for Calls** — Entering calls without researching the prospect, their company, or their likely challenges. WHY BAD: 42% of reps feel they lack sufficient pre-call information. Unprepared reps ask questions already answered on the company website, wasting the prospect's time and destroying credibility. INSTEAD: Spend 5-10 minutes minimum researching before every call: recent news, LinkedIn activity, company website, relevant case studies. SOURCE: Cognism cold calling research. EVIDENCE: A

44. **Discounting Time Investment** — Not accounting for the full cost of sales activities (prep, travel, meetings, proposals) when qualifying deals. WHY BAD: A deal requiring 40 hours of sales time at a 25% close rate effectively costs 160 hours per win. Without this math, reps pursue deals that can't justify the investment. INSTEAD: Calculate cost-per-deal-hour. Set minimum deal size thresholds that justify the sales investment required. SOURCE: Sales economics research. EVIDENCE: B

45. **Ignoring Champion Development** — Finding an internal champion but not equipping them to sell internally. WHY BAD: Champions want to help but can't articulate your value proposition in their internal meetings. Without enablement materials, they go in unarmed. INSTEAD: Create "champion kits" with internal business cases, ROI calculators, and executive summaries they can share. SOURCE: MEDDIC champion methodology; Gartner. EVIDENCE: B

46. **Binary Win/Loss Thinking** — Treating every deal outcome as just "won" or "lost" without analysis. WHY BAD: Without win/loss analysis, you can't learn or improve. You don't know if losses were due to price, product, timing, competition, or sales execution. INSTEAD: Conduct formal win/loss analysis on every significant deal. Interview the buyer. Identify patterns and systemic issues. SOURCE: Win/loss analysis best practices; Clozd research. EVIDENCE: B

47. **Over-Reliance on Product Demo** — Using the demo as the primary selling tool regardless of deal stage or buyer type. WHY BAD: Demos without discovery context are feature dumps. Executive buyers don't want to see every button. Premature demos waste time and miss the mark. INSTEAD: Demo only after discovery. Tailor demo to discovered needs. For executives, focus on business outcomes; for users, focus on workflow. SOURCE: Demo2Win; enterprise sales methodology. EVIDENCE: B

48. **Burning Bridges After Lost Deals** — Not following up professionally after losing a deal. WHY BAD: Today's lost deal is tomorrow's opportunity. The winning vendor may fail. The champion may move companies. Unprofessional exits eliminate future chances. INSTEAD: Congratulate their choice. Stay in touch. Provide value without expectation. SOURCE: Relationship selling research. EVIDENCE: B

49. **Selling Based on Relationship Alone** — Relying on personal relationships without delivering business value. WHY BAD: The Challenger research shows "Relationship Builders" are the lowest-performing sales profile. Relationships without insight and value don't drive complex B2B decisions. INSTEAD: Lead with insight and value. Relationships deepen through competence and trust, not friendships. SOURCE: Dixon & Adamson, "The Challenger Sale" (6,000 rep study). EVIDENCE: A

50. **Ignoring Competitor Wins Data** — Not tracking why competitors win or what they're doing differently. WHY BAD: Without competitive intelligence, you can't adjust strategy, positioning, or messaging. You keep losing for the same reasons. INSTEAD: Track competitive wins/losses systematically. Adjust talk tracks, positioning, and product feedback based on patterns. SOURCE: Competitive intelligence best practices; win/loss analysis. EVIDENCE: B

## 50 BEST PRACTICES

1. **SPIN Question Framework** — Structure discovery around Situation, Problem, Implication, and Need-Payoff questions. WHY: Rackham's 12-year study of 35,000 sales calls showed top performers ask far more Problem and Implication questions. This approach is the most empirically validated sales methodology in existence. SOURCE: Neil Rackham, "SPIN Selling." EVIDENCE: A

2. **Challenger Selling Approach** — Teach prospects something new, tailor the message, and take control of the conversation. WHY: Dixon & Adamson's study of 6,000 sales reps found the Challenger profile significantly outperforms all others, especially in complex sales. Relationship Builders performed worst. SOURCE: Dixon & Adamson, "The Challenger Sale." EVIDENCE: A

3. **Multi-Threading Into Accounts** — Build relationships with 3+ stakeholders in every deal. WHY: Gartner research shows 6-10 decision-makers in B2B buying groups. Single-threaded deals die when champions leave. Multi-threaded deals are 3x more likely to close. SOURCE: Gartner B2B buying research; MEDDIC. EVIDENCE: A

4. **MEDDIC Qualification** — Systematically qualify using Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion. WHY: Provides objective deal health assessment. Prevents hope-based forecasting. Forces reps to verify critical elements before investing time. SOURCE: MEDDIC methodology; PTC/Salesforce adoption data. EVIDENCE: A

5. **60/40 Listen-to-Talk Ratio** — Aim for the prospect to talk 60% of the time during discovery calls. WHY: Gong.io data from millions of calls shows top performers maintain a 43:57 talk-to-listen ratio. More listening = more information = better-tailored solutions = higher close rates. SOURCE: Gong.io conversation intelligence. EVIDENCE: A

6. **Value-Based Selling** — Quantify the business value of your solution before discussing price. WHY: When prospects understand the ROI (e.g., "$500K savings per year"), a $100K price tag feels reasonable. Without value context, any price feels expensive. SOURCE: Value selling research; ROI selling methodology. EVIDENCE: B

7. **Consultative Problem Diagnosis** — Position yourself as a trusted advisor who diagnoses problems before prescribing solutions. WHY: HubSpot research shows 69% of buyers want reps who listen to their needs, 61% want relevant information. Consultative approaches yield 25-40% higher repeat business than high-pressure. SOURCE: HubSpot "Buyers Speak Out"; consultative selling research. EVIDENCE: A

8. **Quality Over Quantity Prospecting** — 10 researched, personalized outreach attempts over 50 generic ones. WHY: Top-performing SDRs (6-10% success rate) vs. average (2-3%) differ primarily in targeting precision and personalization quality. Research investment has 3x+ return on conversion. SOURCE: Cognism 2025 cold calling report; ZoomInfo. EVIDENCE: A

9. **Mutual Action Plans** — Create shared documents with agreed next steps, timelines, and responsibilities for both sides. WHY: Mutual action plans create accountability, maintain momentum, and surface blockers early. They transform a sales process into a collaborative buying process. SOURCE: Enterprise sales best practices. EVIDENCE: B

10. **Win/Loss Analysis** — Conduct formal post-deal analysis on every significant opportunity, win or lose. WHY: Win/loss analysis reveals patterns invisible in individual deals. Product gaps, pricing issues, competitive weaknesses, and sales execution problems emerge from aggregate data. SOURCE: Clozd win/loss research. EVIDENCE: B

11. **Referral-Based Prospecting** — Systematically ask satisfied customers for warm introductions. WHY: Referred leads convert 3-5x higher than cold outreach and have 16% higher CLV. Yet most reps never ask. It's the highest-ROI prospecting activity. SOURCE: Nielsen trust research; referral marketing data. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Pre-Call Research (5-10 Min Minimum)** — Research every prospect before outreach: company news, LinkedIn activity, likely challenges. WHY: 42% of reps lack sufficient pre-call info. Prepared reps demonstrate respect for the prospect's time and ask more insightful questions. SOURCE: Cognism cold calling research. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Behavioral Lead Scoring** — Prioritize leads by engagement behavior, not just demographic fit. WHY: A prospect who visited pricing 3x and downloaded a case study is more ready than a perfect-fit company with no engagement. Behavioral signals indicate intent. SOURCE: HubSpot; Marketo lead scoring research. EVIDENCE: B

14. **Structured Sales Handoff to CS** — Formal, documented transition from sales to customer success/implementation. WHY: Poor handoffs are the #1 driver of early churn. Information loss means customers repeat everything. Expectations set in sales are unknown to CS. Structured handoffs prevent this. SOURCE: Gainsight customer success research. EVIDENCE: B

15. **Champion Enablement** — Equip internal champions with materials to sell on your behalf (business cases, ROI calculators, executive summaries). WHY: Champions want to help but can't articulate your value in their internal meetings without tools. Enabled champions close deals faster and at higher rates. SOURCE: MEDDIC champion methodology; Gartner. EVIDENCE: B

16. **Expansion Revenue Focus** — Proactively identify and pursue expansion opportunities with existing customers. WHY: Existing customers convert at 60-70% vs. 5-20% for new prospects. Expansion CAC is a fraction of new acquisition. It's the most capital-efficient growth lever. SOURCE: Marketing Metrics; Gainsight expansion data. EVIDENCE: A

17. **Consistent Daily Activity** — Maintain steady prospecting and pipeline-building activity throughout the quarter, not just month 3. WHY: Pipeline built in month 1 closes in month 3. Consistent activity prevents quarterly hockey sticks and desperate discounting. SOURCE: Sales productivity research. EVIDENCE: B

18. **Cost-of-Inaction Selling** — Quantify what it costs the prospect to maintain the status quo. WHY: 60% of qualified deals end in "no decision" (Challenger research). The real competitor is inaction. Making the cost of doing nothing concrete creates urgency without artificial pressure. SOURCE: Dixon & Adamson, "The Challenger Sale." EVIDENCE: A

19. **Objections as Buying Signals** — Treat objections as indicators of engagement, not rejection. WHY: A prospect who objects is thinking seriously about your solution. Indifference is worse than objections. Skilled objection handling deepens understanding and builds trust. SOURCE: Rackham objection handling research. EVIDENCE: B

20. **Competitive Positioning Without FUD** — Address competitors factually, respectfully, and by reframing evaluation criteria. WHY: FUD destroys YOUR credibility, not theirs. Prospects verify claims. Confident, factual competitive positioning builds trust and shifts the buying criteria to your strengths. SOURCE: Challenger Sale reframing; trust-based selling. EVIDENCE: B

SOURCE: Sales economics research. EVIDENCE: B

Generic demos feel irrelevant. Tailored demos create "that's exactly what I need" moments. SOURCE: Challenger Sale; Demo2Win methodology. EVIDENCE: B

23. **Mapping the Buying Committee** — Identify all stakeholders (economic buyer, technical evaluator, end users, legal, procurement) early. Value-added follow-ups build credibility and demonstrate ongoing understanding of their needs. SOURCE: Gong.io follow-up research; sales development best practices. EVIDENCE: B

WHY: Deals don't die in sales — they die in procurement. Knowing the process allows you to proactively prepare materials, references, and responses for each gate. SOURCE: MEDDIC methodology. SOURCE: Cognism 2025 outreach data. EVIDENCE: A

Prospects value salespeople who help them see their business differently. Product pitches are commoditized. SOURCE: Dixon & Adamson, "The Challenger Sale." EVIDENCE: A

## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS ABOUT SALES

1. **"Cold calling is dead"** — LLMs often declare cold calling obsolete. Data shows 4.82% average success rate in 2024, with top performers at 6-10%+. Cold calling works when done with research and quality. It's not dead; bad cold calling is dead.

2. **"The Challenger Sale replaced all other methodologies"** — LLMs over-recommend the Challenger approach universally. Challenger is optimized for complex B2B sales. Transactional sales, SMB, and certain cultures require different approaches. SPIN, Solution Selling, and Sandler all have valid applications.

3. **"Relationship selling doesn't work"** — LLMs misinterpret the Challenger research to mean relationships don't matter. The research showed the "Relationship Builder" PROFILE performed worst, not that relationships don't matter. Strong relationships built on competence and insight are essential.

4. **"AI will replace salespeople"** — LLMs overstate AI's role in replacing the sales function. Research shows AI augments discovery, research, and admin but cannot replace the human judgment, empathy, and adaptability required in complex B2B sales conversations.

5. **"Always ask open-ended questions"** — LLMs recommend open-ended questions universally. Rackham's research shows the mix matters: Situation questions (closed) establish context; Problem questions (open) explore pain; Implication questions (open) deepen urgency. Over-reliance on open questions without structure is aimless.

6. **"Discounting shows flexibility"** — LLMs sometimes frame discounting positively as "flexibility." Research shows a 10% discount on 40% margins requires 33% volume increase to break even. Discounting trains buyers to expect it and devalues the brand.

7. **"More features = easier to sell"** — LLMs correlate feature richness with sales success. The opposite is often true — feature complexity creates decision paralysis. The Paradox of Choice (Schwartz) shows too many options reduce purchase likelihood.

8. **"Send the proposal to move the deal forward"** — LLMs recommend sending proposals as a deal-advancement tactic. Without verbal alignment, proposals get ignored or shopped. The proposal should CONFIRM agreement, not create it.

9. **"Social selling means posting on LinkedIn"** — LLMs reduce social selling to content posting. Real social selling is using social networks for prospect research, warm introductions, relationship nurturing, and trigger event monitoring — not just broadcasting.

10. **"Objection handling is about having the right response"** — LLMs provide scripted objection responses. Effective objection handling requires understanding the underlying concern, which varies by context. A memorized response to "it's too expensive" misses whether the real issue is budget, value perception, or timing.
