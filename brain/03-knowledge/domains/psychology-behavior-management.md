# Behavioral Psychology: Evidence-Based Practices

> **Anti-Stupidity Shield**: This document counters pop-psychology myths rampant in LLM training data.
> Evidence ratings: **A** = meta-analysis/systematic review, **B** = multiple RCTs/replications, **C** = single well-designed study or expert consensus.
> Last updated: 2026-03-19

---

## 50 BAD PRACTICES (Myths & Misconceptions)

1. **NLP (Neuro-Linguistic Programming)** — Claims that eye movements reveal lying, "anchoring" emotions, and "reframing" as therapeutic technique. WHY BAD: Over 80% of NLP research cannot be reproduced; of 33 relevant papers, 54.5% were non-supportive, only 27.3% supportive (Witkowski, 2010). Multiple literature reviews and meta-analyses failed to show evidence for NLP's assumptions. INSTEAD: Use CBT or ACT, which have robust meta-analytic support. SOURCE: Witkowski (2010) "Thirty-Five Years of Research on NLP"; Passmore & Rowson (2019) systematic review. EVIDENCE: A

2. **Learning Styles (Visual/Auditory/Kinesthetic)** — Teaching should match individual "learning styles" for optimal outcomes. WHY BAD: Pashler et al. (2008) found no credible evidence for the matching hypothesis. Across four meta-analyses the average effect size is d = 0.04 (essentially zero). INSTEAD: Use evidence-based learning strategies: retrieval practice, spaced repetition, interleaving, elaborative interrogation. SOURCE: Pashler et al. (2008) Psychological Science in the Public Interest; PMC meta-analysis (2024). EVIDENCE: A

3. **10,000-Hour Rule** — 10,000 hours of practice guarantees expertise. WHY BAD: Gladwell oversimplified Ericsson's deliberate practice research. Macnamara et al. (2014) meta-analysis of 88 studies found deliberate practice explained only 26% variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports. INSTEAD: Focus on deliberate practice quality (feedback, stretch goals, expert coaching), not raw hours. Genetics, starting age, and opportunity all matter. SOURCE: Macnamara et al. (2014) Psychological Science; Ericsson (1993). EVIDENCE: A

4. **Maslow's Hierarchy as Rigid Pyramid** — Needs must be satisfied sequentially from bottom to top. WHY BAD: Maslow himself never drew a pyramid. Wahba & Bridwell (1976) found little empirical support for strict hierarchy. Tay & Diener (2011) cross-cultural study showed people in poverty pursue higher-order needs. Western, individualistic cultural bias in original sample. INSTEAD: Treat needs as concurrent and culturally variable. Use self-determination theory (autonomy, competence, relatedness). SOURCE: Wahba & Bridwell (1976); Tay & Diener (2011). EVIDENCE: B

5. **Left Brain / Right Brain Personality Types** — People are dominantly "left-brained" (logical) or "right-brained" (creative). WHY BAD: University of Utah (2013) study of 1,000+ brain scans across 7,000 regions found no evidence of hemispheric dominance in individuals. Both hemispheres work in tandem during creative and quantitative tasks. INSTEAD: Understand that lateralization exists for specific functions (language tends left, spatial tends right) but people are not "types." SOURCE: Nielsen et al. (2013) PLOS ONE. EVIDENCE: B

6. **Lie Detection via Body Language** — You can reliably detect lies through nonverbal cues (avoiding eye contact, fidgeting, etc.). WHY BAD: DePaulo & Bond meta-analysis of 206 studies (24,000+ observers): accuracy barely above chance (~54%). Micro-expression training produced below-chance accuracy. No single behavior is reliably indicative of deception. INSTEAD: Use evidence-based investigative interviewing (PEACE model, cognitive interview technique). SOURCE: DePaulo & Bond (2006); Vrij et al. (2021) PMC. EVIDENCE: A

7. **Catharsis Theory (Venting Anger Helps)** — Expressing anger through punching bags, screaming, etc. releases tension. WHY BAD: Bushman (2002) found venting increases anger and aggression. Rumination group was most aggressive. Doing nothing was more effective than venting. Replicated across multiple studies since Hornberger (1959). INSTEAD: Use cognitive reappraisal, distraction, or mindfulness-based emotion regulation. SOURCE: Bushman (2002) PSPB. EVIDENCE: B

8. **Power Posing** — Adopting "power poses" for 2 minutes increases testosterone and risk-taking. WHY BAD: Ranehill et al. (2015) larger replication found no physiological or behavioral effects. Simmons & Simonsohn (2016) showed original findings disappeared after correcting for selective reporting. Co-author Dana Carney publicly abandoned the theory in 2016. INSTEAD: Focus on genuine competence-building and cognitive preparation techniques. SOURCE: Ranehill et al. (2015); Simmons & Simonsohn (2016) Data Colada. EVIDENCE: B

9. **Marshmallow Test as Destiny** — A child's ability to delay gratification at age 4 predicts life success. WHY BAD: Watts et al. (2018) replication with 900+ children found effect was half the original size and reduced by two-thirds when controlling for socioeconomic factors. Sperber et al. (2024) found marshmallow test does not reliably predict adult functioning. INSTEAD: Address socioeconomic inequality; recognize environmental factors in self-regulation development. SOURCE: Watts et al. (2018) Psychological Science; Sperber et al. (2024) Child Development. EVIDENCE: B

10. **Stanford Prison Experiment as Valid Science** — Demonstrates that anyone becomes evil in the right situation. WHY BAD: Le Texier (2018-2019) revealed guards were coached to be tough, Zimbardo designed conclusions in advance, study was not peer-reviewed, and participants were not fully immersed. Not a controlled experiment but a demonstration with demand characteristics. INSTEAD: Understand situational factors through methodologically sound research; reference Reicher & Haslam's BBC Prison Study. SOURCE: Le Texier (2019) American Psychologist. EVIDENCE: B

11. **Milgram's Obedience as Universal Law** — 65% of people will obey authority to the point of killing. WHY BAD: Lacked standardization; "prods" (prompts) were central but unstudied; participants may not have believed the cover story; coaching and deception issues. Reicher & Haslam reinterpret as "engaged followership" not blind obedience. INSTEAD: Understand obedience as context-dependent, influenced by identification with authority's mission. SOURCE: Kaposi (2022) Social and Personality Psychology Compass. EVIDENCE: B

12. **"Chemical Imbalance" Model of Depression** — Depression is caused by low serotonin that antidepressants correct. WHY BAD: Moncrieff et al. (2022) umbrella review in Molecular Psychiatry found no convincing evidence depression is caused by serotonin abnormalities. The model oversimplifies a complex condition with biological, psychological, and social factors. INSTEAD: Use biopsychosocial model; recognize antidepressants may work through mechanisms other than "correcting" imbalances. SOURCE: Moncrieff et al. (2022) Molecular Psychiatry. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Dunning-Kruger Effect (Popular Version)** — Stupid people are too stupid to know they're stupid (the "Mount Stupid" graph). WHY BAD: The popular graph is not from the original paper. Gignac & Zajenkowski (2020) showed the effect is mostly a statistical artifact of autocorrelation and regression to the mean. Random data produces the same pattern. Actual relationship between ability and self-assessment is approximately linear. INSTEAD: Recognize that everyone has calibration problems; experts underestimate themselves, novices overestimate, but the effect is far more modest than depicted. SOURCE: Gignac & Zajenkowski (2020) Intelligence; Fix (2022). EVIDENCE: B

14. **Myers-Briggs (MBTI) as Personality Science** — 16 types reliably describe personality and predict outcomes. WHY BAD: ~50% of people get a different type when retested after 5 weeks. No predictive validity for job performance, career success, or relationship satisfaction. Not used in serious personality research. INSTEAD: Use Big Five (OCEAN) personality model, which has robust predictive validity and test-retest reliability. SOURCE: Pittenger (2005); Grant (2013) critique. EVIDENCE: B

15. **Positive Thinking Without Action** — Simply visualizing success leads to achievement. WHY BAD: Oettingen's research found positive fantasies about the future actually sap energy and reduce motivation. People who only visualize success are less likely to achieve goals. INSTEAD: Use mental contrasting (WOOP: Wish, Outcome, Obstacle, Plan) which combines positive visualization with obstacle identification and if-then planning. SOURCE: Oettingen (2014) "Rethinking Positive Thinking." EVIDENCE: B

16. **Ego Depletion / Willpower as Finite Resource** — Self-control is a muscle that gets depleted with use. WHY BAD: Hagger et al. pre-registered 23-lab replication found no ego depletion effect. Second 36-lab mega-study (3,531 participants) found data four times more likely under null hypothesis. INSTEAD: Understand self-control as influenced by motivation, beliefs, and context rather than a depletable resource. SOURCE: Hagger et al. (2016) multilab replication; Vohs et al. (2021). EVIDENCE: A

17. **Stereotype Threat as Large, Robust Effect** — Reminding people of stereotypes dramatically impairs performance. WHY BAD: Flore & Wicherts (2015) meta-analysis found small effect (d = .22) that drops to d = .07 (non-significant) after correcting for publication bias. Schimmack (2019) estimates bias inflated supportive studies from 14% to 84%. Effect disappears in realistic settings. INSTEAD: Address systemic inequality directly rather than focusing on threat priming in lab settings. SOURCE: Flore & Wicherts (2015); Schimmack (2019) Replicability Index. EVIDENCE: A

18. **Bystander Effect as Universal Apathy** — More bystanders = less help (everyone freezes). WHY BAD: Philpot et al. (2019) analyzed 219 real-world CCTV incidents across three countries: bystanders intervened in over 90% of conflicts. More bystanders actually increased likelihood of at least one person intervening. INSTEAD: Understand that the original lab paradigm (ambiguous emergencies) doesn't generalize well to real-world violent incidents. SOURCE: Philpot et al. (2019) American Psychologist. EVIDENCE: B

19. **Group Brainstorming Superiority** — Groups generate more and better ideas than individuals working alone. WHY BAD: Diehl & Stroebe (1987) and subsequent studies consistently show individuals produce more ideas (both quantity and quality) than interactive groups. Main cause: production blocking (waiting for turns). INSTEAD: Use brainwriting, nominal group technique, or hybrid approaches (individual ideation followed by group discussion). SOURCE: Diehl & Stroebe (1987); Stroebe & Diehl (1992). EVIDENCE: A

20. **Growth Mindset Interventions as Transformative** — Teaching students that intelligence is malleable dramatically improves achievement. WHY BAD: Sisk et al. (2018) meta-analysis found average effect d = 0.08 (50th to 53rd percentile). Macnamara & Burgoyne (2022) found d = 0.05, non-significant after publication bias correction. Li & Bates (2019) failed to replicate the foundational Mueller & Dweck (1998) study. INSTEAD: Growth mindset may have modest benefits for low-achieving/low-SES students specifically, but is not a universal intervention. Focus on effective teaching practices. SOURCE: Sisk et al. (2018); Macnamara & Burgoyne (2022). EVIDENCE: A

21. **Emotional Intelligence (EQ) as Clear Construct** — EQ is a well-defined intelligence that predicts success better than IQ. WHY BAD: Multiple competing definitions and measurement tools with poor convergent validity. Ability-based measures (MSCEIT) show no correlation with leadership effectiveness in some studies. Self-report measures overlap substantially with Big Five personality traits. Consensus scoring means only majority-endorsed answers count as "intelligent." INSTEAD: Measure specific emotional competencies (emotion recognition, regulation) rather than a single "EQ" score. SOURCE: Frontiers in Psychology (2019) systematic review. EVIDENCE: B

22. **"We Only Use 10% of Our Brain"** — Vast untapped potential exists in unused brain regions. WHY BAD: Neuroimaging shows all brain areas have known functions and are active over the course of a day. Damage to any area causes deficits. The myth likely arose from misquoting early neuroscience. INSTEAD: Understand that cognitive improvement comes from better strategies, not "unlocking" unused capacity. SOURCE: Beyerstein (1999); multiple neuroimaging studies. EVIDENCE: B

23. **Subliminal Advertising Works** — Hidden messages in media control behavior. WHY BAD: Vicary's 1957 "Drink Coca-Cola" study was fabricated (he later admitted it). Meta-analyses show subliminal priming effects are tiny, short-lived, and don't influence complex purchasing decisions. INSTEAD: Understand that conscious persuasion techniques (framing, anchoring) are far more powerful. SOURCE: Pratkanis (1992); Greenwald et al. (1991). EVIDENCE: A

24. **Mozart Effect** — Listening to Mozart makes you smarter. WHY BAD: Rauscher et al. (1993) found a tiny, temporary spatial reasoning boost lasting ~15 minutes. Chabris (1999) meta-analysis found effect was small (d = 0.09) and not specific to Mozart. INSTEAD: Active music training (learning an instrument) does show cognitive benefits. SOURCE: Chabris (1999) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

25. **Repressed Memories** — Traumatic memories are buried in the unconscious and can be "recovered" in therapy. WHY BAD: The "memory wars" of the 1990s showed that recovered memory therapy often creates false memories. McNally (2003) review found no convincing evidence for the Freudian repression mechanism. Memory is reconstructive, not a recording. INSTEAD: Use trauma-focused CBT or EMDR (which does not require "recovering" memories). SOURCE: McNally (2003); Loftus (2005). EVIDENCE: B

26. **Polygraph / Lie Detector as Reliable** — Polygraphs accurately detect deception. WHY BAD: National Academy of Sciences (2003) concluded polygraph accuracy is "inherently ambiguous" and far from perfect. High false-positive rates. Measures arousal, not lying. Easily beaten by countermeasures. INSTEAD: Use evidence-based investigative interviewing, cognitive load techniques. SOURCE: NAS (2003) "The Polygraph and Lie Detection." EVIDENCE: A

27. **Grief Follows Five Stages (Kubler-Ross)** — Everyone goes through denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance in order. WHY BAD: Kubler-Ross developed stages for people facing their own death, not bereavement. No empirical evidence for sequential stages. Grief is highly individual and non-linear. Bonanno (2009) found most people are resilient, not devastated. INSTEAD: Use the dual-process model of bereavement (Stroebe & Schut) which accounts for oscillation between loss-oriented and restoration-oriented coping. SOURCE: Bonanno (2009); Stroebe & Schut (1999). EVIDENCE: B

28. **Addiction as Moral Failure or Pure Brain Disease** — Addiction is either a choice (moral model) or a hijacked brain (disease model). WHY BAD: Both models are oversimplified. The brain disease model (Leshner, 1997) ignores social, environmental, and developmental factors. The moral model ignores neurobiology. Most people recover without treatment, often by "maturing out." INSTEAD: Use biopsychosocial model; recognize that addiction exists on a spectrum influenced by genetics, environment, development, and social factors. SOURCE: Heather (2017); Lewis (2015) "The Biology of Desire." EVIDENCE: B

29. **"Dopamine = Pleasure Chemical"** — Dopamine is the brain's pleasure molecule. WHY BAD: Dopamine is primarily about motivation, anticipation, and salience, not pleasure itself. Berridge's research distinguishes "wanting" (dopamine) from "liking" (opioid system). INSTEAD: Understand dopamine as a learning/prediction signal that drives approach behavior, not a simple pleasure chemical. SOURCE: Berridge & Robinson (1998); Schultz (2007). EVIDENCE: B

30. **Full Moon Effects on Behavior** — Full moons cause increased crime, ER visits, and strange behavior ("lunacy"). WHY BAD: Rotton & Kelly (1985) meta-analysis of 37 studies found no relationship between lunar phases and human behavior. Effect size accounted for less than 1% of variance. INSTEAD: Attribute apparent patterns to confirmation bias and illusory correlation. SOURCE: Rotton & Kelly (1985) Psychological Bulletin. EVIDENCE: A

31. **Sugar Causes Hyperactivity in Children** — Sugar makes kids "hyper." WHY BAD: Wolraich et al. (1995) meta-analysis of 16 controlled studies found sugar does not affect children's behavior or cognitive performance. Parent expectations create perceived effects (blinded studies show no difference). INSTEAD: Monitor overall nutrition quality; recognize that excitement at events where sugar is present may be the actual cause. SOURCE: Wolraich et al. (1995) JAMA. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Opposites Attract** — Relationship compatibility comes from complementary personalities. WHY BAD: Montoya et al. (2008) meta-analysis found similarity is a far stronger predictor of attraction than complementarity. People prefer partners who share their values, attitudes, and personality traits. INSTEAD: Focus on similarity in core values, attachment style compatibility, and communication skills. SOURCE: Montoya et al. (2008). EVIDENCE: A

33. **Priming Effects as Large and Robust** — Subtle cues (e.g., words about aging make you walk slower) powerfully influence behavior. WHY BAD: Social priming has been a major casualty of the replication crisis. Doyen et al. (2012) failed to replicate the classic Bargh elderly walking study. Many flagship priming studies have failed replication. INSTEAD: Recognize that context matters but effects are likely much smaller than originally claimed, if they exist at all. SOURCE: Doyen et al. (2012); Open Science Collaboration (2015). EVIDENCE: B

34. **Criminal Profiling as Science** — FBI-style profiling accurately predicts offender characteristics. WHY BAD: Snook et al. (2007) found profilers performed no better than untrained individuals. Profiling lacks empirical validation and relies on clinical intuition vulnerable to cognitive biases. INSTEAD: Use actuarial risk assessment tools with demonstrated predictive validity. SOURCE: Snook et al. (2007) Criminal Justice and Behavior. EVIDENCE: B

35. **Rorschach Inkblot Test as Diagnostic Tool** — Rorschach reliably diagnoses personality disorders and mental illness. WHY BAD: Wood et al. (2003) comprehensive review found most Rorschach scores lack adequate validity. Only a few variables show modest validity, and those overlap with self-report measures. INSTEAD: Use empirically validated assessment instruments (MMPI-2, structured clinical interviews). SOURCE: Wood et al. (2003) "What's Wrong with the Rorschach?" EVIDENCE: B

36. **"Emotional Reasoning" as Valid Decision-Making** — Trusting your gut feelings is as good as analytical thinking for complex decisions. WHY BAD: Dijksterhuis' "unconscious thought theory" (sleep-on-it decisions are better) failed multiple replication attempts. Gut feelings work for domain experts with feedback-rich environments, not general decisions. INSTEAD: Use structured decision-making for complex choices; reserve intuition for domains where you have genuine expertise with rapid feedback. SOURCE: Nieuwenstein et al. (2015) replication failure. EVIDENCE: B

37. **Hypnosis as Mind Control** — Hypnosis can make people do things against their will or recover accurate memories. WHY BAD: Hypnosis cannot override free will. "Recovered" memories under hypnosis are unreliable and often confabulated. Hypnotic suggestibility varies widely across individuals. INSTEAD: Hypnosis has legitimate uses as an adjunct for pain management and some anxiety conditions, but not for memory recovery. SOURCE: APA Division 30 position statement; Lynn et al. (2015). EVIDENCE: B

38. **"Blank Slate" / Tabula Rasa** — Human behavior is entirely shaped by environment with no innate component. WHY BAD: Twin studies consistently show substantial genetic contributions to personality (~40-60%), intelligence (~50-80%), and many behavioral traits. Gene-environment interactions are the rule. INSTEAD: Use interactionist framework: genes influence dispositions, environments shape expression. SOURCE: Polderman et al. (2015) Nature Genetics meta-analysis of all twin studies. EVIDENCE: A

39. **Oxytocin as "Love Hormone"** — Oxytocin simply promotes bonding and trust. WHY BAD: Nave et al. (2015) failed to replicate the intranasal oxytocin trust effect. Oxytocin also increases in-group favoritism, envy, and aggression toward out-groups. Effects are highly context-dependent. INSTEAD: Understand oxytocin as a social salience modulator, not a simple prosocial chemical. SOURCE: Nave et al. (2015); Shamay-Tsoory & Abu-Akel (2016). EVIDENCE: B

40. **"Internet Addiction" as Clinical Disorder** — Internet use is addictive in the same way as substances. WHY BAD: Conceptual confusion between compulsive use, problematic use, and addiction proper. Most "internet addiction" research uses non-validated screening tools with arbitrary cutoffs. The underlying problem is usually anxiety, depression, or social isolation. INSTEAD: Assess for underlying mental health conditions; evaluate specific problematic behaviors rather than internet use generally. SOURCE: Kardefelt-Winther (2017) WHO position. EVIDENCE: B

41. **Neuromyth: "Critical Periods" Are Absolute** — After a critical period, the brain cannot learn certain skills. WHY BAD: While sensitive periods exist (especially for language and vision), the brain retains substantial plasticity throughout life. Adult neuroplasticity is well-documented. INSTEAD: Understand sensitive periods as windows of enhanced (not exclusive) learning capacity; adult learning is possible but may require more effort. SOURCE: Kolb & Gibb (2011); Hensch (2005). EVIDENCE: B

42. **"Type A Personality" Causes Heart Disease** — Being competitive and impatient directly causes cardiac problems. WHY BAD: The original Friedman & Rosenman research was partly funded by tobacco companies to deflect from smoking. Meta-analyses show the Type A construct is too broad; only the hostility component has modest predictive value. INSTEAD: Focus on specific risk factors: chronic hostility/anger, lack of social support, depression. SOURCE: Myrtek (2001) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

43. **Birth Order Effects on Personality** — Firstborns are leaders, middle children are peacemakers, youngest are rebels. WHY BAD: Large-scale studies (Rohrer et al., 2015; Damian & Roberts, 2015) with 20,000+ participants found no meaningful birth-order effects on personality. Effects found in within-family designs don't replicate in between-family designs. INSTEAD: Recognize that family dynamics, SES, and individual differences matter far more than ordinal position. SOURCE: Rohrer et al. (2015) PNAS. EVIDENCE: B

44. **Violent Video Games Cause Violence** — Playing violent games leads to real-world aggression. WHY BAD: Effect sizes are very small (r ~ .06-.15) and methodologically questionable. Przybylski & Weinstein (2019) pre-registered study of 1,004 teens found no evidence. Crime rates have dropped as game sales increased. INSTEAD: Focus on known risk factors for violence: poverty, abuse history, substance abuse, access to weapons. SOURCE: Przybylski & Weinstein (2019) Royal Society Open Science. EVIDENCE: B

45. **Facilitated Communication for Autism** — A facilitator can help non-verbal autistic individuals type their thoughts. WHY BAD: Controlled studies consistently show the facilitator, not the person with autism, is the source of the communications. ASHA and APA have position statements against it. INSTEAD: Use evidence-based AAC (augmentative and alternative communication) devices. SOURCE: Mostert (2001); ASHA position statement. EVIDENCE: B

46. **"Grit" as Independent Predictor of Success** — Grit (passion + perseverance) is the key to achievement beyond ability. WHY BAD: Credee et al. (2017) meta-analysis found grit's correlation with performance (rho = .18) was largely due to the perseverance facet, which overlaps with conscientiousness. Grit adds little predictive value beyond Big Five conscientiousness. INSTEAD: Measure conscientiousness directly; focus on structural supports and skill development. SOURCE: Credee et al. (2017) Journal of Personality and Social Psychology. EVIDENCE: A

47. **Freudian Psychoanalysis as Primary Treatment** — Free association, dream analysis, and years-long psychoanalysis are the best treatment for mental illness. WHY BAD: For most conditions, shorter evidence-based treatments (CBT, ACT, DBT) achieve comparable or superior outcomes in 12-20 sessions vs. years. Psychoanalytic concepts like penis envy and Oedipus complex lack empirical support. INSTEAD: Start with evidence-based treatments matched to the condition; psychodynamic therapy has some evidence for specific conditions but not as primary first-line treatment for most. SOURCE: Driessen et al. (2015); Cuijpers et al. (2019). EVIDENCE: A

48. **"Whole Brain Teaching" / Brain Gym** — Specific physical movements activate brain regions and improve learning. WHY BAD: No neuroscience evidence supports Brain Gym claims. Exercises like "cross-crawl" supposedly integrating brain hemispheres lack any mechanism. The program makes pseudoscientific claims about brain function. INSTEAD: Physical exercise in general does support cognitive function and learning, but not through the mechanisms Brain Gym claims. SOURCE: Hyatt (2007) review; Spaulding et al. (2010). EVIDENCE: B

49. **"Peak-End Rule" as Universal Law** — People always judge experiences by their peak and end moments. WHY BAD: While Kahneman's peak-end research is real, it's often overgeneralized. The effect varies by domain, duration, and individual differences. It works best for retrospective evaluations of brief experiences, not for all judgment contexts. INSTEAD: Use peak-end as one heuristic among many; recognize that overall duration and variety also matter in many contexts. SOURCE: Kahneman et al. (1993); limitations reviewed by Ganzach & Karsahi (1995). EVIDENCE: C

50. **"Mirror Neurons" Explain Empathy and Social Cognition** — Mirror neurons are the basis for empathy, language, and civilization. WHY BAD: While mirror neurons exist (discovered in macaques by Rizzolatti), claims about their role in human empathy, autism, and language are vastly overstated. Hickok (2014) critiques the "broken mirror theory of autism" as lacking evidence. INSTEAD: Understand empathy as a multi-component process involving different neural systems; mirror neurons may play a modest role. SOURCE: Hickok (2014) "The Myth of Mirror Neurons." EVIDENCE: B

## 50 BEST PRACTICES (Evidence-Based Behavioral Psychology)

1. **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — Structured, time-limited therapy targeting thoughts, behaviors, and emotions. Large effect sizes for depression (g = 0.74), anxiety (g = 0.77), and many other conditions. Most extensively validated psychotherapy approach. SOURCE: Hofmann et al. (2012) meta-analysis of meta-analyses; Cuijpers et al. (2019). EVIDENCE: A

2. **Implementation Intentions (If-Then Planning)** — Forming specific if-then plans: "If [situation], then I will [action]." Meta-analysis of 94 studies found medium-to-large effect (d = 0.65) on goal attainment. Works by creating automatic situation-response links. SOURCE: Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

3. **Spaced Repetition** — Distributing learning over time rather than massing (cramming). One of the most robust findings in memory research, with effect sizes consistently large (d > 0.5). Works across ages, materials, and skills. SOURCE: Cepeda et al. (2006) meta-analysis; Dunlosky et al. (2013). EVIDENCE: A

4. **Retrieval Practice (Testing Effect)** — Actively recalling information strengthens memory more than re-reading. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) demonstrated long-term retention benefits. Meta-analysis shows robust effect across educational settings. SOURCE: Rowland (2014) meta-analysis; Dunlosky et al. (2013). EVIDENCE: A

5. **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** — Supporting autonomy, competence, and relatedness enhances intrinsic motivation and well-being. Deci & Ryan's framework has strong evidence across education, work, health, and sport. Autonomy-supportive environments produce better outcomes than controlling ones. SOURCE: Ryan & Deci (2000); Gagne & Deci (2005). EVIDENCE: A

6. **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)** — Promotes psychological flexibility through acceptance, mindfulness, and values-based action. Meta-analyses show comparable efficacy to CBT; may be superior for quality-of-life outcomes. SOURCE: Gloster et al. (2020) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

7. **Behavioral Activation for Depression** — Systematically increasing engagement with rewarding activities. Non-inferior to CBT for depression, simpler to deliver, and effective even in severe depression. SOURCE: Dimidjian et al. (2006); Richards et al. (2016) Lancet. EVIDENCE: A

8. **Motivational Interviewing** — Collaborative, non-confrontational approach to resolving ambivalence about behavior change. Meta-analyses show efficacy for substance use, medication adherence, diet/exercise, and gambling. SOURCE: Lundahl et al. (2010) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

9. **Exposure Therapy for Anxiety** — Gradual, systematic exposure to feared stimuli. Gold-standard treatment for phobias, PTSD, OCD, and panic disorder. Effect sizes consistently large. INSTEAD of avoidance or pure relaxation. SOURCE: Abramowitz et al. (2019). EVIDENCE: A

10. **Mental Contrasting (WOOP)** — Combining positive visualization with obstacle identification and if-then planning. Oettingen's research shows this beats pure positive thinking or pure dwelling on obstacles across multiple domains. SOURCE: Oettingen (2014); Cross & Sheffield (2019) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: B

11. **Nudge Architecture (Choice Architecture)** — Designing environments to make desired behaviors easier (defaults, salience, social norms). Evidence-based when applied ethically (transparent, easy to opt-out). Default effects are particularly robust. SOURCE: Thaler & Sunstein (2008); Jachimowicz et al. (2019) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Habit Formation via Cue-Routine-Reward** — Creating automatic behaviors by linking consistent cues to routines with intrinsic rewards. Lally et al. (2010) found habit formation takes 18-254 days (median 66), not the pop-myth "21 days." SOURCE: Lally et al. (2010) European Journal of Social Psychology. EVIDENCE: B

13. **Cognitive Reappraisal** — Reinterpreting the meaning of emotion-eliciting situations. Meta-analyses show it effectively reduces negative emotions and is more adaptive than suppression. Works across cultures and ages. SOURCE: Webb et al. (2012) meta-analysis; Gross (2015). EVIDENCE: A

14. **Growth Through Adversity (Post-Traumatic Growth)** — Recognizing that some people develop new strengths after trauma, while validating the difficulty. Different from toxic positivity: acknowledges suffering while noting genuine positive change in ~50% of trauma survivors. SOURCE: Tedeschi & Calhoun (2004); Helgeson et al. (2006) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: B

15. **Interleaving Practice** — Mixing different topics or skills during practice rather than blocking. Consistently improves learning and transfer, particularly for math and motor skills. SOURCE: Rohrer et al. (2015); Pan (2015) Scientific American. EVIDENCE: B

16. **Elaborative Interrogation** — Asking "why" and "how" questions about material being learned. Generates connections between new and existing knowledge, improving retention. SOURCE: Dunlosky et al. (2013) effectiveness review. EVIDENCE: B

17. **Social Norms Feedback** — Providing individuals with information about what others actually do (descriptive norms) rather than what they should do. Effectively reduces alcohol consumption, energy use, and other behaviors. SOURCE: Schultz et al. (2007); Miller & Prentice (2016). EVIDENCE: A

18. **Operant Conditioning Principles (Applied Behavior Analysis)** — Systematic application of reinforcement and extinction. Gold standard for autism spectrum interventions, with large meta-analytic evidence base. Also effective for organizational behavior management. SOURCE: Virues-Ortega (2010) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

19. **Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)** — 8-week structured program combining meditation, body scanning, and yoga. Meta-analyses show moderate effects on anxiety (d = 0.63), depression (d = 0.59), and psychological distress. SOURCE: Khoury et al. (2013) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

20. **Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) for BPD** — Skills-based therapy combining mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness. Reduces self-harm, suicidality, and hospitalizations in borderline personality disorder. SOURCE: Storeboe et al. (2020) Cochrane review. EVIDENCE: A

21. **Contingency Management** — Providing tangible reinforcers for verified behavior change (e.g., drug-free urine tests). Among the most effective addiction treatments, with large effect sizes. Underutilized due to moral objections to "paying" people. SOURCE: Benishek et al. (2014) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

22. **Desensitization Through Graded Exposure** — Gradual exposure to feared stimuli in a hierarchy from least to most anxiety-provoking. Core component of evidence-based anxiety treatment. SOURCE: Wolpe (1958); numerous meta-analyses. EVIDENCE: A

23. **Commitment Devices** — Pre-committing to a course of action to overcome present bias (e.g., removing junk food from house, automatic savings). Behavioral economics approach with strong evidence. SOURCE: Bryan et al. (2010). EVIDENCE: B

24. **Psychoeducation** — Providing structured education about mental health conditions to patients and families. Meta-analyses show it improves treatment adherence, reduces relapse, and decreases family burden across conditions. SOURCE: Colom et al. (2003); Sin & Norman (2013) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

25. **Functional Behavioral Assessment** — Identifying the function (purpose) of problematic behavior before intervening. Leads to more effective interventions than topography-based approaches. Required by IDEA for students with disabilities. SOURCE: Hanley et al. (2003). EVIDENCE: B

26. **Goal Setting Theory (Locke & Latham)** — Specific, challenging goals with feedback lead to higher performance than vague or easy goals. One of the most replicated findings in organizational psychology (d = 0.42-0.80). SOURCE: Locke & Latham (2002); meta-analysis across 40+ years. EVIDENCE: A

27. **Cognitive Load Management** — Designing instruction and tasks to respect working memory limitations (~4 items). Reducing extraneous load while optimizing germane load improves learning. SOURCE: Sweller et al. (2019) Educational Psychology Review. EVIDENCE: A

28. **Self-Monitoring** — Systematically tracking one's own behavior (e.g., food intake, exercise, mood). Meta-analyses show it is one of the most effective components of behavior change interventions. SOURCE: Michie et al. (2009) meta-analysis of behavior change techniques. EVIDENCE: A

29. **Shaping (Successive Approximation)** — Reinforcing progressively closer approximations to a desired behavior. Core ABA technique with decades of evidence for skill building. SOURCE: Skinner (1953); Cooper et al. (2007). EVIDENCE: B

30. **Perspective-Taking Training** — Structured exercises in seeing situations from others' viewpoints. Improves empathy and reduces prejudice more effectively than mere exposure to diversity. SOURCE: Todd & Galinsky (2014). EVIDENCE: B

31. **Behavioral Experiments in Therapy** — Testing feared predictions through real-world experiments. Core CBT technique that provides experiential disconfirmation of catastrophic beliefs. More effective than pure verbal challenging. SOURCE: McMillan & Lee (2010). EVIDENCE: B

32. **Pre-Commitment and Ulysses Contracts** — Making binding decisions in advance to prevent future impulsive choices. Used effectively in addiction treatment, financial planning, and health behavior. SOURCE: Ariely & Wertenbroch (2002). EVIDENCE: B

33. **Active Learning Strategies** — Engaging learners through problem-solving, discussion, and application rather than passive listening. Freeman et al. (2014) meta-analysis: active learning reduces failure rates by 55% and increases exam performance by 0.47 SD. SOURCE: Freeman et al. (2014) PNAS. EVIDENCE: A

34. **Emotion Labeling (Affect Labeling)** — Simply naming emotions reduces their intensity. fMRI studies show labeling activates prefrontal cortex and reduces amygdala activation. Simple, no-cost intervention. SOURCE: Lieberman et al. (2007). EVIDENCE: B

35. **Behavioral Contracting** — Formal agreements specifying target behaviors, reinforcers, and consequences. Effective in educational, clinical, and organizational settings. SOURCE: O'Banion & Whaley (1981); Kazdin (2012). EVIDENCE: B

36. **Stimulus Control** — Modifying environmental cues to promote desired behaviors and reduce undesired ones (e.g., putting running shoes by the door). Well-established behavior change technique, especially for insomnia and habit change. SOURCE: Bootzin & Epstein (2011) for insomnia. EVIDENCE: A

37. **Parent Management Training** — Teaching parents consistent reinforcement, clear commands, and appropriate consequences. Most effective treatment for childhood conduct problems, with large effect sizes. SOURCE: Dretzke et al. (2009) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

38. **Relapse Prevention (Marlatt Model)** — Identifying high-risk situations and developing coping strategies before relapse occurs. Effective for substance use, eating disorders, and other behavioral problems. SOURCE: Irvin et al. (1999) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

39. **Values Clarification Exercises** — Identifying and articulating personal values to guide behavior. Core ACT component; self-affirmation interventions based on values reduce defensiveness and improve health behaviors. SOURCE: Cohen & Sherman (2014). EVIDENCE: B

40. **Time-Based Prospective Memory Cues** — Using external reminders (timers, alarms, calendar alerts) rather than relying on internal memory for future intentions. Dramatically improves follow-through on health behaviors and medication adherence. SOURCE: Zogg et al. (2012). EVIDENCE: B

41. **Behavioral Momentum** — Starting with easy, high-compliance requests before more difficult ones. Well-documented in ABA and educational psychology. Builds a "momentum" of compliance. SOURCE: Nevin & Grace (2000). EVIDENCE: B

42. **Extinction with Reinforcement of Alternatives (DRA)** — Ignoring unwanted behavior while reinforcing a desired alternative. More effective and ethical than punishment alone. Standard ABA practice. SOURCE: Petscher et al. (2009) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

43. **Cognitive Defusion (ACT Technique)** — Distancing from unhelpful thoughts without trying to change their content (e.g., "I'm having the thought that..."). Reduces the behavioral impact of negative thoughts without the struggle of thought suppression. SOURCE: Levin et al. (2012) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: B

44. **Prompt-Fade Strategies** — Providing guidance that is systematically reduced as competence develops. Prevents prompt dependence while building independent skills. SOURCE: MacDuff et al. (2001). EVIDENCE: B

45. **Self-Compassion Practices (Neff)** — Treating oneself with kindness during failure rather than self-criticism. Meta-analyses show associations with lower anxiety, depression, and stress, and greater well-being and resilience. Not the same as self-esteem (which can be fragile). SOURCE: Zessin et al. (2015) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

46. **Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA)** — Collecting data in real-time in natural environments rather than relying on retrospective self-report. Reduces recall bias and provides more accurate behavioral data. SOURCE: Shiffman et al. (2008). EVIDENCE: B

47. **Premack Principle** — Using a high-frequency behavior as a reinforcer for a low-frequency behavior ("First eat vegetables, then dessert"). Simple, effective, and well-documented across settings. SOURCE: Premack (1959); Konarski et al. (1987). EVIDENCE: B

48. **Systematic Desensitization** — Pairing relaxation with gradually more anxiety-provoking stimuli. Classic behavioral technique with strong evidence for specific phobias. SOURCE: Wolpe (1958); Tryon (2005) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

49. **Token Economies** — Using symbolic rewards (tokens, points) exchangeable for backup reinforcers. Effective in classrooms, psychiatric units, and correctional facilities. Long history of evidence. SOURCE: Matson & Boisjoli (2009). EVIDENCE: A

50. **Behavioral Sleep Medicine (CBT-I)** — Cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia: sleep restriction, stimulus control, cognitive restructuring. Recommended as first-line treatment over medication by AASM. Effect sizes comparable to or larger than medication, with longer-lasting effects. SOURCE: Trauer et al. (2015) meta-analysis; AASM guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS (Behavioral Psychology)

1. **LLMs conflate pop-Dunning-Kruger with the actual paper.** The popular "Mount Stupid" graph does not appear in Kruger & Dunning (1999). The actual finding is about miscalibration, not stupidity, and much of the effect is statistical artifact.

2. **LLMs treat Maslow's pyramid as established science.** Maslow never drew a pyramid, the hierarchy is not empirically validated as sequential, and it reflects Western cultural bias.

3. **LLMs recommend NLP techniques as if they're evidence-based.** NLP is classified as pseudoscience by mainstream psychology. Training data includes many NLP practitioners' websites.

4. **LLMs describe learning styles as fact.** Training data is saturated with educational content promoting VARK despite decades of disconfirming evidence.

5. **LLMs overstate the power of "positive thinking."** Oettingen's research shows pure positive visualization reduces motivation. Effective goal pursuit requires mental contrasting with obstacle planning.

6. **LLMs present the Stanford Prison Experiment uncritically.** The study is methodologically compromised and should not be cited as evidence that "anyone can become evil" in the right context.

7. **LLMs describe ego depletion as established fact.** The concept has failed large-scale replication. Willpower is better understood as motivation-dependent, not resource-dependent.

8. **LLMs oversimplify neurotransmitter functions.** "Dopamine = pleasure," "serotonin = happiness," "oxytocin = love" are all reductive mischaracterizations. Each neurotransmitter has complex, context-dependent roles.

9. **LLMs treat the 10,000-hour rule as Ericsson's finding.** Ericsson emphasized deliberate practice quality, not hours. Gladwell's popularization dropped the crucial qualifiers.

10. **LLMs present grief stages as universal.** The Kubler-Ross model was for dying patients, not the bereaved, has no empirical validation as a sequential process, and most people show resilience rather than prolonged devastation.

# Management Psychology: Evidence-Based Practices

## 50 BAD PRACTICES (Myths & Misconceptions)

1. **Micromanagement as "Attention to Detail"** — Excessive monitoring and control of employee work. WHY BAD: Significantly negatively correlated with job autonomy (r = -.44 to -.55). 55% of employees say it hindered productivity; 68% say decreased morale; 39% changed jobs to avoid it. Stifles creativity, erodes trust, increases turnover. INSTEAD: Set clear expectations, provide autonomy, use outcome-based evaluation. SOURCE: Vu (2025) systematic literature review; survey data. EVIDENCE: B

2. **Open Office = More Collaboration** — Removing walls and doors increases teamwork. WHY BAD: Harvard Business School study found face-to-face interactions dropped 70% in open offices while digital messaging spiked. Steelcase survey: 86 minutes/day lost to noise. 42,000-person study found lower satisfaction with "ease of interaction" than enclosed offices. INSTEAD: Provide variety of spaces: quiet focus rooms, collaboration zones, social areas. SOURCE: Bernstein & Turban (2018) Royal Society; James et al. (2021). EVIDENCE: B

3. **Stack Ranking / Forced Distribution** — Rating employees on a bell curve, terminating the bottom tier. WHY BAD: Microsoft employees called it "the most destructive process inside of Microsoft." Creates zero-sum competition, hoarding of information, avoidance of collaboration, and sabotage. Deming argued ranking destroys pride in workmanship. Microsoft abandoned it in 2013. INSTEAD: Use individual development plans, continuous feedback, team-based metrics. SOURCE: Vanity Fair (2012); Deming (1986). EVIDENCE: B

4. **"Culture Eats Strategy" Without Defining Culture** — Vague invocations of "culture" as a magical force. WHY BAD: Culture is measurable and manageable only when defined in specific behavioral terms. Saying "culture eats strategy" without behavioral specifics is meaningless—it becomes an excuse for not doing the analytical work. INSTEAD: Define culture as observable behavioral norms, measure it, and align it with strategy. Use Schein's three levels model (artifacts, espoused values, underlying assumptions). SOURCE: Schein (2010); Groysberg et al. (2018) HBR. EVIDENCE: C

5. **Psychological Safety Theater** — Talking about psychological safety without creating it. WHY BAD: Simply declaring "this is a safe space" without behavioral change is performative. True psychological safety requires leaders to model vulnerability, respond non-punitively to mistakes, and actively invite dissent. Google's Project Aristotle and Edmondson's research show it must be behaviorally demonstrated. INSTEAD: Train leaders in specific behaviors: admitting mistakes, asking genuine questions, thanking people for raising problems. SOURCE: Edmondson (1999); Google Project Aristotle (2012). EVIDENCE: B

6. **Annual Performance Reviews as Primary Feedback Mechanism** — Once-a-year formal evaluation. WHY BAD: Only 14% of employees say reviews inspire improvement (Gallup). 98% of organizations say their systems deliver little value. Suffer from recency bias, halo effect, and fundamental attribution error. INSTEAD: Implement continuous feedback cycles (weekly check-ins, quarterly reviews), real-time recognition, and developmental conversations. Adobe, GE, Deloitte have all abandoned annual reviews. SOURCE: Gallup (2017); Gartner continuous feedback study. EVIDENCE: B

7. **"Servant Leadership" as Buzzword** — Using the term without specific behavioral practices. WHY BAD: While servant leadership has meta-analytic support (Lee, 2020; incremental validity over transformational leadership), it's often reduced to a slogan. Without behavioral specifics (listening, empathy, stewardship, community-building), it becomes empty rhetoric. INSTEAD: Define servant leadership as specific behaviors: active listening, empathy demonstrations, developing others' capabilities, community-building actions. Measure these behaviors. SOURCE: Lee (2020) meta-analysis; Eva et al. (2019). EVIDENCE: B

8. **Toxic Positivity in Workplace** — Forcing employees to be positive, suppressing negative emotions. WHY BAD: Faking happiness at work is associated with increased anxiety, higher heart rates, cardiovascular problems. Creates emotional exhaustion and isolation. Reduces creativity by preventing problem identification. Catalyzes quiet quitting. INSTEAD: Practice emotional authenticity; create space for constructive criticism; distinguish between optimism and denial. SOURCE: University of Michigan psychology research; Atlassian workplace study. EVIDENCE: B

9. **Meeting-Heavy Culture** — Defaulting to meetings for all communication. WHY BAD: Workers waste 24 billion hours/year in unproductive meetings globally. 76% report exhaustion on heavy meeting days. At many large companies, ~6 hours/person/week spent in ineffective meetings (~$25,000/employee/year). 41.9% of meetings rated as unproductive. INSTEAD: Use async communication by default; require agendas and decision ownership for meetings; implement "no meeting" days. SOURCE: CIPD (2023) evidence review; Rogelberg et al. (2006). EVIDENCE: B

10. **Multitasking as Productivity** — Encouraging simultaneous task handling. WHY BAD: Sophie Leroy's "attention residue" research shows switching tasks leaves cognitive residue from the previous task. Takes average 9.5 minutes to return to productive flow. APA reports up to 40% productivity loss from task switching. INSTEAD: Encourage deep work blocks, batch similar tasks, protect focus time. SOURCE: Leroy (2009) OBHDP; APA task-switching research. EVIDENCE: B

11. **"10x Developer" / Superstar Hiring** — Seeking mythical 10x performers rather than building effective teams. WHY BAD: Original 1968 Sackman study had small sample in outdated context. Nichols' study found 90% of developers fall within modest performance ranges. Same person's performance varies significantly across tasks. INSTEAD: Focus on team effectiveness, psychological safety, and removing obstacles for capable performers. SOURCE: Sackman et al. (1968); SEI analysis (Nichols). EVIDENCE: B

12. **Carrot-and-Stick Motivation** — Using only rewards and punishments to motivate knowledge workers. WHY BAD: Deci et al. (1999) meta-analysis of 128 studies: tangible rewards significantly undermine intrinsic motivation (d = -0.40). Controlling incentives reduce creativity and complex problem-solving performance. INSTEAD: Support autonomy, competence, and relatedness (SDT). Use rewards to signal competence, not to control behavior. SOURCE: Deci et al. (1999); Pink (2009) "Drive." EVIDENCE: A

13. **Hiring Based on Unstructured Interviews** — Free-form conversational interviews. WHY BAD: Schmidt & Hunter (1998) meta-analysis: unstructured interviews have low predictive validity (r = .38) vs. structured interviews (r = .51). Subject to confirmation bias, halo effect, and similar-to-me bias. INSTEAD: Use structured interviews with predetermined questions, standardized scoring rubrics, and work sample tests. SOURCE: Schmidt & Hunter (1998). EVIDENCE: A

14. **"Fail Fast" Without Learning Systems** — Encouraging failure without systematic learning mechanisms. WHY BAD: Failure without structured reflection and knowledge capture simply repeats mistakes. Edmondson (2011) distinguishes preventable failures (bad), complex failures (unavoidable), and intelligent failures (good) - only the last type is valuable, and only with learning systems. INSTEAD: Create after-action reviews, blameless postmortems, and institutional memory systems. SOURCE: Edmondson (2011) HBR "Strategies for Learning from Failure." EVIDENCE: C

15. **Ignoring Cognitive Biases in Decision-Making** — Not using structured decision processes to counter biases. WHY BAD: Kahneman's "noise" research shows that professionals making identical judgments vary dramatically (e.g., underwriters differ by 55% on same case). Anchoring, availability, and confirmation biases systematically distort decisions. INSTEAD: Use decision checklists, pre-mortems, reference-class forecasting, and structured analytic techniques. SOURCE: Kahneman, Sibony, & Sunstein (2021) "Noise." EVIDENCE: A

16. **One-Size-Fits-All Management Style** — Managing everyone the same way regardless of skill and motivation level. WHY BAD: Hersey & Blanchard's situational leadership research (and subsequent models) shows different readiness levels require different approaches. High-skill/high-will employees need delegation; low-skill/high-will need coaching. INSTEAD: Adapt leadership style to individual and situation. Use situational leadership frameworks. SOURCE: Hersey & Blanchard (1969); meta-analyses by Thompson & Vecchio (2009). EVIDENCE: B

17. **Believing "Culture Fit" Prevents Diversity** — Using cultural fit as hiring criterion without behavioral definition. WHY BAD: "Culture fit" often means "people like me," leading to homogeneity, groupthink, and discrimination. Rivera (2015) found interviewers defined fit as shared leisure activities and personality. INSTEAD: Hire for "culture add"—what new perspectives and capabilities someone brings. Define fit as shared values and work standards, not demographics or hobbies. SOURCE: Rivera (2015) American Sociological Review. EVIDENCE: B

18. **Assuming Correlation Between Hours Worked and Output** — Equating long hours with productivity. WHY BAD: Pencavel (2015) found productivity per hour declines sharply after 50 hours/week and falls to near zero after 55 hours. Stanford research showed output at 70 hours barely exceeded output at 56 hours. Overwork increases errors, accidents, and health costs. INSTEAD: Measure output, not hours. Support recovery time. Encourage sustainable work practices. SOURCE: Pencavel (2015) Economic Journal. EVIDENCE: B

19. **Neglecting Onboarding** — Minimal orientation for new hires ("sink or swim"). WHY BAD: Bauer (2010) research shows structured onboarding increases new hire retention by 82% and productivity by over 70%. Poor onboarding is the #1 driver of early turnover. INSTEAD: Implement structured 90-day onboarding with clear milestones, buddy systems, and regular check-ins. SOURCE: Bauer (2010); SHRM onboarding research. EVIDENCE: B

20. **Using Personality Tests for Hiring Decisions** — MBTI, DISC, or similar tools for selection. WHY BAD: MBTI has no predictive validity for job performance. DISC is a typology without selection evidence. Even Big Five traits explain only ~5-15% of job performance variance alone. INSTEAD: Use structured interviews, cognitive ability tests, and work samples—these have the highest predictive validity. SOURCE: Schmidt & Hunter (1998); Morgeson et al. (2007). EVIDENCE: A

21. **Treating All Turnover as Bad** — Trying to retain everyone. WHY BAD: Functional turnover (poor performers leaving) benefits organizations. Abelson & Baysinger (1984) proposed optimal turnover rate—too little turnover leads to stagnation and complacency. INSTEAD: Focus on retaining high performers and culture carriers; measure regrettable turnover specifically. SOURCE: Abelson & Baysinger (1984); Hausknecht & Trevor (2011). EVIDENCE: B

22. **Overreliance on Financial Incentives** — Using bonuses and raises as primary motivators. WHY BAD: Herzberg's two-factor theory (confirmed by subsequent research): money is a "hygiene factor"—its absence demotivates, but its presence doesn't sustain motivation. For complex cognitive work, extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation (Deci, 1999). INSTEAD: Ensure fair compensation (remove dissatisfaction), then focus on autonomy, mastery, and purpose. SOURCE: Herzberg (1959); Deci et al. (1999). EVIDENCE: A

23. **"Best Practices" Without Context** — Blindly adopting what worked elsewhere. WHY BAD: Pfeffer & Sutton (2006) "evidence-based management" movement shows that practices are context-dependent. Google's practices won't work at a 20-person startup. Agile works differently in different cultures. INSTEAD: Use evidence-based management: evaluate local context, run small experiments, measure outcomes. SOURCE: Pfeffer & Sutton (2006). EVIDENCE: C

24. **Consensus-Driven Decision Making for All Decisions** — Requiring everyone to agree. WHY BAD: Slows decision-making dramatically, often produces lowest-common-denominator solutions, and amplifies groupthink. Janis (1972) showed consensus-seeking led to catastrophic policy decisions (Bay of Pigs, Challenger). INSTEAD: Use appropriate decision modes: autocratic for urgent/low-stakes, consultative for complex decisions, consensus only for high-commitment items. SOURCE: Janis (1972); Vroom & Yetton (1973). EVIDENCE: B

25. **Ignoring the Peter Principle** — Promoting top performers to management without assessing management capability. WHY BAD: Benson et al. (2019) study of 53,000+ salespeople found that the best salespeople made the worst managers, and every promotion of a top seller decreased subordinate performance by 7.5%. INSTEAD: Create dual career tracks (individual contributor and management); assess management aptitude separately from technical skill. SOURCE: Benson et al. (2019) Quarterly Journal of Economics. EVIDENCE: B

26. **Email as Default Communication** — Using email for everything from project management to brainstorming. WHY BAD: Kushlev & Dunn (2015) found checking email frequently increases stress. Email lacks context, tone, and immediate feedback. Complex discussions degrade when asynchronous. INSTEAD: Match communication medium to message type. Use email for informational updates, real-time conversation for complex discussions, project tools for task tracking. SOURCE: Dabbish et al. (2005); Mark et al. (2012). EVIDENCE: B

27. **Treating Engagement Surveys as Sufficient** — Measuring engagement without acting on results. WHY BAD: Gallup data shows only 33% of employees are engaged; surveying without action actually decreases engagement further because it signals management doesn't care. INSTEAD: Close the feedback loop: share results transparently, create action plans with employee input, follow through. SOURCE: Gallup State of the Global Workplace; Macey & Schneider (2008). EVIDENCE: B

28. **Hero Culture / Individual Heroics** — Celebrating people who work all-nighters to fix crises. WHY BAD: Signals systemic dysfunction. Hero culture discourages process improvement, creates bus-factor risks, and normalizes unsustainable work patterns. The hero is often the person who caused the crisis through shortcuts. INSTEAD: Celebrate reliability, planning, and proactive problem prevention. Invest in systems that prevent crises. SOURCE: Perrow (1984) "Normal Accidents"; DevOps research (Forsgren et al., 2018). EVIDENCE: C

29. **Assuming Feedback = Criticism** — Providing only corrective feedback. WHY BAD: Losada ratio research (positive-to-negative feedback) has methodological issues, but the underlying principle that people need positive recognition is well-supported. Gottman's research on relationships (5:1 ratio) is more robust. Feedback that only focuses on deficits creates defensiveness and avoidance. INSTEAD: Balance recognition of strengths with developmental feedback. Focus on future behavior rather than past failures. SOURCE: Gottman (1999); Kluger & DeNisi (1996) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: B

30. **Remote Work = Lower Productivity** — Assuming people working from home slack off. WHY BAD: Bloom et al. (2015) RCT at Ctrip (16,000 employees): remote workers showed 13% performance increase, worked more hours, took fewer breaks and sick days, and had 50% lower attrition. Stanford and Microsoft Research studies confirm. INSTEAD: Measure output not presence; provide flexibility with accountability; invest in remote collaboration tools. SOURCE: Bloom et al. (2015) Quarterly Journal of Economics. EVIDENCE: B

31. **Mandatory Fun / Forced Team Building** — Requiring participation in social activities (trust falls, escape rooms, etc.). WHY BAD: Forced social activities can alienate introverts, people with disabilities, and those with caregiving responsibilities. Often performative and don't address actual team dysfunction. Can feel coercive. INSTEAD: Create organic opportunities for connection; address real team dynamics through structured retrospectives and shared purpose. SOURCE: Allen & Hecht (2004). EVIDENCE: C

32. **"Move Fast and Break Things"** — Embracing speed at the expense of quality and safety. WHY BAD: Works only in specific contexts (early-stage startups with low-stakes products). In regulated industries, safety-critical systems, or at scale, breaking things has compounding costs. Technical debt accumulates. INSTEAD: Move fast with guardrails: automated testing, graduated rollouts, feature flags, blameless postmortems. SOURCE: Forsgren et al. (2018) "Accelerate." EVIDENCE: B

33. **Seniority-Based Compensation** — Paying primarily based on years of service. WHY BAD: Rewards tenure rather than contribution. Research shows performance varies more within experience levels than between them. Can demotivate high-performing newer employees. INSTEAD: Use competency-based and performance-based compensation that reflects actual contribution, while ensuring fair base pay. SOURCE: Gerhart & Milkovich (1990). EVIDENCE: B

34. **Punishing Failure** — Creating consequences for failed projects or experiments. WHY BAD: Directly undermines psychological safety and innovation. Edmondson (2019) shows organizations that punish failure get less reporting of problems, more cover-ups, and slower learning. Errors become hidden until they cascade. INSTEAD: Distinguish between process failures (negligence, violation) and outcome failures (well-executed experiments that didn't work). Only the former deserve consequences. SOURCE: Edmondson (2019) "The Fearless Organization." EVIDENCE: B

35. **Information Hoarding by Leaders** — Restricting information flow as a power mechanism. WHY BAD: Reduces team effectiveness, creates bottlenecks, prevents coordination, and signals distrust. Team performance correlates strongly with information transparency (Bunderson & Sutcliffe, 2003). INSTEAD: Default to transparency; create shared dashboards and regular information-sharing rituals; restrict only truly sensitive information. SOURCE: Bunderson & Sutcliffe (2003); Mesmer-Magnus & DeChurch (2009) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: B

36. **Talent War / Only Hiring "A Players"** — Believing the organization should only hire the top 10% of candidates. WHY BAD: Creates unsustainable recruitment costs, ignores the importance of team composition, and leads to homogeneity. "A player" definitions are usually subjective and biased. Most value comes from team dynamics, not individual talent. INSTEAD: Hire for team composition and culture add; develop talent internally; create systems that make good performers great. SOURCE: Groysberg (2010) "Chasing Stars." EVIDENCE: B

37. **Ignoring Manager Quality** — Investing in strategy and process while neglecting manager development. WHY BAD: Gallup research: managers account for at least 70% of the variance in employee engagement scores. Bad managers are the #1 reason people quit. Yet most managers receive minimal training. INSTEAD: Invest heavily in manager selection (assess for empathy, coaching ability, communication) and ongoing development. SOURCE: Gallup (2015); Buckingham & Coffman (1999). EVIDENCE: B

38. **Change Management as One-Time Event** — Treating organizational change as a project with a beginning and end. WHY BAD: Kotter's research shows 70% of change initiatives fail, often because organizations treat change as an event rather than ongoing capability. Resistance builds when change is imposed rather than co-created. INSTEAD: Build change capability as a continuous organizational competency. Involve affected stakeholders early. Use Prosci's ADKAR model with ongoing reinforcement. SOURCE: Kotter (1996); Beer & Nohria (2000). EVIDENCE: B

39. **Confusing Busyness with Productivity** — Equating activity with output. WHY BAD: Bruch & Ghoshal (2002) found only 10% of managers were both purposeful and energetic. 40% were "busy but not effective," engaged in activity without strategic focus. Busyness is often a status signal, not a measure of value creation. INSTEAD: Measure outcomes, not activity. Use OKRs or similar frameworks to align effort with impact. SOURCE: Bruch & Ghoshal (2002) HBR; Newport (2016). EVIDENCE: C

40. **Zero-Based Trust** — Requiring employees to "earn" trust before being given autonomy. WHY BAD: Starting from distrust creates a self-fulfilling prophecy. Research on trust shows that extending trust first yields higher returns in organizational settings (high-trust organizations outperform low-trust by 286% in total shareholder return). INSTEAD: Start with reasonable trust and verify. Use Luhmann's framework: trust-but-verify is more efficient than prove-then-trust. SOURCE: Zak (2017); Great Place to Work research. EVIDENCE: B

41. **Ignoring Cognitive Diversity in Teams** — Building teams based on demographic diversity alone. WHY BAD: Demographic diversity without cognitive diversity doesn't improve decision-making. Reynolds & Lewis (2017) HBR research shows cognitive diversity (different thinking styles and problem-solving approaches) predicts team performance better than demographic diversity alone. INSTEAD: Ensure cognitive diversity (different perspectives, knowledge bases, problem-solving approaches) alongside demographic diversity. SOURCE: Reynolds & Lewis (2017); Woolley et al. (2010). EVIDENCE: B

42. **Always-On Work Culture** — Expecting immediate responses at all hours. WHY BAD: Perlow & Porter (2009) found that "predictable time off" (mandatory disconnection) actually improved team performance at BCG. Always-on culture creates chronic stress, reduces recovery, and decreases creativity. INSTEAD: Establish response-time expectations by channel, create explicit off-hours boundaries, model disconnection from the top. SOURCE: Perlow & Porter (2009) HBR; Sonnentag (2012). EVIDENCE: B

43. **Management by Walking Around (MBWA) as Sufficient** — Believing informal check-ins replace structured management processes. WHY BAD: While MBWA can build rapport, it's subject to availability bias, favors extroverts and proximate employees, and doesn't scale. Remote/hybrid work makes it impossible. Without structured follow-up, it's just socializing. INSTEAD: Combine informal connection with structured one-on-ones, documented goals, and systematic performance management. SOURCE: Amabile & Kramer (2011). EVIDENCE: C

44. **Ignoring the Impact of Physical Environment** — Not considering how workspace design affects cognition and behavior. WHY BAD: Research shows noise levels, temperature, lighting, and layout all significantly affect performance, creativity, and well-being. The GSA workplace study found environmental satisfaction strongly predicts job satisfaction. INSTEAD: Design workspaces intentionally, with input from occupants. Provide temperature and lighting control where possible. SOURCE: Veitch et al. (2007); Seddigh et al. (2014). EVIDENCE: B

45. **Training Without Transfer Support** — Investing in training programs without ensuring skills transfer to the job. WHY BAD: Baldwin & Ford (1988) and subsequent research show only 10-20% of training transfers to the workplace. Without post-training practice opportunities, supervisor support, and real-world application, training investment is largely wasted. INSTEAD: Design for transfer: include on-the-job application, manager involvement, spaced practice, and accountability mechanisms. SOURCE: Baldwin & Ford (1988); Blume et al. (2010) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

46. **Believing Leadership Is Innate** — "Leaders are born, not made." WHY BAD: While genetics explain ~30% of variance in leadership emergence (Arvey et al., 2006), the majority is developable. Leadership effectiveness depends heavily on learnable skills: communication, feedback, delegation, strategic thinking. INSTEAD: Invest in evidence-based leadership development; use development assignments, coaching, and feedback-rich experiences. SOURCE: Arvey et al. (2006); Day et al. (2014). EVIDENCE: B

47. **Using Gamification Without Understanding Motivation** — Slapping points, badges, and leaderboards on everything. WHY BAD: When gamification relies on extrinsic rewards (points, badges), it can undermine intrinsic motivation (the overjustification effect). Leaderboards can demotivate those not at the top. Effect fades as novelty wears off. INSTEAD: Design gamification that supports autonomy (choice), competence (progress feedback), and relatedness (collaboration), aligned with SDT principles. SOURCE: Hamari et al. (2014) meta-analysis; Deci & Ryan (2000). EVIDENCE: B

48. **Ignoring Job Crafting Potential** — Designing rigid job descriptions that prevent employee autonomy. WHY BAD: Wrzesniewski & Dutton (2001) showed employees naturally "craft" their jobs to find meaning. Organizations that suppress this reduce engagement. Job crafting correlates with performance and well-being. INSTEAD: Allow and encourage job crafting within role boundaries. Let employees shape tasks, relationships, and perceptions of their work. SOURCE: Wrzesniewski & Dutton (2001); Rudolph et al. (2017) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: B

49. **Assuming Linear Scaling** — Believing what works at 10 people works at 100 or 1,000. WHY BAD: Communication complexity grows quadratically (n*(n-1)/2 possible connections). Processes that work for small teams break at scale. Dunbar's number (~150) limits meaningful social connections. INSTEAD: Redesign processes at each growth stage; create smaller autonomous teams within larger organizations; invest in coordination mechanisms. SOURCE: Dunbar (1992); Brooks (1975) "The Mythical Man-Month." EVIDENCE: B

50. **Ignoring the Research-Practice Gap** — Not using evidence-based management practices. WHY BAD: Pfeffer & Sutton (2006) found that managers make decisions based on ideology, casual benchmarking, and personal experience rather than evidence. Medical field adopted evidence-based practice decades ago; management lags behind. INSTEAD: Read research syntheses, consult meta-analyses, run internal experiments, partner with I/O psychologists. SOURCE: Rousseau (2006); Pfeffer & Sutton (2006). EVIDENCE: B

## 50 BEST PRACTICES (Evidence-Based Management Psychology)

1. **Psychological Safety (Edmondson)** — Creating environments where people feel safe to take interpersonal risks. Google's Project Aristotle found it's the #1 predictor of team effectiveness. Sales teams with high safety exceeded targets by 17%. Must be behaviorally demonstrated, not just declared. SOURCE: Edmondson (1999); Google Project Aristotle. EVIDENCE: B

2. **Self-Determination Theory in Management** — Supporting autonomy, competence, and relatedness in the workplace. Autonomy-supportive managers produce subordinates with higher satisfaction, engagement, and well-being. SDT is one of the most validated motivation frameworks. SOURCE: Deci, Connell, & Ryan (1989); Gagne & Deci (2005). EVIDENCE: A

3. **Structured Interviewing** — Using predetermined questions with standardized scoring. Predictive validity r = .51 vs. .38 for unstructured interviews. Adding work sample tests (r = .54) further improves prediction. Simple, cost-effective improvement. SOURCE: Schmidt & Hunter (1998) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

4. **Continuous Feedback Cycles** — Regular, timely feedback rather than annual reviews. Increases engagement by 40% and performance by 26% (Gartner). 14.9% lower turnover with regular strength-based feedback (Gallup). Adobe saw voluntary turnover drop 30% after switching. SOURCE: Gartner (2018); Gallup; Adobe case study. EVIDENCE: B

5. **Goal Setting with Specificity and Challenge** — Locke & Latham's goal-setting theory. Specific, challenging goals consistently outperform vague or easy goals (d = 0.42-0.80 across hundreds of studies). Add feedback and commitment mechanisms for maximum effect. SOURCE: Locke & Latham (2002) 40-year review. EVIDENCE: A

6. **Active Listening in Leadership** — Demonstrating genuine understanding and engagement when employees speak. Meta-analysis of 400,020 observations: perceived listening correlates r = .39 with work outcomes, with stronger effects on relationship quality. SOURCE: Meta-analysis (2024) Journal of Business and Psychology. EVIDENCE: A

7. **Job Demands-Resources Model** — Balancing job demands with resources (autonomy, feedback, social support, development). Well-validated framework predicting burnout (when demands exceed resources) and engagement (when resources exceed demands). SOURCE: Bakker & Demerouti (2007); Schaufeli & Taris (2014). EVIDENCE: A

8. **Team Debriefing / After-Action Reviews** — Systematic post-event learning discussions. Tannenbaum & Cerasoli (2013) meta-analysis: debriefs improve performance by approximately 20-25%. The US Army's AAR process is widely validated. SOURCE: Tannenbaum & Cerasoli (2013). EVIDENCE: A

9. **Transformational Leadership Behaviors** — Idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration. Judge & Piccolo (2004) meta-analysis: transformational leadership correlates r = .44 with leader effectiveness. More effective than transactional for innovation and extra-role behavior. SOURCE: Judge & Piccolo (2004) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

10. **Pre-Mortem Analysis** — Before starting a project, imagining it has failed and working backward to identify why. Klein (2007) showed this reduces overconfidence and surfaces risks that standard planning misses. Simple to implement, no cost. SOURCE: Klein (2007); Kahneman endorsement. EVIDENCE: C

11. **Autonomy-Supportive Management** — Providing choice, rationale for tasks, and acknowledging feelings. Meta-analyses link autonomy support to intrinsic motivation, well-being, job satisfaction, and lower burnout. Core SDT application. SOURCE: Deci & Ryan (2000); Slemp et al. (2018) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Psychological Empowerment** — Fostering meaning, competence, self-determination, and impact in employees. Meta-analysis shows strong positive effects from structural empowerment, supportive leadership, and trust on workers' psychological empowerment. SOURCE: Seibert et al. (2011) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Evidence-Based Hiring (Work Sample Tests)** — Testing candidates with actual job-related tasks. Schmidt & Hunter (1998): work samples have the highest predictive validity (r = .54) of any single selection method. Fair, face-valid, and practical. SOURCE: Schmidt & Hunter (1998). EVIDENCE: A

14. **Manager as Coach** — Shifting from directive management to coaching-based leadership. Grant et al. (2009) meta-analysis: workplace coaching improves performance (d = 0.36), well-being (d = 0.46), and self-efficacy. More effective than training alone. SOURCE: Jones et al. (2016) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

15. **Dual Career Tracks** — Creating paths for advancement outside management. Addresses the Peter Principle by allowing top individual contributors to advance in responsibility, pay, and status without requiring management. Reduces forced promotions into unsuitable roles. SOURCE: Allen & Katz (1986); Benson et al. (2019). EVIDENCE: B

16. **Team Composition Design** — Intentionally composing teams for cognitive diversity and complementary skills. Woolley et al. (2010) found "collective intelligence" correlates with social sensitivity, conversational turn-taking, and proportion of women, not average individual IQ. SOURCE: Woolley et al. (2010) Science. EVIDENCE: B

17. **Prosocial Motivation** — Connecting work to its impact on others. Grant (2007) showed fundraising callers who met scholarship recipients raised 171% more money. Seeing the impact of work increases motivation and persistence. SOURCE: Grant (2007); Grant (2013) "Give and Take." EVIDENCE: B

18. **Structured Onboarding Programs** — 90-day programs with milestones, buddy systems, and regular check-ins. Increases retention by 82% and productivity by 70%+. One of the highest-ROI management practices available. SOURCE: Bauer (2010); SHRM. EVIDENCE: B

19. **Decision Rights Clarity (RACI/RAPID)** — Explicitly defining who makes which decisions. Reduces conflict, speeds execution, and prevents decision paralysis. Bain research shows high-performing organizations excel at decision clarity. SOURCE: Rogers & Blenko (2006) HBR. EVIDENCE: C

20. **Blameless Postmortems** — Investigating failures without assigning personal blame, focusing on systemic causes. Core DevOps practice. Forsgren et al. (2018) found it's a key predictor of high-performing technology organizations. Increases error reporting and learning. SOURCE: Forsgren et al. (2018) "Accelerate." EVIDENCE: B

21. **Span of Control Optimization** — Sizing teams to 5-9 direct reports for most management contexts. Gallup research on 92,252 teams: engagement peaks at 8-9 direct reports. Too narrow wastes management talent; too wide prevents adequate support. SOURCE: Gallup (2024); span of control research. EVIDENCE: B

22. **Leader-Member Exchange (LMX) Quality** — Building high-quality relationships with all team members, not just favorites. Meta-analyses show LMX quality predicts job satisfaction (rho = .50), organizational commitment, and performance. INSTEAD of in-group/out-group dynamics. SOURCE: Gerstner & Day (1997) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

23. **Regular One-on-One Meetings** — Weekly or biweekly individual meetings between manager and direct report. Gallup data: employees who meet regularly with their manager are almost 3x more likely to be engaged. Structure with agenda, but allow flexibility. SOURCE: Gallup (2016); Microsoft research on manager effectiveness. EVIDENCE: B

24. **Organizational Justice** — Ensuring fair processes (procedural), outcomes (distributive), and interactions (interactional). Meta-analyses show justice perceptions predict organizational commitment, citizenship behavior, and turnover. Procedural justice is often more important than distributive. SOURCE: Colquitt et al. (2001) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

25. **Job Crafting Support** — Encouraging employees to proactively shape their work tasks, relationships, and meaning. Rudolph et al. (2017) meta-analysis shows job crafting correlates with engagement, satisfaction, and performance. INSTEAD of rigid job descriptions. SOURCE: Rudolph et al. (2017) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

26. **Minimum Viable Bureaucracy** — Only enough process to prevent specific, known failure modes. Over-engineering process creates compliance theater without safety. Under-engineering creates chaos. Right-size to organizational maturity. SOURCE: Hamel (2011); Forsgren et al. (2018). EVIDENCE: C

27. **Predictable Time Off** — Creating regular, protected time for recovery and non-work activities. Perlow & Porter (2009) BCG study: teams with mandatory time off performed better and were more satisfied. Recovery is productive, not wasteful. SOURCE: Perlow & Porter (2009) HBR. EVIDENCE: B

28. **Reference Class Forecasting** — Estimating project timelines by looking at similar past projects rather than "inside view" planning. Kahneman & Tversky's research shows people are systematically over-optimistic when planning. Outside view is more accurate. SOURCE: Kahneman & Tversky (1979); Flyvbjerg (2006). EVIDENCE: B

29. **Servant Leadership Behaviors (Specific)** — Listening, empathy, awareness, persuasion, conceptualization, foresight, stewardship, commitment to growth, community-building. Meta-analysis of 130 studies: incremental predictive validity over transformational leadership for trust and organizational citizenship. SOURCE: Lee (2020) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

30. **Deliberate Practice in Professional Development** — Structured practice with feedback targeting specific skill weaknesses. Not just "more experience" but focused, effortful improvement with expert guidance. More effective than passive training. SOURCE: Ericsson et al. (1993); Fadde & Klein (2012). EVIDENCE: B

31. **High-Performance Work Systems (HPWS)** — Bundles of complementary HR practices (selection, training, compensation, involvement). Combs et al. (2006) meta-analysis: HPWS increase organizational performance by about 20%. Individual practices are less effective in isolation. SOURCE: Combs et al. (2006) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Strength-Based Management** — Identifying and leveraging individual strengths rather than only fixing weaknesses. Gallup research: people who use strengths daily are 6x more likely to be engaged. Teams focused on strengths show 12.5% greater productivity. SOURCE: Gallup (2016); Clifton & Harter (2003). EVIDENCE: B

33. **Deep Work Protection** — Creating uninterrupted time blocks for cognitively demanding work. Newport (2016) synthesized research showing deep, focused work produces disproportionate value. Cal Newport's framework has organizational applications. SOURCE: Newport (2016); Leroy (2009) attention residue research. EVIDENCE: B

34. **Transparent Communication** — Sharing information openly by default, restricting only what's truly sensitive. Great Place to Work data: high-trust organizations (built on transparency) outperform low-trust by 2-3x on financial metrics. SOURCE: Zak (2017); Great Place to Work Institute. EVIDENCE: B

35. **Intrinsic Reward Design** — Designing jobs to be inherently motivating through variety, identity, significance, autonomy, and feedback (Hackman & Oldham Job Characteristics Model). One of the most validated job design frameworks, predicting satisfaction and performance. SOURCE: Hackman & Oldham (1976); Humphrey et al. (2007) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

36. **Meetings with Agendas and Decisions** — Every meeting has a purpose, agenda, and expected outcomes. CIPD evidence review: structured meetings are rated significantly more effective. Time-boxed agendas reduce meeting duration and increase satisfaction. SOURCE: CIPD (2023); Rogelberg et al. (2006). EVIDENCE: B

37. **Asynchronous Communication Default** — Using written, non-real-time communication for most information sharing. Reduces meeting load, respects different time zones and working patterns, creates documentation, and allows deep work. SOURCE: Perlow et al. (2017); Basecamp/37signals research. EVIDENCE: C

38. **Systems Thinking in Management** — Understanding organizations as complex adaptive systems with feedback loops, emergence, and non-linear effects. Senge (1990) "The Fifth Discipline" - focusing on systemic causes rather than individual blame. SOURCE: Senge (1990); Sterman (2000). EVIDENCE: B

39. **Competency-Based Development** — Defining clear competency models and assessing gaps for development planning. More effective than generic training because it targets specific skill gaps with measurable progression. SOURCE: Campion et al. (2011). EVIDENCE: B

40. **Realistic Job Previews** — Showing candidates the actual nature of the job (including downsides) before they accept. Reduces turnover by 10-30% by managing expectations and enabling self-selection. One of the most cost-effective retention tools. SOURCE: Phillips (1998) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

41. **Cognitive Task Analysis** — Systematically analyzing expert decision-making to capture tacit knowledge. Enables more effective training, better decision support systems, and reduced reliance on individual experts. SOURCE: Crandall et al. (2006); Clark & Estes (2008). EVIDENCE: B

42. **Feedback Seeking Behavior Encouragement** — Creating environments where employees actively seek feedback rather than waiting for it. Anseel et al. (2015) meta-analysis: feedback-seeking behavior correlates with performance and creativity. INSTEAD of top-down-only feedback. SOURCE: Anseel et al. (2015) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

43. **Team Mental Models** — Developing shared understanding of tasks, team processes, and member capabilities. Mohammed et al. (2010) meta-analysis: shared mental models predict team performance (rho = .35-.52). Critical for coordination. SOURCE: Mohammed et al. (2010). EVIDENCE: A

44. **Workload Balance and Recovery** — Managing work intensity with adequate recovery periods. Sonnentag (2012) recovery research: psychological detachment from work during off-hours predicts next-day engagement and reduces burnout. SOURCE: Sonnentag & Fritz (2015) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

45. **Error Management Culture** — Treating errors as learning opportunities rather than threats. Frese & Keith (2015): error management training outperforms error avoidance training for adaptive transfer. Organizations with error management culture innovate more. SOURCE: Frese & Keith (2015); Van Dyck et al. (2005). EVIDENCE: B

46. **Participative Decision-Making (Where Appropriate)** — Involving team members in decisions that affect their work. Meta-analyses show participative management correlates with satisfaction and performance, particularly for complex tasks requiring implementation commitment. SOURCE: Cotton et al. (1988); Wagner (1994) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

47. **Mentoring Programs (Structured)** — Formal mentoring with clear objectives, matching criteria, and evaluation. Allen et al. (2004) meta-analysis: mentoring correlates with career success (r = .22-.29), job satisfaction, and intention to stay. Structured programs outperform informal mentoring. SOURCE: Allen et al. (2004) meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

48. **Process Improvement Methodology (Lean/Kaizen)** — Continuous incremental improvement of work processes with employee involvement. Toyota Production System principles have strong evidence for reducing waste, improving quality, and engaging workers. SOURCE: Womack & Jones (1996); Shah & Ward (2003). EVIDENCE: B

49. **Emotional Regulation Training for Leaders** — Teaching leaders to recognize and manage their emotional responses. Especially important because leader emotions are contagious (emotional contagion research). Leaders' moods predict team performance and climate. SOURCE: Barsade (2002); Walter & Bruch (2009). EVIDENCE: B

50. **Evidence-Based Management Practice** — Making decisions based on the best available research evidence, organizational data, professional expertise, and stakeholder input. The management equivalent of evidence-based medicine. Consistently outperforms intuition-based management for complex decisions. SOURCE: Rousseau (2006); Barends et al. (2014). EVIDENCE: B

## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS (Management Psychology)

1. **LLMs recommend brainstorming in groups as universally superior.** Research consistently shows individual ideation produces more and better ideas. LLMs should recommend brainwriting or hybrid approaches.

2. **LLMs treat the "10x developer" as fact.** The original research is methodologically weak, decades old, and does not support the heroic narrative. Team effectiveness matters more than individual superstars.

3. **LLMs present open offices as promoting collaboration.** The evidence strongly shows the opposite. LLMs should recommend varied workspace designs with quiet focus areas.

4. **LLMs oversimplify motivation as "find your passion."** Self-determination theory shows motivation depends on autonomy, competence, and relatedness, not passion-matching. Extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation for interesting tasks.

5. **LLMs recommend personality tests (especially MBTI) for hiring.** MBTI has no predictive validity for job performance. Structured interviews and work samples are evidence-based alternatives.

6. **LLMs present leadership styles in simplistic dichotomies.** Leadership is situational and multi-dimensional. Effective leadership depends on context, follower readiness, and task characteristics.

7. **LLMs give "work-life balance" advice without mentioning recovery research.** Sonnentag's research on psychological detachment is more evidence-based than vague balance advice. The key is cognitive recovery from work demands.

8. **LLMs repeat "culture eats strategy" without noting this is an untestable slogan.** Culture and strategy interact; neither "eats" the other. Culture must be defined behaviorally to be managed.

9. **LLMs present psychological safety as merely "being nice."** Edmondson's research shows psychological safety includes the ability to challenge ideas, raise concerns, and admit mistakes, not just comfort and agreeableness.

10. **LLMs default to individual-level explanations for organizational problems.** Systems thinking is underrepresented in LLM training data. Most organizational problems have systemic causes (processes, incentives, structures) rather than being attributable to individual character flaws.
