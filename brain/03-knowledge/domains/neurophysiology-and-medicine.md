# Neurophysiology: Evidence-Based Practices

> **Anti-Stupidity Shield** -- This domain is SAFETY-CRITICAL. Bad neurophysiology or medical advice can cause real harm. Every claim below is grounded in Cochrane systematic reviews, WHO guidelines, or major peer-reviewed journals. Evidence grades: **A** = Cochrane systematic review / major meta-analysis, **B** = RCT or high-quality cohort study, **C** = expert consensus / narrative review.

---

## 50 BAD PRACTICES (Neuromyths & Misconceptions)

1. **"We only use 10% of our brain"** -- The claim that 90% of the brain is inactive or unused. WHY BAD: Neuroimaging (fMRI, PET) shows virtually all brain regions have known functions and are active over a 24-hour period; even small lesions cause significant deficits. INSTEAD: Recognize that different regions activate for different tasks, but no large region is idle. SOURCE: Beyerstein (1999), *Scientific American*; Herculano-Houzel (2002). EVIDENCE: B

2. **Learning styles (visual/auditory/kinesthetic)** -- The claim that students learn better when taught in their "preferred" modality. WHY BAD: A massive systematic review of 189 studies found no consistent evidence that matching instruction to learning style improves outcomes; the "meshing hypothesis" fails. INSTEAD: Use multimodal instruction and evidence-based pedagogies (retrieval practice, spaced repetition). SOURCE: Pashler et al. (2008), *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*; Frontiers systematic review (2020). EVIDENCE: A

3. **Left-brain / right-brain personality** -- The idea that people are dominantly "left-brained" (logical) or "right-brained" (creative). WHY BAD: A University of Utah study scanned 1,011 people aged 7-29, examined 7,000 brain regions, and found no evidence of whole-brain lateralization phenotypes. Both hemispheres cooperate on virtually all complex tasks. INSTEAD: Understand that lateralization exists for specific functions (e.g., language areas in left hemisphere) but does not define personality types. SOURCE: Nielsen et al. (2013), *PLoS ONE*; University of Utah fMRI study. EVIDENCE: B

4. **"Neurons that fire together wire together" as complete explanation** -- Hebb's rule oversimplified to suggest any co-activation permanently strengthens connections. WHY BAD: Hebbian plasticity is one mechanism among many (spike-timing-dependent plasticity, homeostatic plasticity, synaptic pruning, LTD). Connections also weaken, are eliminated, and require specific timing windows. INSTEAD: Describe plasticity as bidirectional and multi-mechanistic, governed by timing, neuromodulation, and homeostatic constraints. SOURCE: Abbott & Nelson (2000), *Nature Neuroscience*. EVIDENCE: B

5. **Dopamine = pleasure chemical** -- The popular framing of dopamine as "the pleasure molecule." WHY BAD: Schultz's work (1997+) demonstrated dopamine encodes reward prediction error (the difference between expected and received reward), not pleasure itself. Dopamine drives motivation, wanting, and learning -- "liking" is mediated more by opioid and endocannabinoid systems. INSTEAD: Describe dopamine as a prediction-error and motivational signal. SOURCE: Schultz (1997), *Science*; Berridge & Robinson (2016); Watabe-Uchida et al. (2017), *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*. EVIDENCE: B

6. **Serotonin = happiness chemical** -- Claiming low serotonin directly causes depression. WHY BAD: A landmark 2022 umbrella review in *Molecular Psychiatry* (Moncrieff et al.) found no consistent evidence that depression is caused by low serotonin. SSRIs help some patients but their mechanism is unclear and likely involves negative-emotion processing changes, not simple "serotonin correction." INSTEAD: Describe depression as multifactorial (genetic, environmental, inflammatory, neuroplastic) and SSRI mechanisms as incompletely understood. SOURCE: Moncrieff et al. (2022), *Molecular Psychiatry*; Bremshey et al. (2024), *Journal of Neurochemistry*. EVIDENCE: A

7. **Unlimited neuroplasticity at any age** -- Hype that "you can rewire your brain" without limits. WHY BAD: Neuroplasticity is real but age-dependent and constrained. Meta-analysis shows age-dependent reduction in cortical plasticity measures. Critical periods exist for language, vision, and other functions. Adult plasticity operates within narrower bounds. INSTEAD: Describe plasticity as real but bounded -- greatest during development, progressively constrained with age, and not a miracle. SOURCE: British Academy review on neuroplasticity hype; Pascual-Leone et al. (2005), *Annual Review of Neuroscience*. EVIDENCE: A

8. **Brain training games improve general cognition** -- Claims by Lumosity and similar that games produce broad cognitive transfer. WHY BAD: Meta-analyses show people improve on the trained tasks but show little to no far-transfer to untrained cognitive abilities or real-world function. The FTC fined Lumosity $2M for deceptive advertising. INSTEAD: For cognitive health, use physical exercise, social engagement, sleep optimization, and learning novel complex skills. SOURCE: Simons et al. (2016), *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*; Owen et al. (2010), *Nature*. EVIDENCE: A

9. **Classical music makes babies smarter (Mozart Effect)** -- Playing Mozart to infants or children boosts intelligence. WHY BAD: The original 1993 study (36 college students, not babies) showed a small, transient spatial reasoning boost lasting ~15 minutes. Multiple replications failed. Meta-analysis shows no particular effect of Mozart beyond general arousal effects of any stimulating music. INSTEAD: Music education has separate, modest benefits, but passive listening does not raise IQ. SOURCE: Pietschnig et al. (2010), meta-analysis in *Intelligence*; Steele et al. (1999), *Psychological Science*. EVIDENCE: A

10. **Multitasking as a trainable skill** -- The belief that people can effectively do two cognitive tasks simultaneously. WHY BAD: Cognitive psychology demonstrates the brain rapidly switches between tasks with a "switch cost" -- up to 40% loss in productive time. True dual-task processing is limited to highly automated tasks. INSTEAD: Use focused single-tasking with time-blocking; acknowledge that task-switching degrades performance, memory encoding, and accuracy. SOURCE: Monsell (2003), *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*; APA task-switching research. EVIDENCE: B

11. **Memory works like a video recording** -- Assuming memories are accurate, complete replays of events. WHY BAD: Decades of research by Loftus and others show memory is reconstructive. False memories can be implanted in 25-30% of participants through suggestion. Leading questions alter recall (car crash studies). INSTEAD: Understand memory as constructive, schema-driven, and vulnerable to suggestion, misinformation, and time-dependent decay. SOURCE: Loftus & Palmer (1974); Loftus (2005), *Learning & Memory*. EVIDENCE: B

12. **"Brain food" supplements boost cognition in healthy adults** -- Ginkgo biloba, omega-3 supplements, etc. marketed for brain health. WHY BAD: Cochrane reviews found ginkgo biloba does not prevent dementia or cognitive decline. Omega-3 supplementation shows no consistent cognitive benefit in healthy adults (Cochrane 2012). INSTEAD: A balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and fish has modest associations with better cognitive aging, but no single supplement is proven. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Birks & Grimley Evans (2009) on ginkgo; Sydenham et al. (2012) on omega-3. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Alcohol kills brain cells** -- The claim that drinking destroys neurons. WHY BAD: Moderate alcohol does not kill neurons directly. Chronic heavy drinking damages dendrites (reversible with abstinence in many cases) and causes Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome via thiamine deficiency, not direct neuron death. Binge drinking damages white matter connectivity. INSTEAD: Clarify that chronic heavy use damages neural communication and causes brain atrophy, but the mechanism is not simple "cell death." SOURCE: Harper & Matsumoto (2005), *Alcohol Research & Health*. EVIDENCE: B

14. **Humans have five senses** -- Limiting senses to sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell. WHY BAD: Humans have at minimum proprioception, vestibular sense, thermoception, nociception, interoception, and others. Neuroscience identifies 9-21+ distinct sensory modalities. INSTEAD: Teach a broader model of sensation including internal senses. SOURCE: Durie (2005), *New Scientist*. EVIDENCE: C

15. **You can't grow new neurons as an adult** -- The old dogma that neurogenesis stops at birth. WHY BAD: Adult neurogenesis was confirmed in the hippocampus (Eriksson et al., 1998) and olfactory bulb. However, the extent and functional significance remain debated (Sorrells et al., 2018 vs. Boldrini et al., 2018). INSTEAD: Adult neurogenesis occurs in limited regions but its scope and impact are still actively researched; avoid both extremes. SOURCE: Eriksson et al. (1998), *Nature Medicine*; ongoing debate in *Nature* and *Cell Stem Cell*. EVIDENCE: B

16. **Brain size determines intelligence** -- Bigger brain = smarter person. WHY BAD: Correlation between brain volume and IQ is weak (~r = 0.24). Neural efficiency, connectivity patterns, and cortical thickness matter more than raw size. Neanderthals had larger brains than modern humans. INSTEAD: Intelligence relates to neural efficiency, white-matter connectivity, and cortical organization. SOURCE: Pietschnig et al. (2015), meta-analysis in *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*. EVIDENCE: A

17. **Subliminal messages control behavior** -- Hidden messages in ads/media reliably alter behavior. WHY BAD: Meta-analyses show subliminal priming produces small, unreliable, context-dependent effects on behavior. No evidence supports the conspiracy theory of subliminal control. INSTEAD: Acknowledge that priming exists but is weak, context-dependent, and not a tool for mind control. SOURCE: Greenwald et al. (1996); meta-analysis by Trappey (1996). EVIDENCE: A

18. **Sugar causes hyperactivity in children** -- The widespread belief that sugar intake causes behavioral problems. WHY BAD: A meta-analysis of 23 studies found sugar does not affect behavior or cognition in children. Parental expectation bias drives the perceived effect. INSTEAD: Monitor overall diet quality, not sugar specifically, for behavioral effects. SOURCE: Wolraich et al. (1995), *JAMA* meta-analysis. EVIDENCE: A

19. **We have a "chemical imbalance" causing mental illness** -- Generic framing of all mental illness as neurotransmitter deficiency. WHY BAD: This model was never scientifically validated as a general theory. Mental disorders involve complex interactions of genetics, epigenetics, neural circuits, inflammation, environment, and psychology. INSTEAD: Use biopsychosocial models. SOURCE: Moncrieff et al. (2022); Deacon (2013), *Clinical Psychology Review*. EVIDENCE: A

20. **Vaccines cause autism** -- The claim from retracted Wakefield (1998) study. WHY BAD: The study was fraudulent, retracted, and Wakefield lost his medical license. Multiple large-scale studies (>1.2 million children) found zero association. INSTEAD: Vaccinate according to WHO/CDC schedules; vaccines are among the most evidence-supported interventions in medicine. SOURCE: Taylor et al. (2014), *Vaccine* (meta-analysis, 1.2M children); Cochrane Review: Demicheli et al. (2012). EVIDENCE: A

21. **Concussions are minor injuries** -- "Just a bump on the head." WHY BAD: Concussions are traumatic brain injuries. Repeated concussions cause chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE). Even single concussions can cause weeks of cognitive impairment. INSTEAD: Take all head injuries seriously; follow graduated return-to-activity protocols. SOURCE: McCrory et al. (2017), *British Journal of Sports Medicine* (Concussion in Sport Group consensus). EVIDENCE: B

22. **Coma patients are unconscious like in movies** -- Comas depicted as peaceful sleep from which people wake fully recovered. WHY BAD: Comas involve varying levels of consciousness (minimally conscious state, vegetative state). Recovery is gradual, often incomplete, and varies enormously. INSTEAD: Understand the spectrum of disorders of consciousness. SOURCE: Giacino et al. (2014), *Nature Reviews Neurology*. EVIDENCE: B

23. **Psychopathy is untreatable** -- The belief that antisocial personality is completely fixed. WHY BAD: While challenging, emerging evidence suggests some interventions (e.g., decompression model, reward-based approaches) show modest effects, especially in youth. The "nothing works" narrative discourages research. INSTEAD: Acknowledge difficulty while supporting continued research into evidence-based interventions. SOURCE: Salekin (2002), *Clinical Psychology Review*. EVIDENCE: C

24. **"Crossed wires" cause dyslexia** -- The myth that dyslexia involves reversed visual perception. WHY BAD: Dyslexia is primarily a phonological processing deficit, not a visual problem. Letter reversals are normal in early development and not specific to dyslexia. INSTEAD: Focus on phonological awareness interventions. SOURCE: Shaywitz & Shaywitz (2005), *Biological Psychiatry*. EVIDENCE: B

25. **Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) is barbaric/brain-damaging** -- ECT stigmatized as torture. WHY BAD: Modern ECT under anesthesia is one of the most effective treatments for severe, treatment-resistant depression (remission rates 50-70%). Cognitive side effects are typically transient. INSTEAD: Present ECT as evidence-based for severe depression, with informed consent about transient memory effects. SOURCE: UK ECT Review Group (2003), *The Lancet*; Cochrane Review. EVIDENCE: A

26. **Neurotransmitter imbalances can be measured by blood tests** -- Marketing of "neurotransmitter panels." WHY BAD: Peripheral neurotransmitter levels do not reflect brain levels. Blood serotonin is mostly in platelets and gut, not brain. These tests have no diagnostic validity. INSTEAD: Psychiatric diagnosis relies on clinical assessment, not blood neurotransmitter levels. SOURCE: Hinz et al. (2010) critique; APA practice guidelines. EVIDENCE: C

27. **Brain damage is always permanent** -- All brain injury is irreversible. WHY BAD: While some damage is permanent, considerable recovery occurs through neuroplastic reorganization, especially with early intensive rehabilitation. Stroke recovery can continue for years. INSTEAD: Emphasize rehabilitation potential while being honest about limitations. SOURCE: Cramer et al. (2011), *Stroke* (AHA/ASA guidelines on neuroplasticity post-stroke). EVIDENCE: B

28. **Meditation rewires the brain in weeks** -- Exaggerated claims from mindfulness marketing. WHY BAD: While meditation research shows some structural and functional brain changes, many studies suffer from small samples, lack of active controls, and publication bias. A 2018 meta-analysis found effects were smaller than popularly claimed. INSTEAD: Meditation has modest evidence for stress, anxiety, and attention, but exaggerated "brain rewiring" claims are not supported. SOURCE: Van Dam et al. (2018), *Perspectives on Psychological Science*. EVIDENCE: B

29. **Prefrontal cortex is fully developed at 25** -- The precise "age 25" claim. WHY BAD: Brain maturation is continuous and variable. The "25" number comes from one MRI study. Myelination continues into the 30s. Individual variation is enormous. INSTEAD: Say prefrontal maturation continues into mid-to-late 20s with significant individual variability. SOURCE: Lebel & Beaulieu (2011), *NeuroImage*. EVIDENCE: B

30. **Mirror neurons explain empathy, autism, and civilization** -- Excessive claims about mirror neurons. WHY BAD: The "broken mirror" theory of autism has been largely abandoned. Mirror neurons are documented in macaques; their exact human role is debated. They do not single-handedly explain empathy. INSTEAD: Describe mirror neurons as one component of action understanding, not a grand unifying theory. SOURCE: Hickok (2009), *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*. EVIDENCE: B

31. **Traumatic memories are "repressed" and recoverable** -- Freudian repression of traumatic events, recovered in therapy. WHY BAD: No reliable evidence supports the "repression" mechanism. "Recovered memory therapy" has been shown to create false memories. Traumatic events are more often remembered intrusively, not forgotten. INSTEAD: Distinguish between normal forgetting and the pseudo-scientific concept of repression; use trauma-focused CBT. SOURCE: McNally (2003), *Remembering Trauma*; Loftus (1993). EVIDENCE: B

32. **Neurofeedback cures ADHD** -- Neurofeedback marketed as a proven ADHD treatment. WHY BAD: Cochrane-grade reviews and well-controlled trials (including sham-controlled) show neurofeedback is probably not efficacious when blinding is adequate. INSTEAD: First-line ADHD treatments are stimulant medication and behavioral interventions. SOURCE: Cortese et al. (2016), *Journal of the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*. EVIDENCE: A

33. **Gluten causes brain fog in non-celiac individuals** -- Claims that gluten impairs cognition in everyone. WHY BAD: In non-celiac individuals without gluten sensitivity, no consistent evidence supports cognitive effects. Nocebo effects are strong in gluten challenge studies. INSTEAD: Test for celiac disease and non-celiac gluten sensitivity before attributing symptoms to gluten. SOURCE: Biesiekierski et al. (2013), *Gastroenterology* (the study that revised the original gluten sensitivity findings). EVIDENCE: B

34. **Essential oils treat neurological conditions** -- Aromatherapy cures seizures, dementia, etc. WHY BAD: No rigorous evidence supports essential oils as treatment for any neurological condition. Some (e.g., camphor, eucalyptus) can be neurotoxic, especially in children. INSTEAD: Use evidence-based treatments; essential oils may offer mild relaxation but are not therapeutic. SOURCE: Posadzki et al. (2012), *International Journal of Risk & Safety in Medicine*. EVIDENCE: C

35. **Sleep deprivation is a productivity tool ("I'll sleep when I'm dead")** -- Glorifying minimal sleep. WHY BAD: Meta-analyses of 61+ studies show sleep restriction significantly impairs attention, working memory, decision-making, and emotional regulation. Chronic sleep debt is linked to neurodegeneration (amyloid-beta accumulation), cardiovascular disease, and immune dysfunction. INSTEAD: Prioritize 7-9 hours of sleep; sleep is when memory consolidation, metabolic waste clearance (glymphatic system), and neural repair occur. SOURCE: Lim & Dinges (2010), *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*; Walker (2017), *Why We Sleep* (with caveats on some claims). EVIDENCE: A

36. **EMDR works because of eye movements specifically** -- Claiming the bilateral eye movement is the active ingredient. WHY BAD: Dismantling studies suggest EMDR works through its exposure-based components. The specific contribution of eye movements is unclear and may be minimal. INSTEAD: EMDR is an evidence-based trauma treatment, but the eye-movement component may not be the active mechanism. SOURCE: Davidson & Parker (2001), *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*; Cochrane Review on PTSD. EVIDENCE: B

37. **Omega-3 supplements treat depression** -- Marketing omega-3 as antidepressant. WHY BAD: Cochrane review (Appleton et al., 2021) found low-quality evidence for a small benefit of EPA but not DHA; effects were small and inconsistent. INSTEAD: A balanced diet with fish is reasonable; omega-3 supplements are not a proven monotherapy for depression. SOURCE: Appleton et al. (2021), Cochrane Database. EVIDENCE: A

38. **You lose most body heat through your head** -- Often extended to claims about brain vulnerability. WHY BAD: The head loses heat proportional to its surface area (~10%), not the 40-50% claimed. The myth originated from a flawed 1950s military study where subjects wore insulated suits but no hats. INSTEAD: Heat loss is proportional to exposed surface area. SOURCE: Pretorius et al. (2006), *British Medical Journal*. EVIDENCE: B

39. **CTE can only result from professional sports** -- Limiting CTE risk to elite athletes. WHY BAD: CTE has been found in amateur athletes, military veterans, and domestic abuse survivors. Repetitive sub-concussive impacts matter, not just diagnosed concussions. INSTEAD: Take all repetitive head impacts seriously regardless of competitive level. SOURCE: McKee et al. (2016), *JAMA*. EVIDENCE: B

40. **Nootropics ("smart drugs") reliably enhance cognition** -- Modafinil, racetams, etc. marketed as cognitive enhancers. WHY BAD: Evidence for cognitive enhancement in healthy individuals is weak and inconsistent. Modafinil helps in sleep deprivation but does not reliably boost normal cognition. Long-term safety data are lacking. INSTEAD: Sleep, exercise, and learning novel skills have better evidence for cognitive optimization. SOURCE: Battleday & Brem (2015), *European Neuropsychopharmacology* (with limitations noted). EVIDENCE: B

41. **Brain scans can "read minds" or detect lies reliably** -- fMRI lie detection, brain-reading marketing. WHY BAD: fMRI measures blood oxygenation as a proxy for activity. Reverse inference (activity pattern = specific thought) is unreliable. No brain-scan lie detection method meets legal evidentiary standards. INSTEAD: Brain imaging reveals general regional activity, not specific thoughts. SOURCE: Poldrack (2006), *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. EVIDENCE: B

42. **Phrenology-adjacent personality tests based on brain structure** -- Relating skull/brain shape to personality. WHY BAD: Phrenology was debunked in the 19th century. Modern attempts to map personality to specific brain structures remain highly speculative and unreliable at the individual level. INSTEAD: Personality is a complex emergent property of distributed brain networks. SOURCE: Kanai & Rees (2011) -- acknowledging weak associations at group level only. EVIDENCE: C

43. **Cryonics preserves brain function for future revival** -- Marketing of brain freezing for resurrection. WHY BAD: Current freezing methods cause ice crystal formation that destroys cellular ultrastructure. There is no evidence that vitrification preserves functional connectomics. Revival from cryopreservation remains science fiction. INSTEAD: Acknowledge the aspiration while being honest that no current evidence supports functional preservation. SOURCE: Scientifically analyzed by Hendricks (2015) in *Medical Hypotheses*. EVIDENCE: C

44. **Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) at home is safe and effective** -- Consumer tDCS devices marketed for brain enhancement. WHY BAD: Effects in controlled studies are small, inconsistent, and highly parameter-dependent. Home use risks burns, incorrect electrode placement, and unknown long-term effects. INSTEAD: tDCS is an experimental research tool, not a consumer product; effects are modest at best. SOURCE: Horvath et al. (2015), meta-analysis in *Neuropsychologia*. EVIDENCE: A

45. **Brain death is reversible** -- Misunderstanding brain death as recoverable. WHY BAD: Brain death (complete cessation of all brain function) is legally and medically death. No recovery from properly diagnosed brain death has ever been documented. Confusion arises from conflation with persistent vegetative state. INSTEAD: Clearly distinguish brain death from other disorders of consciousness. SOURCE: American Academy of Neurology guidelines on brain death. EVIDENCE: C

46. **Hormones directly control complex behavior in simple ways** -- "Testosterone = aggression," "oxytocin = love." WHY BAD: Hormones modulate neural circuits probabilistically; they don't deterministically cause specific behaviors. Testosterone relates to status-seeking (which can be prosocial), and oxytocin can increase in-group favoritism and out-group aggression. INSTEAD: Describe hormones as modulators that interact with context, learning, and individual differences. SOURCE: Sapolsky (2017), *Behave*; De Dreu et al. (2010) on oxytocin. EVIDENCE: B

47. **Internet addiction is an established neurological disorder** -- Equating internet overuse with substance addiction. WHY BAD: "Internet addiction" is not in the DSM-5 or ICD-11 as a distinct disorder (gaming disorder is). Neural parallels with substance addiction are superficial. Pathologizing common behavior risks overdiagnosis. INSTEAD: Recognize problematic internet use as a behavioral concern requiring further research. SOURCE: Aarseth et al. (2017), *Journal of Behavioral Addictions* (scholarly debate). EVIDENCE: C

48. **All seizures involve convulsions** -- Epilepsy = dramatic convulsions. WHY BAD: Many seizures are non-convulsive (absence seizures, focal aware seizures). Patients may stare blankly, perform repetitive movements, or have brief sensory disturbances. INSTEAD: Educate about the spectrum of seizure presentations. SOURCE: ILAE (International League Against Epilepsy) classification 2017. EVIDENCE: C

49. **Neurodegenerative diseases start when symptoms appear** -- Parkinson's, Alzheimer's begin at diagnosis. WHY BAD: Alzheimer's pathology begins 15-20 years before clinical symptoms. Parkinson's has a long prodromal phase with sleep and gut symptoms. INSTEAD: Understand neurodegeneration as a decades-long process; early biomarkers are a major research focus. SOURCE: Jack et al. (2013), *Lancet Neurology* (biomarker staging model). EVIDENCE: B

50. **"Chemical-free" lifestyle protects the brain** -- Equating all synthetic chemicals with neurotoxicity. WHY BAD: Water is a chemical. The dose makes the poison (Paracelsus). Many synthetic compounds are safe; many natural ones are neurotoxic (e.g., botulinum, tetrodotoxin, amatoxin). INSTEAD: Evaluate substances by evidence of specific harm at relevant doses, not by "natural vs. synthetic" categories. SOURCE: General toxicology principles; Ames & Gold (2000), *Mutation Research*. EVIDENCE: C

## 50 BEST PRACTICES (Neurophysiology)

1. **Prioritize 7-9 hours of sleep** -- Sleep is essential for memory consolidation (hippocampal replay), synaptic homeostasis (Tononi & Cirelli SHY model), glymphatic waste clearance, and emotional regulation. Meta-analyses of 61+ studies confirm significant cognitive impairment from restriction. SOURCE: Consensus from AASM/SRS; Walker (2017). EVIDENCE: A

2. **Regular aerobic exercise for brain health** -- 150+ min/week of moderate aerobic exercise increases BDNF, hippocampal volume, and improves executive function. Cochrane reviews confirm exercise is comparable to antidepressants and therapy for depression. SOURCE: Erickson et al. (2011), *PNAS*; Cochrane Review (2024) on exercise and depression. EVIDENCE: A

3. **Spaced repetition for long-term memory** -- Distributing learning over time with increasing intervals exploits the spacing effect, one of the most robust findings in memory research. Far superior to massed practice ("cramming"). SOURCE: Cepeda et al. (2006), meta-analysis in *Psychological Bulletin*. EVIDENCE: A

4. **Active retrieval practice** -- Testing yourself on material is more effective than re-reading or highlighting. The "testing effect" shows 50-80% improvement in long-term retention. SOURCE: Roediger & Karpicke (2006), *Psychological Science*. EVIDENCE: A

5. **Social engagement for cognitive health** -- Social interaction is consistently associated with reduced cognitive decline and dementia risk. Isolation is a risk factor comparable to smoking. SOURCE: Kuiper et al. (2015), meta-analysis in *Ageing Research Reviews*. EVIDENCE: A

6. **Stress management via evidence-based methods** -- Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which damages hippocampal neurons and impairs prefrontal function. CBT, mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), and physical activity reduce chronic stress. SOURCE: McEwen (2007), *Physiology & Behavior*; Goyal et al. (2014), *JAMA Internal Medicine*. EVIDENCE: A

7. **Mediterranean dietary pattern for neuroprotection** -- The Mediterranean diet (rich in vegetables, fish, olive oil, nuts) is associated with slower cognitive decline and reduced dementia risk. SOURCE: Valls-Pedret et al. (2015), *JAMA Internal Medicine* (PREDIMED trial). EVIDENCE: B

8. **Interleaved practice for skill acquisition** -- Mixing different types of problems or skills during practice leads to better long-term transfer than blocked practice. SOURCE: Rohrer & Taylor (2007), *Instructional Science*. EVIDENCE: B

9. **Novel complex learning for cognitive reserve** -- Learning new languages, musical instruments, or complex skills builds cognitive reserve -- a buffer against age-related decline. SOURCE: Stern (2012), *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*. EVIDENCE: B

10. **Consistent sleep-wake schedule** -- Regular sleep timing (even on weekends) aligns circadian rhythms, improving sleep quality and cognitive performance. SOURCE: American Academy of Sleep Medicine clinical guidelines; Chaput et al. (2020). EVIDENCE: B

11. **Dual-task training for fall prevention in elderly** -- Combining cognitive and motor tasks during rehabilitation improves balance and reduces falls in older adults. SOURCE: Plummer et al. (2015), *Physical Therapy*; Cochrane Review on fall prevention. EVIDENCE: A

12. **Evidence-based treatments for insomnia (CBT-I first-line)** -- Cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia is more effective than medication long-term and is recommended as first-line by AASM. SOURCE: Trauer et al. (2015), meta-analysis in *Annals of Internal Medicine*; AASM guidelines (2021). EVIDENCE: A

13. **Moderate caffeine consumption for alertness** -- 100-400mg caffeine improves alertness and attention. Timing matters (avoid 6+ hours before sleep). Tolerance develops but baseline effects remain. SOURCE: Einother & Giesbrecht (2013), *Psychopharmacology*. EVIDENCE: B

14. **Graduated return-to-activity after concussion** -- Step-wise return protocol prevents second-impact syndrome and aids recovery. Rest followed by gradual reintroduction of cognitive and physical activity. SOURCE: McCrory et al. (2017), 5th International Concussion Consensus (Berlin). EVIDENCE: B

15. **Treating hearing loss to reduce dementia risk** -- Hearing loss is the largest modifiable risk factor for dementia. Hearing aids are associated with reduced cognitive decline. SOURCE: Livingston et al. (2020), *Lancet* Commission on dementia prevention. EVIDENCE: A

16. **Multi-domain interventions for dementia prevention** -- Combining physical activity, cognitive training, vascular risk management, and social engagement (FINGER trial model). SOURCE: Ngandu et al. (2015), *The Lancet* (FINGER trial). EVIDENCE: B

17. **Trauma-focused CBT for PTSD** -- Evidence-based psychotherapies (TF-CBT, CPT, prolonged exposure) are first-line for PTSD, superior to medication alone. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Bisson et al. (2013); NICE guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

18. **Adequate hydration for cognitive performance** -- Even mild dehydration (1-2% body mass) impairs attention, working memory, and mood. Thirst is a reasonable guide for most people. SOURCE: Ganio et al. (2011), *British Journal of Nutrition*. EVIDENCE: B

19. **Screen light management before sleep** -- Blue-enriched light suppresses melatonin and delays sleep onset. Evening blue-light filtering or reduced screen use 1-2 hours before bed supports circadian alignment. SOURCE: Chang et al. (2015), *PNAS*. EVIDENCE: B

20. **Biopsychosocial model for mental health** -- Integrating biological, psychological, and social factors rather than reductionist biochemical explanations. Leads to more comprehensive treatment. SOURCE: Engel (1977), *Science*; adopted by WHO and APA. EVIDENCE: C

21. **Graduated exposure for phobias and anxiety** -- Systematic desensitization and graded exposure therapy are highly effective for specific phobias (NNT ~3-4). SOURCE: Wolitzky-Taylor et al. (2008), *Clinical Psychology Review*. EVIDENCE: A

22. **Moderate alcohol or abstinence for brain health** -- Even moderate alcohol consumption is associated with dose-dependent brain volume reduction. No "safe" threshold established for brain health. SOURCE: Topiwala et al. (2017), *BMJ*; Daviet et al. (2022), *Nature Communications*. EVIDENCE: B

23. **Treating hypertension to prevent cognitive decline** -- Blood pressure management reduces vascular dementia risk. SPRINT-MIND trial showed intensive BP lowering reduced MCI risk. SOURCE: SPRINT MIND Investigators (2019), *JAMA*. EVIDENCE: B

24. **Continuous learning and intellectual engagement** -- Higher lifetime educational and occupational complexity is associated with greater cognitive reserve. Never too late to start, though earlier is better. SOURCE: Stern (2012); Livingston et al. (2020). EVIDENCE: B

25. **Proper assessment before psychiatric medication** -- Thorough clinical assessment, structured interviews, and ruling out medical causes before prescribing psychotropic medication. SOURCE: APA Practice Guidelines; NICE guidelines. EVIDENCE: C

26. **Enriched environments for brain development** -- For developing brains, diverse sensory, social, and cognitive stimulation promotes neural development. Deprivation causes measurable deficits. SOURCE: Diamond (1988), classic enrichment studies; Shonkoff & Phillips (2000), *From Neurons to Neighborhoods*. EVIDENCE: B

27. **Evidence-based concussion screening** -- Use validated tools (SCAT5, King-Devick test) for sideline concussion assessment rather than subjective judgment. SOURCE: Echemendia et al. (2017), *British Journal of Sports Medicine*. EVIDENCE: B

28. **Stimulant medication for ADHD as first-line (in combination with behavioral therapy)** -- Stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamine) have the largest evidence base for ADHD, with effect sizes of 0.8-1.0. Combined with behavioral strategies is optimal. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Storeboe et al. (2015); MTA study (1999). EVIDENCE: A

29. **Mindfulness-based interventions for chronic pain** -- MBSR shows modest but consistent benefits for chronic pain, stress, and anxiety, comparable to CBT for some conditions. SOURCE: Hilton et al. (2017), *Annals of Internal Medicine*. EVIDENCE: A

30. **Avoiding benzodiazepines as first-line for anxiety** -- Benzos have dependency risk, cognitive impairment, and increased fall/fracture risk in elderly. SSRIs/SNRIs and CBT are preferred first-line. SOURCE: NICE guidelines; American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria. EVIDENCE: A

31. **Maintaining cardiovascular health for brain health** -- "What's good for the heart is good for the brain." Cardiovascular risk factors (diabetes, obesity, smoking) are major modifiable risk factors for dementia. SOURCE: Livingston et al. (2020), *Lancet* Commission. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Regular cognitive screening in aging** -- Early detection of cognitive decline enables planning and early intervention. MoCA and MMSE are validated screening tools. SOURCE: Nasreddine et al. (2005), *JAGS* (MoCA validation). EVIDENCE: B

33. **Avoiding polypharmacy in elderly (CNS-active drugs)** -- Multiple CNS-active medications increase confusion, falls, and cognitive impairment risk. Regular medication review is essential. SOURCE: American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria (2019). EVIDENCE: A

34. **Sunlight exposure for circadian rhythm regulation** -- Morning bright light exposure (>10,000 lux) entrains circadian clocks, improving sleep quality, mood, and alertness. SOURCE: Terman & Terman (2005), *CNS Spectrums*; SAD light therapy Cochrane Review. EVIDENCE: B

35. **Evidence-based depression screening in primary care** -- PHQ-9 and other validated instruments for systematic depression screening improve detection rates. SOURCE: USPSTF recommendation (2016); PHQ-9 validation studies. EVIDENCE: A

36. **Constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT) for stroke** -- Forcing use of the affected limb by restraining the unaffected one promotes neuroplastic recovery. One of the most evidence-based stroke rehabilitation approaches. SOURCE: Wolf et al. (2006), *JAMA* (EXCITE trial). EVIDENCE: A

37. **Sleep apnea screening and treatment** -- Untreated obstructive sleep apnea is linked to hippocampal damage, cognitive decline, and cardiovascular risk. CPAP treatment improves outcomes. SOURCE: Osorio et al. (2015), *Neurology*; AASM guidelines. EVIDENCE: B

38. **Protecting against environmental neurotoxins** -- Lead, mercury, organophosphates, and air pollution have documented neurotoxic effects, especially during development. SOURCE: Grandjean & Landrigan (2014), *Lancet Neurology*. EVIDENCE: A

39. **Psychoeducation as treatment component** -- Teaching patients about their neurological/psychiatric condition improves adherence, self-management, and outcomes. SOURCE: Cochrane reviews on psychoeducation for schizophrenia, bipolar, epilepsy. EVIDENCE: A

40. **Ketogenic diet for drug-resistant epilepsy** -- In drug-resistant epilepsy, ketogenic diet reduces seizures by 50%+ in about half of patients. One of the best-evidenced dietary interventions in neurology. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Martin-McGill et al. (2020). EVIDENCE: A

41. **Regular physical activity for Parkinson's disease** -- Exercise (especially high-intensity treadmill, tai chi) improves motor function, balance, and quality of life in PD. SOURCE: Mak et al. (2017), meta-analysis; Cochrane Review on exercise for PD. EVIDENCE: A

42. **Adequate omega-3 through diet (not supplements) for developing brains** -- Dietary DHA is essential for fetal and infant brain development. Maternal fish intake during pregnancy is associated with better neurodevelopmental outcomes. SOURCE: Hibbeln et al. (2007), *The Lancet*. EVIDENCE: B

43. **Validated screening tools for neurodevelopmental disorders** -- Using standardized assessments (ADOS-2 for autism, Conners for ADHD) rather than clinical impression alone. SOURCE: APA, AAP clinical guidelines. EVIDENCE: B

44. **Managing diabetes for neuroprotection** -- Type 2 diabetes doubles dementia risk. Good glycemic control protects against diabetic neuropathy and cognitive decline. SOURCE: Biessels et al. (2014), *Lancet Neurology*. EVIDENCE: A

45. **Music therapy in neurorehabilitation** -- Rhythmic auditory stimulation improves gait in Parkinson's; melodic intonation therapy helps aphasia after stroke. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Magee et al. (2017) on music for acquired brain injury. EVIDENCE: A

46. **Graduated return to cognitive activity after TBI** -- Structured return to school/work with accommodations improves outcomes. Full cognitive rest is not supported; graduated return is optimal. SOURCE: Leddy et al. (2016), *Current Sports Medicine Reports*. EVIDENCE: B

47. **Smoking cessation for brain health** -- Smoking accelerates brain atrophy, white matter damage, and doubles dementia risk. Cessation at any age is beneficial. SOURCE: Livingston et al. (2020); Durazzo et al. (2010), *Alcoholism: Clinical and Experimental Research*. EVIDENCE: A

48. **Limiting alcohol for adolescent brain development** -- The adolescent brain is particularly vulnerable to alcohol-induced white matter and hippocampal damage. SOURCE: Squeglia et al. (2009), *Clinical EEG and Neuroscience*. EVIDENCE: B

49. **Temperature management after cardiac arrest** -- Targeted temperature management (32-36C) after cardiac arrest improves neurological outcomes. SOURCE: Nielsen et al. (2013), *NEJM* (TTM trial). EVIDENCE: A

50. **Early intervention for developmental delays** -- Intensive early intervention (before age 3) for developmental delays produces the greatest gains due to heightened developmental plasticity. SOURCE: Shonkoff & Phillips (2000), *From Neurons to Neighborhoods*; Cochrane reviews on early intervention. EVIDENCE: A

## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS (Neurophysiology)

1. **Presenting dopamine as "the reward chemical"** -- LLMs frequently default to "dopamine is released when you feel pleasure" rather than accurately describing reward prediction error, motivation, and wanting vs. liking.

2. **Overstating neuroplasticity** -- LLMs tend to say "the brain can rewire itself at any age" without mentioning age-dependent constraints, critical periods, or the limits of adult plasticity.

3. **Repeating the "10% of the brain" myth** -- While most LLMs flag this if asked directly, they sometimes implicitly reinforce it by suggesting vast "untapped potential."

4. **Oversimplifying neurotransmitter functions** -- Assigning single emotions to single neurotransmitters (serotonin=happiness, GABA=calm, norepinephrine=alertness) when each has diverse, context-dependent roles.

5. **Conflating correlation and causation in neuroimaging** -- Describing brain scan findings as if they show a region "causing" a behavior, rather than being correlated with activity during a task.

6. **Treating the biopsychosocial model as optional** -- LLMs may default to purely biological explanations for mental illness, neglecting psychological and social determinants.

7. **Hallucinating specific neuroscience citations** -- LLMs fabricate plausible-sounding journal references (wrong year, wrong authors, non-existent papers) at rates of 25-50% in medical contexts.

8. **Exaggerating meditation and mindfulness effects** -- Presenting meditation as a proven cure for numerous conditions when evidence supports modest benefits for specific outcomes.

9. **Confusing brain death with persistent vegetative state** -- LLMs sometimes blur the distinction between irreversible brain death and recoverable disorders of consciousness.

10. **Presenting outdated neuroscience as current** -- Repeating findings from the 1990s-2000s "Decade of the Brain" without noting subsequent revisions, replications, or failures to replicate.

## 50 BAD PRACTICES (Medical Myths & Misconceptions)

1. **Self-diagnosis via internet symptom checkers** -- Googling symptoms and concluding a diagnosis. WHY BAD: Leads to "cyberchondria" -- escalating health anxiety. Systematic reviews show online symptom checking increases anxiety in ~90% of studies, with compulsive reassurance-seeking patterns. Symptom checkers have poor diagnostic accuracy for serious conditions. INSTEAD: Use internet information to prepare for a doctor visit, not replace one. SOURCE: Starcevic & Berle (2013), *Psychological Medicine*; White & Horvitz (2009). EVIDENCE: B

2. **Antibiotics for viral infections** -- Taking antibiotics for colds, flu, or COVID. WHY BAD: Antibiotics treat bacteria, not viruses. One-third of antibiotic prescriptions are unnecessary (CDC). Overuse drives antimicrobial resistance, which WHO calls a top-10 global health threat (1.27M deaths attributed in 2019). INSTEAD: Supportive care for viral infections; antivirals when indicated. SOURCE: WHO AMR factsheet (2024); CDC antibiotic stewardship. EVIDENCE: A

3. **Vitamin C megadosing to prevent/treat colds** -- Taking high-dose vitamin C to ward off illness. WHY BAD: Cochrane review (Hemila & Chalker, 2013) of 29 trials (11,306 participants) found regular vitamin C does NOT reduce cold incidence in the general population. Modest reduction in duration (~8% in adults). Megadoses after symptom onset show no consistent benefit. INSTEAD: Adequate dietary vitamin C is sufficient; supplementation is rational only for those under extreme physical stress. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Hemila & Chalker (2013). EVIDENCE: A

4. **"Detox" products and cleanses** -- Marketed teas, juices, supplements claiming to remove toxins. WHY BAD: The liver and kidneys already detoxify the body. A 2015 review found no solid evidence supporting detox diet claims. Some "detox" supplements cause liver injury (e.g., green tea extract in high doses). INSTEAD: Support liver/kidney health through moderate alcohol use, balanced diet, and maintaining healthy weight. SOURCE: Klein & Kiat (2015), *Journal of Human Nutrition and Dietetics*; Johns Hopkins Medicine. EVIDENCE: B

5. **BMI as sole health indicator** -- Using BMI alone to diagnose obesity or assess health. WHY BAD: BMI doesn't distinguish muscle from fat, doesn't account for fat distribution, and varies by sex and ethnicity. A powerlifter may be "obese" by BMI with excellent metabolic health. Waist circumference predicts cardiometabolic risk better than BMI alone. INSTEAD: Use BMI + waist circumference + metabolic markers for comprehensive assessment. SOURCE: IAS/ICCR Consensus Statement (2020), *Nature Reviews Endocrinology*; Strengths and Limitations of BMI review (2024). EVIDENCE: A

6. **"Natural = safe" fallacy** -- Assuming herbal/natural products are inherently safe. WHY BAD: Serious adverse effects documented include liver/kidney damage, cardiac arrest, and death. Herbal supplements are not FDA-regulated like drugs. Herb-drug interactions (e.g., St. John's Wort reducing efficacy of HIV drugs, contraceptives, warfarin). INSTEAD: Evaluate all substances by evidence of safety and efficacy, not origin. SOURCE: Ernst (2004), *British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology*; Posadzki et al. (2013). EVIDENCE: A

7. **"Toughing out" pain without treatment** -- Avoiding pain medication as weakness. WHY BAD: Untreated acute pain can become chronic through central sensitization. Chronic pain causes neuroplastic changes that are harder to reverse. Adequate pain management improves healing and reduces complications. INSTEAD: Use multimodal pain management (pharmacological + non-pharmacological) per WHO analgesic ladder and current guidelines. SOURCE: WHO analgesic ladder; Woolf & Salter (2000), *Science*. EVIDENCE: B

8. **Ignoring mental health as "weakness"** -- Stigmatizing mental health conditions and avoiding treatment. WHY BAD: Stigma is the 4th-ranked barrier to help-seeking. >70% of people with mental illness globally receive no treatment. Untreated mental illness worsens physical health outcomes and increases mortality. INSTEAD: Normalize mental health care; systematic screening in primary care; evidence-based treatments available. SOURCE: Clement et al. (2015), *Psychological Medicine* (systematic review); WHO Mental Health Atlas. EVIDENCE: A

9. **Extreme elimination diets without medical indication** -- Removing entire food groups (gluten, dairy, lectins) without diagnosed condition. WHY BAD: Risk of nutrient deficiencies (calcium, vitamin D, B vitamins), disordered eating patterns, and delayed diagnosis of actual conditions (celiac, IBD). Psychological harms include food anxiety and social isolation. INSTEAD: Elimination diets only under medical supervision for diagnosed or suspected food allergies/intolerances. SOURCE: Skypala et al. (2015), *Clinical and Experimental Allergy*. EVIDENCE: B

10. **"8 glasses of water per day" as universal rule** -- Prescribing exactly 64 oz of water daily for everyone. WHY BAD: No scientific evidence supports this specific number (Valtin, 2002). The original recommendation included water from food. Individual needs vary by body size, activity, climate, and health status. INSTEAD: Use thirst as primary guide; caffeinated beverages count toward fluid intake. SOURCE: Valtin (2002), *American Journal of Physiology*. EVIDENCE: B

11. **Anti-vaccination based on debunked claims** -- Refusing vaccines based on autism fears, "toxin" concerns, or natural immunity preference. WHY BAD: Wakefield's study was fraudulent and retracted. Meta-analysis of 1.2M+ children found zero association with autism. Vaccines prevent ~4-5M deaths annually (WHO). INSTEAD: Follow WHO/CDC immunization schedules; vaccines are among the most evidence-supported interventions in medicine. SOURCE: Taylor et al. (2014), *Vaccine*; Cochrane Review: Demicheli et al. (2012). EVIDENCE: A

12. **Alternative medicine replacing evidence-based treatment for serious conditions** -- Using homeopathy, naturopathy, or energy healing as primary cancer/diabetes treatment. WHY BAD: Systematic reviews consistently show homeopathy performs no better than placebo. Patients who use alternative medicine exclusively for curable cancers have 2.5x higher mortality. INSTEAD: Use integrative approaches where evidence supports complementary use, never as replacement for proven treatments. SOURCE: Cochrane Review on homeopathy; Johnson et al. (2018), *JAMA Oncology*. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Routine multivitamin supplementation in well-nourished adults** -- Taking daily multivitamins "for health." WHY BAD: USPSTF (2022) found insufficient evidence that multivitamins prevent cardiovascular disease, cancer, or mortality in generally healthy adults. Some components may be harmful in excess (vitamin A, vitamin E). INSTEAD: Get nutrients from a balanced diet; supplement only documented deficiencies. SOURCE: USPSTF Recommendation (2022); Cochrane Review: Fortmann et al. (2013). EVIDENCE: A

14. **Crash dieting / very-low-calorie diets without supervision** -- Extreme calorie restriction (<800 kcal/day) for rapid weight loss. WHY BAD: Metabolic adaptation, muscle loss, gallstone formation, nutritional deficiencies, and high regain rates. Meta-analyses show most dieters regain weight within 2-5 years. INSTEAD: Moderate caloric deficit (500-750 kcal/day below needs), combined with physical activity and behavioral support. SOURCE: NICE Obesity Guidelines (2014); Mann et al. (2007), *American Psychologist*. EVIDENCE: A

15. **Overuse of CT scans and medical imaging** -- Requesting imaging for every symptom, back pain, headache. WHY BAD: Radiation exposure risk (CT = 100-500x chest X-ray), incidental findings causing anxiety and further unnecessary procedures. For most acute low back pain, imaging doesn't change outcomes. INSTEAD: Follow evidence-based imaging criteria (e.g., Ottawa ankle rules, NEXUS criteria). SOURCE: Choosing Wisely Campaign; AAOS guidelines. EVIDENCE: B

16. **Suppressing fever with medication automatically** -- Treating every low-grade fever with antipyretics. WHY BAD: Fever is an evolved immune response. Low-grade fever (up to 38.5C/101.3F) may enhance immune function. Meta-analyses suggest routine antipyretic use may prolong some infections. INSTEAD: Treat fever when causing significant discomfort; monitor for dangerous fevers (>40C/104F). SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Meremikwu & Oyo-Ita (2002); Sullivan & Farrar (2011), *Pediatrics*. EVIDENCE: B

17. **Prescribing opioids as first-line for chronic non-cancer pain** -- Defaulting to opioids for chronic pain. WHY BAD: Opioids show minimal long-term benefit for chronic non-cancer pain. Addiction risk, tolerance, hyperalgesia, and overdose death risk. INSTEAD: Non-pharmacological treatments first (exercise, CBT, physical therapy); non-opioid medications (NSAIDs, SNRIs). SOURCE: CDC Guideline for Prescribing Opioids (2022 update). EVIDENCE: A

18. **Hormone "optimization" for anti-aging** -- Testosterone or HGH supplementation in normal-range individuals. WHY BAD: Testosterone therapy in eugonadal men increases cardiovascular risk. HGH in healthy adults increases cancer risk, carpal tunnel, diabetes. No evidence of longevity benefit. INSTEAD: Address specific hormone deficiencies when clinically diagnosed. SOURCE: FDA warnings; Vigen et al. (2013), *JAMA*. EVIDENCE: B

19. **Skipping breakfast causes weight gain** -- The myth that breakfast is "the most important meal." WHY BAD: Meta-analysis (Sievert et al., 2019, *BMJ*) found skipping breakfast does not cause weight gain; breakfast eaters may consume more total calories. Meal timing matters less than total intake. INSTEAD: Eat when hungry; meal timing is individual. SOURCE: Sievert et al. (2019), *BMJ*. EVIDENCE: A

20. **Prolonged bed rest for back pain** -- Prescribing rest for acute low back pain. WHY BAD: Cochrane review found bed rest is no better than staying active, and prolonged rest worsens outcomes. INSTEAD: Stay active within pain tolerance; gradual return to normal activities. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Dahm et al. (2010). EVIDENCE: A

21. **Applying ice then heat strictly by protocol for all injuries** -- Rigid RICE protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation). WHY BAD: Emerging evidence (PEACE & LOVE framework) suggests ice may delay healing by reducing inflammation needed for tissue repair. Strict rest may be counterproductive. INSTEAD: Protect, Elevate, Avoid anti-inflammatories, Compress, Educate & Load, Optimism, Vascularization, Exercise (PEACE & LOVE). SOURCE: Dubois & Esculier (2020), *British Journal of Sports Medicine*. EVIDENCE: B

22. **Antibiotic prophylaxis before dental work for all prosthetic joint patients** -- Routine antibiotics before dental procedures for joint replacement patients. WHY BAD: ADA/AAOS updated guidelines state routine prophylaxis is generally NOT recommended; evidence of benefit is lacking and risk of adverse effects and resistance exists. INSTEAD: Individual risk assessment. SOURCE: ADA/AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines (2013, updated). EVIDENCE: B

23. **Routine PSA screening for all men** -- Universal PSA testing for prostate cancer. WHY BAD: USPSTF recommends shared decision-making (ages 55-69) due to high false-positive rates, overdiagnosis, and overtreatment harms (incontinence, erectile dysfunction). INSTEAD: Informed shared decision-making considering individual risk factors. SOURCE: USPSTF Recommendation (2018); Cochrane Review. EVIDENCE: A

24. **Treating asymptomatic bacteriuria in non-pregnant adults** -- Prescribing antibiotics for bacteria in urine without symptoms. WHY BAD: Treatment does not improve outcomes and increases antibiotic resistance, adverse effects, and C. difficile risk. Exception: pregnant women and pre-urological procedures. INSTEAD: Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in non-pregnant adults. SOURCE: IDSA Guidelines (2019). EVIDENCE: A

25. **Using cough suppressants for acute cough in children** -- OTC cough/cold medications for children under 6. WHY BAD: FDA and AAP advise against OTC cough/cold meds in children <6 years. No evidence of efficacy; risk of serious adverse effects. INSTEAD: Honey (>1 year old), fluids, humidification. SOURCE: FDA advisory (2008); Cochrane Review: Smith et al. (2014). EVIDENCE: A

26. **Routine annual "full-body" health check-ups** -- Comprehensive annual physicals for asymptomatic adults. WHY BAD: Cochrane review (Krogsboll et al., 2019) of 17 trials found general health checks do not reduce morbidity or mortality. They increase diagnosis and overtreatment. INSTEAD: Targeted screening based on age, sex, and risk factors per USPSTF guidelines. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Krogsboll et al. (2019). EVIDENCE: A

27. **Steroid injections as long-term solution for tendinopathy** -- Corticosteroid injections for chronic tendon pain. WHY BAD: Short-term pain relief but worse long-term outcomes vs. exercise therapy. Tendon weakening and increased recurrence. INSTEAD: Progressive loading/exercise programs (eccentric exercise) as first-line. SOURCE: Coombes et al. (2010), *The Lancet*. EVIDENCE: A

28. **Conflating dietary cholesterol with blood cholesterol** -- "Avoid eggs because cholesterol." WHY BAD: Dietary cholesterol has modest effect on blood cholesterol for most people. 2020 Dietary Guidelines Advisory Committee removed the specific cholesterol intake limit. INSTEAD: Focus on reducing saturated and trans fats. SOURCE: 2020-2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans; Fernandez (2012), *Current Opinion in Clinical Nutrition*. EVIDENCE: B

29. **Stretching before exercise to prevent injury** -- Static stretching before athletic activity. WHY BAD: Meta-analyses show static stretching before exercise does not prevent injury and may temporarily reduce power output. INSTEAD: Dynamic warm-up; save static stretching for after exercise or separate flexibility sessions. SOURCE: Shrier (2004), *Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine*; Cochrane Review. EVIDENCE: A

30. **Routine use of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) long-term** -- Continuous PPI use without reassessment. WHY BAD: Long-term PPI use is associated with increased risk of C. difficile, pneumonia, bone fractures, hypomagnesemia, kidney disease, and possibly dementia. INSTEAD: Use lowest effective dose; reassess need regularly; step down when possible. SOURCE: FDA safety communication; Moayyedi et al. (2019), *BMJ*. EVIDENCE: A

31. **"Feed a cold, starve a fever"** -- Dietary folklore for illness management. WHY BAD: No evidence supports differential eating based on fever vs. cold. Adequate nutrition and hydration support immune function in both. INSTEAD: Maintain adequate nutrition and fluids during illness. SOURCE: General medical consensus; no supporting evidence for the adage. EVIDENCE: C

32. **Hydrogen peroxide on wounds** -- Using hydrogen peroxide as wound antiseptic. WHY BAD: Hydrogen peroxide is cytotoxic to fibroblasts and delays wound healing. It damages healthy tissue. INSTEAD: Clean wounds with clean running water or normal saline. Use appropriate wound dressings. SOURCE: Lineaweaver et al. (1985), *Archives of Surgery*; wound care guidelines. EVIDENCE: B

33. **Colonic irrigation / hydrotherapy for "cleansing"** -- Regular colonics for health. WHY BAD: No evidence of health benefit. Risks include perforation, infection, electrolyte imbalance, and disruption of gut microbiome. INSTEAD: The colon is self-cleaning. Maintain gut health with fiber, hydration, and probiotics when indicated. SOURCE: Mishori et al. (2011), *Journal of Family Practice*. EVIDENCE: B

34. **Assuming all genetic risk is deterministic** -- "I have the gene, so I'll definitely get the disease." WHY BAD: Most common diseases are polygenic with environmental interactions. Even BRCA1 carriers have 45-85% lifetime breast cancer risk, not 100%. Epigenetics, lifestyle, and environment modulate genetic risk significantly. INSTEAD: Genetic counseling for risk assessment; understand penetrance and modifiable risk factors. SOURCE: ACMG practice guidelines. EVIDENCE: B

35. **Giving aspirin during suspected stroke** -- Treating all emergencies like heart attacks. WHY BAD: Hemorrhagic stroke (15-20% of strokes) would be worsened by aspirin. Cannot distinguish stroke type without imaging. INSTEAD: Call emergency services immediately; do not give aspirin until stroke type is determined by CT/MRI. SOURCE: AHA/ASA Stroke Guidelines (2019). EVIDENCE: B

36. **Using antibacterial soap over regular soap** -- Choosing antibacterial soap for hand hygiene. WHY BAD: FDA (2016) found no evidence that antibacterial soaps with triclosan are more effective than plain soap and water. Triclosan may contribute to antibiotic resistance and endocrine disruption. INSTEAD: Plain soap and water for 20+ seconds; alcohol-based hand sanitizer when soap unavailable. SOURCE: FDA Consumer Safety ruling (2016); Cochrane Review on hand hygiene. EVIDENCE: A

37. **Cracking knuckles causes arthritis** -- Common belief about joint damage. WHY BAD: Multiple studies, including a famous self-experiment over 60 years (Unger, 2009, *Arthritis & Rheumatism*), found no association between knuckle cracking and arthritis. INSTEAD: Knuckle cracking is generally harmless. SOURCE: Castellanos & Axelrod (1990), *Journal of the American Board of Family Practice*. EVIDENCE: B

38. **Thyroid supplements for "subclinical" fatigue without proper testing** -- Self-treating perceived thyroid issues. WHY BAD: Thyroid hormone supplementation in euthyroid individuals offers no benefit and risks arrhythmia, bone loss, and anxiety. INSTEAD: Proper thyroid function testing (TSH, free T4) before any supplementation. SOURCE: TRUST trial (2017), *NEJM*. EVIDENCE: B

39. **IV vitamin drips for wellness** -- "Myers cocktails" and IV vitamin infusions at wellness clinics. WHY BAD: No evidence of benefit in healthy individuals. Risks include infection, air embolism, electrolyte imbalance, and organ damage from excess vitamins. INSTEAD: Oral vitamins (when deficient); balanced diet. SOURCE: Gaby (2002), *Alternative Medicine Review* (limited evidence); FDA safety concerns. EVIDENCE: C

40. **Tilting head back for nosebleeds** -- Common first-aid error. WHY BAD: Causes blood to flow down throat, risking aspiration, nausea, and obscuring blood loss volume. INSTEAD: Lean forward, pinch soft part of nose for 10-15 minutes, breathe through mouth. SOURCE: Standard first-aid guidelines; NHS/Red Cross recommendations. EVIDENCE: C

41. **Cranberry juice for treating UTIs** -- Using cranberry juice as UTI treatment. WHY BAD: Cochrane review (Jepson et al., 2012) found cranberry products do not significantly reduce UTI occurrence. Juice often has high sugar content. INSTEAD: Antibiotics for confirmed UTIs; adequate hydration for prevention. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Jepson et al. (2012). EVIDENCE: A

42. **Routine episiotomy during childbirth** -- Routine surgical cut during delivery. WHY BAD: Cochrane review shows routine episiotomy increases perineal trauma vs. restrictive use. No benefit for mother or baby in routine practice. INSTEAD: Selective episiotomy only when clinically indicated. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Jiang et al. (2017). EVIDENCE: A

43. **Activated charcoal as routine "detox" or hangover cure** -- Consuming activated charcoal regularly for health. WHY BAD: Activated charcoal is a medical treatment for specific acute poisonings (within 1 hour). It does not detoxify the body, interferes with medication absorption, and causes constipation. INSTEAD: Used only in hospital/poison control settings for specific ingestions. SOURCE: Position statement of AACT/EAPCCT (2005). EVIDENCE: B

44. **Using Q-tips (cotton swabs) inside the ear canal** -- Cleaning ears with cotton swabs. WHY BAD: Pushes wax deeper, risks tympanic membrane perforation, and can cause impaction. Ear canals are self-cleaning. INSTEAD: Clean only outer ear; use softening drops if impacted; see a professional for removal. SOURCE: AAO-HNS Clinical Practice Guideline on Cerumen Impaction (2017). EVIDENCE: B

45. **"Starve diabetes" with very low carb** -- Extreme carb elimination for diabetes management. WHY BAD: While carb management is important in diabetes, extreme restriction can cause hypoglycemia (with insulin/sulfonylureas), ketoacidosis (type 1), and nutritional deficiencies. INSTEAD: Individualized carbohydrate management with dietitian guidance. SOURCE: ADA Standards of Medical Care (2024). EVIDENCE: B

46. **Testosterone therapy for "low T" based on symptoms alone** -- Prescribing testosterone based on fatigue/low libido without confirmed hypogonadism. WHY BAD: Many symptoms attributed to "low T" have other causes. Testosterone therapy in eugonadal men increases polycythemia, cardiovascular risk, and fertility disruption. INSTEAD: Confirm low testosterone on two morning samples; investigate underlying causes. SOURCE: AUA/Endocrine Society guidelines. EVIDENCE: B

47. **Using emergency departments for non-emergencies** -- Going to ER for minor complaints. WHY BAD: Overcrowds EDs, delays care for true emergencies, increases cost 3-10x vs. primary care, and often results in less coordinated follow-up. INSTEAD: Primary care, urgent care, telehealth for non-emergencies. SOURCE: CDC NHAMCS data; multiple health system analyses. EVIDENCE: B

48. **Prophylactic tonsillectomy for recurrent sore throats in adults** -- Routine tonsillectomy for frequent pharyngitis. WHY BAD: Cochrane review found limited evidence of meaningful benefit vs. watchful waiting in adults. Surgical risks include hemorrhage, pain, and rare mortality. INSTEAD: Watchful waiting with criteria-based (Paradise criteria) surgical consideration. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Burton et al. (2014). EVIDENCE: A

49. **Chelation therapy for atherosclerosis (outside clinical trials)** -- IV chelation for heart disease. WHY BAD: TACT trial showed modest benefit in diabetic subgroup only; not replicated. Risks include kidney damage, hypocalcemia, and death. Not recommended by AHA. INSTEAD: Evidence-based cardiovascular risk reduction (statins, lifestyle, blood pressure control). SOURCE: Lamas et al. (2013), *JAMA* (TACT trial); AHA scientific statement. EVIDENCE: B

50. **Relying on symptom severity to gauge disease seriousness** -- "I feel fine, so I must be healthy." WHY BAD: Many serious conditions are asymptomatic until advanced (hypertension, type 2 diabetes, many cancers, hepatitis C, HIV). Silent myocardial ischemia causes ~25% of heart attacks. INSTEAD: Age/risk-appropriate screening even when asymptomatic. SOURCE: USPSTF screening recommendations; JNC guidelines for hypertension. EVIDENCE: A

## 50 BEST PRACTICES (Medicine / General Health)

1. **Vaccination per WHO/CDC schedules** -- Following recommended immunization programs. Vaccines prevent 4-5M deaths annually and are among the most cost-effective health interventions in history. SOURCE: WHO Global Vaccine Action Plan; Taylor et al. (2014) meta-analysis confirming safety. EVIDENCE: A

2. **Hand hygiene (20+ seconds with soap and water)** -- The single most effective intervention for preventing infectious disease transmission. Reduces respiratory illness by 16-21% and GI illness by 30%+. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Ejemot-Nwadiaro et al. (2020). EVIDENCE: A

3. **Physical activity: 150-300 min/week moderate or 75-150 min/week vigorous** -- Reduces all-cause mortality by ~30%, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, depression, and multiple cancers. SOURCE: WHO Physical Activity Guidelines (2020); 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines Advisory Committee Scientific Report. EVIDENCE: A

4. **Smoking cessation** -- Smoking causes ~8M deaths/year globally. Cessation at any age reduces risk; even after 60, significant benefits. Combination of counseling + pharmacotherapy (varenicline or NRT) is most effective. SOURCE: WHO FCTC; Cochrane Reviews on NRT, varenicline. EVIDENCE: A

5. **Blood pressure monitoring and treatment** -- Hypertension is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease and stroke. Treatment to <130/80 mmHg reduces events by ~25%. SOURCE: SPRINT trial (2015), *NEJM*; ACC/AHA guidelines (2017). EVIDENCE: A

6. **Evidence-based cancer screening** -- Age/sex-appropriate screening for cervical (Pap/HPV), breast (mammography), colorectal (colonoscopy/FIT), and lung cancer (LDCT for high-risk). SOURCE: USPSTF screening recommendations (updated 2021-2024). EVIDENCE: A

7. **Moderate alcohol consumption or abstinence** -- No amount of alcohol is "safe" for health (Global Burden of Disease 2018). Even light drinking associated with increased cancer risk. Benefits previously attributed to moderate drinking likely confounded. INSTEAD: If you don't drink, don't start for health reasons. SOURCE: GBD Alcohol Collaborators (2018), *The Lancet*. EVIDENCE: A

8. **Mediterranean or DASH dietary pattern** -- Reduces cardiovascular events by ~30% (PREDIMED trial), lowers blood pressure (DASH trial), and is associated with reduced diabetes and cancer risk. SOURCE: Estruch et al. (2018), *NEJM* (PREDIMED, corrected); Sacks et al. (2001), *NEJM* (DASH). EVIDENCE: A

9. **Adequate sleep (7-9 hours for adults)** -- Insufficient sleep is linked to obesity, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, mental illness, and accidents. Recommended by AASM, Sleep Research Society, and WHO. SOURCE: Watson et al. (2015), *Sleep* (AASM/SRS Joint Consensus). EVIDENCE: A

10. **Antibiotic stewardship** -- Using antibiotics only when indicated, for the correct duration, and narrowest spectrum. Reduces resistance development and adverse effects. SOURCE: WHO AWaRe classification; CDC Core Elements of Antibiotic Stewardship. EVIDENCE: A

11. **Statin therapy for appropriate cardiovascular risk** -- High-quality evidence for primary prevention in appropriate risk groups and secondary prevention post-MI. NNT ~20-40 for primary prevention over 5 years. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Taylor et al. (2013); ACC/AHA Cholesterol Guidelines (2018). EVIDENCE: A

12. **CBT as first-line for mild-moderate depression and anxiety** -- Cognitive-behavioral therapy is evidence-based, durable, and avoids medication side effects. NNT ~4-7 for depression remission. SOURCE: Cochrane Reviews on CBT for depression and anxiety; NICE guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

13. **Diabetes prevention through lifestyle modification** -- 150 min/week exercise + 5-7% weight loss reduces type 2 diabetes incidence by 58% in high-risk individuals (more effective than metformin). SOURCE: Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) trial, *NEJM* (2002). EVIDENCE: A

14. **Sun protection for skin cancer prevention** -- Sunscreen (SPF 30+), protective clothing, and avoiding peak UV hours reduce melanoma and non-melanoma skin cancer risk. SOURCE: Green et al. (2011), *Journal of Clinical Oncology* (randomized trial). EVIDENCE: A

15. **Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) for diarrheal disease** -- ORT saves ~1M lives annually. Simple, inexpensive, evidence-based. One of the most important medical innovations of the 20th century. SOURCE: WHO/UNICEF ORT guidelines; *The Lancet* called it "potentially the most important medical advance this century." EVIDENCE: A

16. **Folic acid supplementation before and during early pregnancy** -- 400-800 mcg daily reduces neural tube defects by 50-70%. Should begin 1-3 months before conception. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: De-Regil et al. (2015). EVIDENCE: A

17. **Maintaining healthy weight (BMI 18.5-24.9 with waist circumference consideration)** -- Obesity is associated with 13+ cancer types, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and reduced life expectancy. SOURCE: NCD Risk Factor Collaboration (2016), *The Lancet*; WHO Obesity Guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

18. **Treating confirmed vitamin D deficiency** -- True deficiency (<20 ng/mL) increases fracture risk and is associated with multiple adverse outcomes. Supplementation (600-2000 IU/day) corrects deficiency safely. SOURCE: AACE/Endocrine Society guidelines; Bolland et al. (2014) on avoiding megadosing. EVIDENCE: A

19. **Annual influenza vaccination** -- Reduces flu illness by 40-60% in matched seasons, reduces hospitalizations and mortality, especially in elderly and immunocompromised. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Demicheli et al. (2018); WHO influenza guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

20. **Harm reduction approaches for substance use** -- Needle exchange, naloxone distribution, supervised consumption sites, and medication-assisted treatment (methadone, buprenorphine) reduce deaths and disease transmission. SOURCE: Cochrane Reviews on opioid substitution therapy; WHO harm reduction guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

21. **Dietary fiber (25-30g/day)** -- Reduces colorectal cancer risk, cardiovascular mortality, type 2 diabetes risk, and improves gut health. Most populations consume far less than recommended. SOURCE: Reynolds et al. (2019), *The Lancet* (meta-analysis of 185 prospective studies). EVIDENCE: A

22. **Fall prevention in elderly (multifactorial)** -- Exercise (balance + strength), medication review, vision correction, home hazard modification reduce falls by 20-30%. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Gillespie et al. (2012). EVIDENCE: A

23. **Shared decision-making in medical care** -- Involving patients in treatment decisions improves adherence, satisfaction, and outcomes. Core principle of evidence-based medicine. SOURCE: Elwyn et al. (2012), *BMJ*; Cochrane Review: Stacey et al. (2017) on decision aids. EVIDENCE: A

24. **Iron supplementation for confirmed iron-deficiency anemia** -- Evidence-based treatment for the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide. Every-other-day dosing may improve absorption. SOURCE: WHO guidelines on iron supplementation; Stoffel et al. (2017), *The Lancet Haematology*. EVIDENCE: A

25. **CPR and AED training for laypersons** -- Bystander CPR doubles survival from out-of-hospital cardiac arrest. AED use can increase survival to 50-70% if applied quickly. SOURCE: AHA ILCOR Guidelines (2020); Cochrane Review on public-access defibrillation. EVIDENCE: A

26. **Breastfeeding when possible (6 months exclusive, then complementary)** -- WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months. Reduces infant infections, SIDS, and childhood obesity; benefits maternal health (reduced breast/ovarian cancer). SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Kramer & Kakuma (2012); WHO Global Strategy. EVIDENCE: A

27. **Regular dental care and oral hygiene** -- Oral health is linked to cardiovascular disease, diabetes, adverse pregnancy outcomes. Regular brushing (2x/day), flossing, and dental visits. SOURCE: ADA guidelines; Cochrane Reviews on oral hygiene. EVIDENCE: B

28. **Appropriate use of PPE in healthcare and high-risk settings** -- Masks, gloves, gowns reduce healthcare-associated infections. N95/FFP2 for airborne precautions. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Jefferson et al. (2023) on physical interventions; WHO IPC guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

29. **Preoperative optimization (prehabilitation)** -- Exercise and nutritional optimization before major surgery reduces complications and length of stay. SOURCE: Cochrane reviews on preoperative exercise; ERAS (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery) protocols. EVIDENCE: A

30. **Wound care: moist healing environment** -- Moist wound healing is superior to dry/open wounds for most wound types. Promotes epithelialization and reduces scarring. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Dumville et al. (various); Winter (1962), original moist healing evidence. EVIDENCE: A

31. **Sodium reduction for hypertension** -- Reducing sodium intake to <2g/day reduces blood pressure by 3-5 mmHg systolic. Greater effect in hypertensive individuals. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Graudal et al. (2020); WHO sodium guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

32. **Early mobilization after surgery/illness** -- Getting patients moving within 24 hours of surgery/admission reduces DVT, pneumonia, deconditioning, and length of stay. SOURCE: Cochrane Reviews on early mobilization; ERAS protocols. EVIDENCE: A

33. **Advance care planning** -- Documenting healthcare preferences improves end-of-life care quality, reduces unwanted interventions, and decreases family distress. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Brinkman-Stoppelenburg et al. (2014); Sudore et al. (2017), *JAMA Internal Medicine*. EVIDENCE: A

34. **HPV vaccination for cancer prevention** -- HPV vaccine prevents cervical cancer (and other HPV-related cancers). Population-level data show dramatic reductions in HPV infections and pre-cancerous lesions. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Arbyn et al. (2018); real-world effectiveness data from multiple countries. EVIDENCE: A

35. **Monitoring and treating hyperlipidemia** -- Elevated LDL-cholesterol is a causal risk factor for atherosclerosis. Mendelian randomization studies confirm causality. Treatment reduces cardiovascular events. SOURCE: Ference et al. (2017), *European Heart Journal* (Mendelian randomization); multiple statin RCTs. EVIDENCE: A

36. **Evidence-based chronic disease self-management** -- Structured programs for diabetes, heart failure, COPD, and asthma improve outcomes, reduce hospitalizations, and increase quality of life. SOURCE: Cochrane Reviews on self-management for each condition. EVIDENCE: A

37. **Appropriate anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation** -- AF is a major stroke risk factor. Anticoagulation (DOACs preferred over warfarin) reduces stroke by 60-70%. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Aguilar et al. (2007); RELY, ROCKET-AF, ARISTOTLE trials. EVIDENCE: A

38. **Palliative care integration early in serious illness** -- Early palliative care improves quality of life, mood, and sometimes even survival. Should be concurrent with curative treatment. SOURCE: Temel et al. (2010), *NEJM*; Cochrane Review on early palliative care. EVIDENCE: A

39. **Perioperative glycemic control** -- Managing blood glucose in surgical patients reduces infection, wound complications, and mortality. SOURCE: NICE-SUGAR trial (2009), *NEJM*; ADA perioperative guidelines. EVIDENCE: A

40. **Evidence-based asthma management (ICS as controller therapy)** -- Inhaled corticosteroids are the cornerstone of persistent asthma management. Reduce exacerbations, hospitalizations, and deaths. SOURCE: GINA Global Strategy (2024); Cochrane Reviews on ICS for asthma. EVIDENCE: A

41. **Colorectal cancer screening with FIT or colonoscopy** -- Screening reduces CRC mortality by 40-68%. FIT annually or colonoscopy every 10 years from age 45. SOURCE: USPSTF Recommendation (2021); Cochrane Review: Hewitson et al. (2008). EVIDENCE: A

42. **Proper medication reconciliation at care transitions** -- Reviewing all medications when patients move between settings reduces adverse drug events by 40%+. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Redmond et al. (2018). EVIDENCE: A

43. **Malaria prophylaxis for travelers to endemic areas** -- Appropriate chemoprophylaxis (atovaquone-proguanil, doxycycline, or mefloquine) prevents malaria in travelers. SOURCE: WHO International Travel and Health; Cochrane reviews on malaria prophylaxis. EVIDENCE: A

44. **WASH (Water, Sanitation, Hygiene) interventions** -- Clean water, proper sanitation, and hygiene education prevent diarrheal diseases (2nd leading cause of childhood death globally). SOURCE: WHO/UNICEF WASH programs; Cochrane Reviews on WASH. EVIDENCE: A

45. **Graduated compression stockings for DVT prevention** -- In hospitalized medical/surgical patients, compression reduces DVT risk. SOURCE: Cochrane Review: Sachdeva et al. (2018). EVIDENCE: A

46. **Iodine supplementation in deficient populations** -- Iodine deficiency is the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide. Universal salt iodization prevents cretinism and goiter. SOURCE: WHO iodine supplementation guidelines; Cochrane Review. EVIDENCE: A

47. **Prompt recognition and treatment of sepsis** -- Sepsis kills ~11M annually. Each hour of delayed antibiotics increases mortality by 7.6%. Early recognition, cultures, fluids, and antibiotics save lives. SOURCE: Surviving Sepsis Campaign Guidelines (2021); Kumar et al. (2006). EVIDENCE: A

48. **Cervical cancer screening (Pap + HPV co-testing)** -- Regular screening has reduced cervical cancer mortality by 80%+ where implemented. HPV co-testing extends screening intervals safely. SOURCE: USPSTF Recommendation (2018); Cochrane Review. EVIDENCE: A

49. **Evidence-based management of heart failure** -- Quadruple therapy (ACE-I/ARNI + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2i) reduces mortality and hospitalization in HFrEF. SOURCE: McMurray et al. (2014), *NEJM* (PARADIGM-HF); multiple Cochrane Reviews. EVIDENCE: A

50. **Addressing social determinants of health** -- Health outcomes are more influenced by social factors (poverty, education, housing, food security) than by healthcare alone. Addressing SDOH is the most impactful population health intervention. SOURCE: WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health (2008); Marmot Review. EVIDENCE: A

## 10 COMMON LLM MISCONCEPTIONS (Medicine)

1. **Providing specific dosage recommendations** -- LLMs should NEVER provide specific drug dosages. Dosing depends on weight, renal function, hepatic function, age, drug interactions, and clinical context that an LLM cannot assess.

2. **Treating symptom descriptions as diagnostic** -- LLMs may match symptoms to conditions with false confidence. The same symptoms can indicate benign or life-threatening conditions, and diagnosis requires physical examination, lab work, and clinical judgment.

3. **Recommending supplements as if they were proven treatments** -- LLMs often present supplement evidence more favorably than warranted, failing to distinguish between mechanistic plausibility, in-vitro effects, and actual clinical trial evidence in humans.

4. **Equating statistical significance with clinical significance** -- LLMs may cite studies showing "statistically significant" effects that are clinically meaningless (e.g., 0.5-point change on a 100-point scale).

5. **Hallucinating medical citations** -- LLMs fabricate plausible-sounding journal references at alarming rates (25-50% in medical research contexts). NEVER trust an LLM-generated citation without verification.

6. **Oversimplifying drug mechanisms** -- Describing medications as "doing X" when actual pharmacology is complex (e.g., "SSRIs increase serotonin to treat depression" -- this is the oversimplified, challenged model).

7. **Ignoring medication interactions and contraindications** -- LLMs may recommend a treatment without flagging that it's contraindicated with the patient's other medications, conditions, or situation (e.g., ibuprofen with kidney disease, aspirin with blood thinners).

8. **Presenting outdated medical guidelines as current** -- Medical guidelines are regularly updated. LLMs may present 10-year-old guidelines as current practice (e.g., old cholesterol targets, discontinued screening recommendations).

9. **Failing to recommend "see a doctor" for serious symptoms** -- LLMs may provide reassuring information when symptoms actually warrant urgent medical evaluation (chest pain, sudden neurological symptoms, unexplained weight loss).

10. **Applying population-level evidence to individual patients** -- LLMs present population averages as if they apply uniformly. Individual variation in drug response, disease progression, and treatment efficacy is enormous. Medicine is personalized, not algorithmic.
